## Open-Source Robotics: The Revolution After Google

Google's RT-2 demonstrated the power of **Vision-Language-Action** (VLA) models: a single model that sees, understands a natural language instruction, and generates actions for a robot. Impressive, but proprietary and therefore inaccessible. Two academic projects changed the game: **Octo** and **OpenVLA**. Thanks to them, any research lab can now download a VLA model, fine-tune it on their own robot, and deploy it. It's the equivalent of the shift from GPT-3 (closed) to LLaMA (open) for robotics.

## Octo: The First Open-Source Generalist Robotic Foundation Model

Octo was born from a collaboration between UC Berkeley, Stanford, and Carnegie Mellon. The **need** was clear: the robotics community lacked a shared model that everyone could build on, much like BERT and GPT served as a common foundation in NLP.

**Architecture**: Octo is built on a multi-modal Transformer designed for flexibility.
- **Observation encoder**: the robot's camera images and text instructions are transformed into tokens, the universal language of Transformers
- **Transformer backbone**: fuses visual and textual information to understand the scene and the intent
- **Action head**: generates robotic trajectories, meaning the sequence of movements the robot must execute

The key to Octo is its **modularity**. Unlike RT-2, which was tied to a specific Google robot, Octo accepts different configurations: wrist-mounted or overhead cameras, varied action spaces, different robot morphologies. It's like software that runs on any computer, not just the manufacturer's machine.

**Data**: Octo is trained on the **Open X-Embodiment** dataset, a massive collaborative effort gathering over 800,000 trajectories from 22 different robot types. This diversity is what enables generalization: by seeing many different robots doing varied tasks, Octo learns transferable manipulation principles.

**Results**: you can fine-tune Octo in a few hours on a new robot with only a few hundred demonstrations. It outperforms approaches trained from scratch on the same data volume, proving the value of pre-training. Two sizes are available: Octo-Small (27M parameters) and Octo-Base (93M parameters), lightweight enough to run on embedded hardware.

<Callout type="info" title={"Octo's Modularity"}>
Octo accepts flexible configurations: different cameras, different action spaces, different robot morphologies. It's a "plug-and-play" model for robotics, where RT-2 was a monolithic system tied to a single robot type.
</Callout>

## OpenVLA: The Power of Vision-Language Models

OpenVLA was born from the collaboration between Stanford and Toyota Research Institute. The **approach is fundamentally different** from Octo's: rather than building a model from scratch, OpenVLA **fine-tunes an existing VLM** (Prismatic-7B, based on Llama 2) to turn it into a robotic controller.

**Architecture**:
- **Vision backbone**: SigLIP + DinoV2 for rich image encoding. SigLIP provides vision-language alignment (understanding what you see), DinoV2 provides fine spatial information (understanding where things are)
- **Language backbone**: Llama 2 7B for reasoning and generation. This is the brain that understands instructions and reasons about what to do
- **Action tokenization**: the robot's continuous actions (position x, y, z, rotation, gripper opening) are discretized into 256 bins and added to the model's vocabulary. Generating an action literally amounts to generating a word

**Philosophy**: this is exactly the RT-2 paradigm, but open-source. You take a pre-trained VLM that understands the visual and textual world, fine-tune it on robotic data, and it inherits all the world understanding acquired during web pre-training. OpenVLA knows what a pen, a cup, or a recycling bin is, without ever having seen them in a robotic context.

**Performance**: with its 7 billion parameters, OpenVLA achieves performance comparable to or even better than RT-2-X, while being **entirely open-source** (model, training code, data). This is a massive democratization.

<Callout type="tip" title="The OpenVLA Strategy">
OpenVLA follows the RT-2 paradigm but in a public version. Take a pre-trained VLM, fine-tune it on robotic data, inherit its world understanding. Transparent, reproducible, accessible to all.
</Callout>

## Comparison: Two Complementary Philosophies

| Aspect | Octo | OpenVLA |
|--------|------|---------|
| Size | 27M-93M | 7B |
| Approach | Transformer from scratch | VLM fine-tuning |
| Data | Open X-Embodiment | Open X-Embodiment |
| Web knowledge | No | Yes (Llama 2) |
| Inference speed | Fast | Slower |
| Fine-tuning | Easy, hours | Expensive |
| Semantic reasoning | Limited | Strong |

These two models are complementary rather than competing. **Octo** is lightweight and fast: ideal for embedded applications where latency matters, like a robot that needs to react in real time. **OpenVLA** offers powerful semantic reasoning: ideal when the robot needs to understand complex instructions and reason about the world. The choice depends on the application and available resources.

## Impact on the Community

**Before** Octo and OpenVLA, only Google and a few large industrial labs could work on VLAs. **After**: any university lab can download a model, fine-tune it on their own robot in a few hours, and contribute back to the Open X-Embodiment dataset. It's the virtuous cycle of open source.

**Standardization**: these projects have also standardized interfaces and data formats in robotics. Before, each lab had its own format. Now, there's a common standard that facilitates collaboration and reproducibility, two pillars of scientific research.

<Callout type="warning" title="Current Limitations">
Despite their qualities, these models remain limited to relatively simple manipulation: pick-and-place, opening drawers, moving objects. Dexterous manipulation (with multi-fingered hands), long-duration tasks requiring complex planning, and deformable objects (fabric, rope) remain open challenges that will require the next generation of models.
</Callout>

## Outlook

The open-source ecosystem is accelerating at an impressive pace. **DROID** (a large-scale robotic demonstration dataset), **LeRobot** (Hugging Face's robotics project), and contributions from universities worldwide constantly enrich the available data and models. Convergence is underway: larger models, richer datasets, and increasingly accessible and affordable robotic hardware. This trajectory points toward truly generalist AI robots, capable of adapting to new tasks without starting from scratch.
