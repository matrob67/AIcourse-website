## The Convergence: 50 Years of Separate Evolution, Suddenly Together

For decades, robotics and generative AI evolved in parallel without crossing paths. Industrial robots executed fixed trajectories programmed by engineers, repeating the same motion millions of times. Language models understood and generated text, with no connection to the physical world. Two neighboring but unrelated disciplines.

Since 2022-2023, a revolution has been underway. **Foundation models**, those giants pre-trained on immense data corpora (text, images, videos), are being applied directly to robotics. The **core idea** is compelling: transfer the world understanding acquired by these models (language, vision, reasoning, knowledge of objects and their uses) to the control of physical robots. A model that knows what a cup is, that understands the instruction "put the cup in the cabinet," and that can generate the movements to do it.

<Callout type="info" title={"What Is a Robotic Foundation Model?"}>
A robotic foundation model is a pre-trained model capable of understanding natural language instructions, perceiving the environment through vision, and generating motor actions. It unifies perception, reasoning, and action in a single system, where classical robotics used dozens of separate, specialized modules.
</Callout>

## Why This Changes Everything: The Historical Problem of Robotics

Before foundation models, robotics suffered from deep limitations that held back its deployment outside factories:

- **Limited generalization**: a robot trained to grasp cups couldn't grasp bottles. Every new object required new training or reprogramming
- **Scarce and expensive data**: collecting robotic demonstrations requires physical hardware, human time, and often controlled conditions. Reaching web-scale data volumes is impossible
- **Lack of semantic understanding**: the robot didn't know WHAT it was manipulating, only HOW to manipulate it. It moved an object from point A to point B without understanding it was a pen, a cup, or a tool
- **Fragility**: the slightest change in the environment (lighting, object positions, background) could cause the system to fail

**Foundation models solve several of these problems simultaneously**:
- **Language understanding**: the robot can receive instructions in natural language, not in code
- **World knowledge**: the LLM has absorbed an immense amount of knowledge about objects, their properties, and their uses. It knows that a knife cuts, that a cup gets filled with water, that a drawer opens by pulling
- **Reasoning**: breaking down a complex task into logical sub-tasks ("to make coffee, first find a cup, then the coffee machine, then...")
- **General-purpose vision**: recognizing thousands of objects without specific training on each one

<Callout type="tip" title={"The NLP Analogy"}>
NLP experienced its revolution with BERT and GPT: the shift from specialized models (one model per task) to generalist models (one model for everything). Robotics is following exactly the same path, with a few years' delay. Foundation models are doing to robotics what Transformers did to language.
</Callout>

## The Three Main Approaches



**Vision-Language-Action (VLA)**: the most direct approach. The model takes an image and a text instruction as input, and directly generates the robot's actions. Google's **RT-2** is the flagship example: a VLM pre-trained on the web, fine-tuned on robotic data, that inherits its understanding of the world.

**LLM as High-Level Planner**: the LLM doesn't directly control the motors. It breaks down a complex instruction ("prepare breakfast") into simple steps ("open the fridge, grab the milk, close the fridge, get a bowl...") and delegates each step to specialized low-level controllers. Google's **SayCan** and **Code as Policies** illustrate this approach. The advantage: you can use a standard LLM without any robotic modification.

**World Models**: the robot learns an internal model of the world's physics. It **mentally simulates** the consequences of its actions before executing them. It's like playing chess by imagining moves ahead of time. Excellent for long-term planning and complex object manipulation.

## Major Challenges

**Sim-to-real gap**: models pre-trained in simulation (Isaac Sim, MuJoCo) operate in an idealized world. Transferring to the real world remains a considerable challenge: textures, contact physics, and the imprecisions of reality differ from simulation in ways that are sometimes subtle, sometimes drastic.

**Safety**: an LLM that hallucinates produces incorrect text. A robot that hallucinates can cause physical damage: break an object, knock something over, or worse, injure a human. The reliability and safety of foundation models in robotics are critical issues that don't yet have a satisfactory solution.

**Latency**: robotic control loops often run at 100 Hz or more. Running a model with several billion parameters in real time on an embedded robot (with weight, energy, and cost constraints) is a major engineering challenge. Model distillation and quantization are active research directions.

<Callout type="warning" title={"Technological Maturity: Tempering the Enthusiasm"}>
Despite spectacular progress, robotic foundation models are at the research stage. No mass-market commercial robot relies entirely on a VLA model. The demos are impressive but carefully controlled. Scaling to industrial deployment, with the reliability and robustness that implies, remains largely ahead of us.
</Callout>

## Key Players

**Google DeepMind**: pioneers with RT-1, RT-2, RT-X, SayCan. They defined the VLA paradigm. **Tesla**: Optimus, end-to-end approach inherited from FSD. **Figure AI**: humanoids + OpenAI partnership. **1X Technologies**: NEO, funded by OpenAI. **NVIDIA**: Isaac Sim for simulation + GR00T as a robotic foundation model. **Meta**: open-source contributions and manipulation research.

## Outlook

Robotics is experiencing a moment comparable to the arrival of ImageNet and CNNs for computer vision in 2012. The ingredients are in place: powerful models, GPU-scalable simulation, and massive investment. The coming years will determine whether foundation models give robots the flexibility and intelligence needed to operate reliably in the real world.

The ImageNet parallel is illuminating: before 2012, computer vision was slow, fragile, and specialized. ImageNet + CNNs transformed everything in just a few years. We are potentially at the same inflection point in robotics with foundation models. Only time will tell if the parallel holds.
