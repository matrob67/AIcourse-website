## The Nightmare Pipeline: 10 Years, $2 Billion, 90% Failure Rate

Drug development is one of the longest, most expensive, and riskiest processes in industry. You identify a protein target involved in a disease. You screen millions of molecules to find a "hit" (a molecule that interacts with the target). You optimize this hit into a "lead" (a molecule with good properties). Then come preclinical tests on cells and animals, and finally clinical trials on humans (phase I, II, III). Bottom line: 10 years on average, $2 billion, and 90% of candidates fail along the way. AI intervenes at every step to bring these numbers down.

The full journey: target identification, hit screening (100,000+ molecules tested), lead optimization (thousands of variants), in vitro testing (cell cultures), in vivo testing (animal models), phase I (50-100 patients, safety), phase II (300+ patients, efficacy), phase III (1,000+ patients, comparison with standard of care), and finally regulatory approval.

AI intervenes everywhere: structure prediction, virtual screening, property prediction, and even clinical trial simulation.

## AlphaFold 3: Visualizing the Drug in Action



AlphaFold 2 predicted the structures of isolated proteins. **AlphaFold 3** goes much further by predicting **molecular complexes**: how a small molecule (drug candidate) binds to its protein target, with what orientation, in which binding pocket, and with what atomic interactions.

For drug discovery, this is transformative. You can now visualize the drug in action: see how it inserts into the protein's active site, which protein atoms "grip" the molecule, and what chemical interactions (hydrogen bonds, hydrophobic interactions, van der Waals forces) hold everything in place. This information directly guides optimization: "if we added a hydroxyl group here, we'd create an additional hydrogen bond that would strengthen the binding."

**Architecture**: AlphaFold 3 uses a diffusion engine (unlike AlphaFold 2's Evoformer). This architecture is more flexible and generalizes better to complex interactions between different molecule types.

Performance: AlphaFold 3 outperforms specialized tools (docking, complex prediction) on every type of interaction. It's a molecular Swiss army knife that excels in every specialty.

</Callout>

## Virtual Screening: Testing 37 Billion Molecules Without Chemistry

Virtual screening involves computationally testing millions or billions of molecules to identify those most likely to work, before spending a single dollar on chemistry.

**Classical docking**: you physically simulate how each molecule binds to the target. Accurate but slow (hours per molecule). **DiffDock**: a diffusion model that predicts the binding pose quickly and accurately. **Uni-Mol**: a universal Transformer-based representation that encodes molecules flexibly.

The concrete result: you can test 37 billion molecules virtually (the size of the largest commercial molecule banks), keep the best 1,000 by binding score, then synthesize and biologically test the 100 most promising. 100 tests instead of 37 billion: the reduction factor is astronomical.

AI scoring models can even predict biological activity without full physical simulation, by directly learning the relationship between molecular structure and activity from experimental data.

## De Novo Generation: Inventing Optimal Molecules

Instead of searching among existing molecules (like browsing a library), you can **generate new molecules** optimized for a specific target (like writing a custom book):

- **Generative models** (VAE, GAN, diffusion) on molecular graphs: they learn chemical grammar and invent new valid molecules
- **Reinforcement learning**: simultaneously optimizing multiple conflicting objectives (biological activity, aqueous solubility, low toxicity, ease of synthesis)
- **SMILES or graph representations** to feed deep learning architectures

**Insilico Medicine** created the first AI-designed drug to enter phase II clinical trials (for idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis). **Recursion** combines automated cell imaging with AI for screening. **Generate Biomedicines** designs therapeutic proteins entirely through generative AI.

<Callout type="paper" title="The First AI Drug in Clinical Trials">
INS018-055 (Insilico Medicine): designed by AI in 30 months instead of the usual 4-5 years for the discovery phase. From target identification to clinical phase II. This isn't a shortcut that skips steps: it's the same rigorous process, but with AI-augmented design at every stage.
</Callout>

## Predicting ADMET Properties: Anticipating the Drug's Fate

A brilliant drug in vitro can fail spectacularly in the human body. ADMET properties determine the molecule's real fate:

- **Absorption**: is the molecule absorbed by the digestive tract when swallowed?
- **Distribution**: does it reach the target organ (brain, kidneys, tumor)?
- **Metabolism**: how quickly does the liver break it down? Too fast = no effect. Too slow = toxic accumulation
- **Excretion**: how does the body get rid of it?
- **Toxicity**: hepatotoxicity (liver), cardiotoxicity (heart), mutagenicity (DNA)?

Models like **ADMET-AI** provide estimates of all these properties in milliseconds from the molecular structure alone. Versus months of expensive biological tests. It's a first filter that eliminates problematic molecules before even synthesizing them, saving time and money.

## Persistent Challenges

**Experimental validity**: a promising molecule in silico must be synthesized and tested to confirm its activity. The gap between prediction and reality remains significant.

**Synthesizability**: generative models can invent molecules that look magnificent on paper but are impossible or very difficult to make in the lab. Integrating synthesizability as a generation constraint is an active research area.

**Noisy data**: biological activity data is scarce, noisy (experimental variability), and often biased (positive results get published, negative ones don't).

**Regulation**: regulators (FDA, EMA) don't yet have a specific framework for AI-designed drugs. The validation process remains the same, but questions of transparency and reproducibility are new.

<Callout type="warning" title={"The Bottleneck Remains Biological Experimentation"}>
AI massively accelerates the computational phase of drug discovery. But experimental validation remains long and expensive. Synthesizing the molecule, testing it on cells, then on animals, then on humans in three phases of clinical trials: no AI can skip these steps. AI doesn't replace clinical trials -- it better guides the candidates entering them.
</Callout>

## Outlook

The convergence of AlphaFold 3 (structure and interaction prediction), generative models (optimal molecule design), and robotic screening platforms (automated high-throughput testing) shapes the future of drug discovery. The goal: reduce development time from 10 years to 2-3 years, and cost from $2 billion to a few hundred million. The first clinical results from AI-designed drugs will tell us whether this promise materializes.
