## The Humanoid Returns: 50 Years of Waiting, Then Suddenly Everything Accelerates

Honda's ASIMO could climb stairs in 2000. Slow, fragile, and limited to carefully prepared demonstrations. The world shrugged: "Why would you want a humanoid robot when an industrial arm does the job just fine?"

Since 2022, everything has changed. Tesla Optimus, Figure AI, 1X NEO. Billions of dollars in investment. The key idea: **foundation models** (vision + language + reasoning) are game-changers. For the first time, a robot can potentially **understand a natural language instruction and execute it** in an unstructured environment. It's no longer an automaton following a script, it's an agent that understands and adapts.

<Callout type="info" title="Why the Human Form?">
The world is designed by and for humans. Doors, stairs, tools, workstations, vehicles: everything is sized for our body shape. A humanoid robot can theoretically integrate into any existing environment without infrastructure modifications. That's a powerful economic argument. But the technical challenge is proportional to the ambition: manual dexterity and bipedal balance remain fiendishly difficult.
</Callout>

## The Three Major Players

**Tesla Optimus**: Tesla transfers its vision stack developed for FSD (Full Self-Driving) to the humanoid robot. Cameras only, no LiDAR. End-to-end approach similar to autonomous driving. Hands with 11 degrees of freedom for manipulation. Target price: under $20,000 for mass adoption. Tesla's strategic advantage: factories capable of mass production, and billions of vision images collected by its vehicle fleet.

**Figure AI** (over $2 billion raised): the most publicized startup, thanks to a **strategic partnership with OpenAI**. Integration of multimodal models for language and visual world understanding. The Figure 02 weighs 60 kg and has 16 hours of battery life. Focus on logistics and manufacturing. The demos are impressive: the robot converses naturally, identifies surrounding objects, reasons about what it needs to do, and executes simple tasks competently. Investors: Microsoft, NVIDIA, OpenAI, Jeff Bezos.

**1X Technologies** (funded by OpenAI): NEO is a bipedal humanoid designed for the home environment. EVE, their wheeled robot, is already deployed for security and surveillance tasks. 1X's approach emphasizes embodied AI and learned world models, with a particular focus on intrinsic safety and gentle interaction with humans.

**Other notable players**: Boston Dynamics (electric Atlas, the most physically agile), Agility Robotics (Digit, already in production at Amazon), Unitree (H1 and G1, aggressive pricing under $20,000), Sanctuary AI (Phoenix, remarkably dexterous hands).

## The Typical Training Pipeline

How do you train a humanoid robot to do useful tasks? The current pipeline involves several stages:

1. **Teleoperation**: a human remotely controls the robot with a controller, haptic gloves, or an exoskeleton. The observations (images, sensors) and actions (arm and leg movements) are recorded simultaneously
2. **Imitation Learning**: a deep learning model learns to reproduce the human demonstrations. It observes what the human did in each situation and learns the corresponding policy
3. **RL in simulation**: refinement of the policy in simulators like Isaac Sim or MuJoCo. In simulation, you can run millions of attempts without risking damage to the robot
4. **Sim-to-real**: transfer of the policy to the physical robot, with domain randomization to bridge the gap between simulation and reality
5. **Real-world fine-tuning**: continuous adaptation on the real robot to correct transfer imperfections

Foundation models like **NVIDIA GR00T** aim to accelerate each step by providing a pre-trained base model that each manufacturer can adapt to its own robot.

## Target Applications by Time Horizon

**Short term (2024-2026)**: logistics (moving bins and packages in warehouses), manufacturing (assistance on production lines), inspection and security (site surveillance).

**Medium term (2026-2030)**: retail (in-store assistance, inventory), healthcare (assisting the elderly, carrying loads for medical staff), construction (repetitive site tasks).

**Long term (2030+)**: domestic robot (cleaning, cooking, tidying up), home care (robotic caregiver), and general labor (replacing repetitive physical tasks across multiple sectors).

<Callout type="warning" title="The Gap Between Demos and Reality">
Demo videos are impressive. But a great deal of caution is warranted. Many are teleoperated (a human controls the robot behind the scenes), sped up, or represent the best attempt out of 100. The ability of a humanoid to operate reliably and autonomously for 8 straight hours in an uncontrolled real environment remains a major and unsolved challenge. Skepticism is justified.
</Callout>

## Major Technical Challenges

**Reliability**: operating 8 hours or more without mechanical, software, or electrical failure. Current industrial robots have an MTBF (mean time between failures) of tens of thousands of hours. Humanoids are far from that.

**Cost**: reaching a price under $20,000 to enable mass adoption. Robotic hardware remains expensive (motors, sensors, dexterous hands).

**Safety**: physically interacting with humans without ever injuring them. A 60 kg humanoid falling on someone can cause serious injuries.

**Battery life**: current batteries limit autonomy and add weight.

**Dexterity**: fine manipulation comparable to the human hand (21 degrees of freedom, tactile feedback, force control) remains an open challenge.

Goldman Sachs estimates the humanoid robot market at $38 billion by 2035. Other analysts are more optimistic ($100 billion) or more skeptical. The uncertainty reflects the difficulty of predicting how quickly the technical challenges will be solved.

The massive current investments are a bet that foundation models will solve the robotic intelligence problem. The outcome is not yet written.
