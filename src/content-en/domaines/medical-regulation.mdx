## Why Regulate AI That Diagnoses Cancer?

An algorithm misses a cancer on a radiograph: the patient loses months of precious treatment time. An algorithm gives a false positive: the patient undergoes an unnecessary invasive biopsy and weeks of anxiety. Medical AI hasn't taken the Hippocratic oath, but it makes decisions that directly affect patient health. Regulation ensures that these systems are safe, performant, and reliable before being deployed in clinical settings.

**SaMD** (Software as a Medical Device) is the key concept framing all of this. The principle: **any software that fulfills a medical function is a medical device**, even if it doesn't reside in any physical apparatus. A cancer detection algorithm on a radiograph, running on a cloud server somewhere, is legally a SaMD and must be regulated exactly like a stethoscope or an MRI scanner.

<Callout type="info" title="SaMD: A New Category">
Historically, a "medical device" meant a physical object: a cardiac stent, a pacemaker, a scalpel. Now, software that aids in diagnosis is classified as a medical device. There's no exception for AI. Regulation applies fully, and that's a good thing for patients.
</Callout>

## Regulation in the United States (FDA)

The FDA offers three authorization pathways, adapted to the level of risk:

**510(k)**: the most common pathway for medical AI. The manufacturer demonstrates that its device is "substantially equivalent" to an already approved device (the predicate device). Concretely, if a pulmonary nodule detection algorithm functionally resembles another already authorized, it can take this pathway. No clinical trials generally required, and the process takes a few months. It's fast, but the notion of "substantial equivalence" for AI algorithms is sometimes vague.

**De Novo**: for devices with no existing equivalent but moderate risk. Requires solid clinical performance data. This is the pathway taken when the AI does something truly new. Average timeline: several months.

**PMA** (Premarket Approval): for high-risk devices. Complete clinical trials required, thorough review. It's long (years) and expensive. Rarely used for AI currently, but it's the mandatory pathway for the most critical systems.

**PCCP** (Predetermined Change Control Plan): a 2023 innovation specifically designed for AI. The idea is brilliant: manufacturers pre-approve a plan for future algorithm updates. As long as modifications stay within the predefined framework (for example, re-training on more data without changing the architecture), there's no need to go through the full process again. It's a smart adaptation for AI, which by nature evolves and improves continuously.

**Numbers**: over 950 AI/ML devices have been authorized by the FDA (mid-2024), the majority in radiology. The pace is accelerating year over year, a sign that industry and regulator are finding their rhythm.

<Callout type="tip" title="FDA and LLMs: A New Challenge">
GPT-4 or Claude aren't medical devices per se. But when a developer integrates an LLM into an application that gives medical advice, the line becomes blurry. The regulatory framework adapted to medical LLMs is still evolving. The FDA is actively thinking about it.
</Callout>

## Regulation in Europe (MDR)

**Regulation (EU) 2017/745** (MDR, Medical Device Regulation) governs all medical devices in Europe, including AI SaMDs.

**Risk-based classification**:
- **Class I**: non-decisional assistance (simple visualization, no direct impact on clinical decision)
- **Class IIa**: diagnostic assistance for non-critical conditions (benign dermatology, for example)
- **Class IIb**: diagnostic assistance for critical conditions (cancer detection, cardiovascular emergencies)
- **Class III**: high risk, direct treatment control (automated medication dosing, for example)

**Conformity assessment**: except for Class I, an independent notified body evaluates conformity. The manufacturer must provide a complete technical dossier demonstrating safety and performance. CE marking is mandatory for any product placed on the European market.

**Rule 11** of the MDR: this rule specific to SaMDs classifies software according to the severity of the targeted medical condition and the importance of the information for the clinical decision. A cancer detection algorithm is typically classified as IIb, which implies a rigorous evaluation process.

## The European AI Act: An Additional Layer

The **AI Act** (adopted in 2024) adds an additional regulatory layer specific to AI:

SaMDs of Class IIb and III are automatically classified as "high-risk" AI systems under the AI Act. This imposes additional obligations: documented risk management, quality and governance of training data, transparency toward users, mandatory human oversight, technical robustness, and cybersecurity.

**Implementation timeline**:
- February 2025: prohibition of unacceptable-risk AI systems
- August 2025: transparency obligations for all AI systems
- August 2026: full obligations for high-risk systems (SaMDs included)

<Callout type="warning" title="Double Regulation = Increased Complexity">
Developers in Europe face double regulation: MDR (as a medical device) AND the AI Act (as an AI system). This complex overlap significantly increases compliance costs and administrative burden. It's a particularly heavy challenge for startups that lack the legal resources of large corporations.
</Callout>

## Cross-Cutting Requirements: What Everyone Must Prove

**Training data**: provenance must be documented, biases analyzed and mitigated, representativeness ensured (sex, age, ethnicity), and annotation quality verified. A model trained only on Caucasian patients cannot claim universal authorization.

**Clinical performance**: you must demonstrate sensitivity (detecting true positives), specificity (avoiding false positives), all in clinical studies on independent populations (not the same data as training), with comparison to the current standard of care, and analysis of performance by demographic subgroups.

**Post-market surveillance**: once the device is on the market, you must monitor its real-world performance in clinical conditions, detect drift (data drift, when real-world data changes compared to training data), report incidents, and update if necessary.

**Continuous learning**: AI that continuously learns changes behavior over time. How do you regulate a device in perpetual evolution? The FDA's PCCP is the first attempt at an answer, but the challenge remains largely open.

## International Issues

Regulatory fragmentation is a major obstacle for developers. A SaMD authorized by the FDA in the United States is not automatically authorized in Europe, Japan (PMDA), or China (NMPA). Each jurisdiction has its own requirements, processes, and timelines. International harmonization efforts (via the IMDRF) are advancing, but slowly. International deployment of a medical AI device remains a regulatory obstacle course.
