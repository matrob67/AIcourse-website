## Moravec's Paradox: Why Grasping Is Harder Than Playing Chess

Picking up a coffee cup? Trivial for you -- you do it without thinking. For a robot? It's a nightmare. It must perceive the object in 3D, estimate its exact pose (position and orientation), plan the arm trajectory while avoiding obstacles, control grip force with precision (too much = the cup breaks, too little = it slips), handle unpredictable contacts and friction, and adapt in real time if something doesn't go as planned.

This is **Moravec's paradox**: tasks you find easy (grasping, walking, seeing) are extremely complex for a robot. Tasks you find hard (playing chess, calculating, storing data) are easy to program. The explanation? Evolution spent hundreds of millions of years optimizing object manipulation. Math is recent: just a few thousand years. AI excels at the recent stuff and struggles with the ancient stuff.

<Callout type="info" title={"Why Grasping Is Hell for a Robot"}>
Manipulation combines 3D perception, pose estimation, motion planning, force control, and real-time adaptation, all in a continuous and stochastic world. The slightest error has immediate consequences: a failed grasp, a dropped object, an unexpected collision. There's no "almost succeeded" in robotic manipulation.
</Callout>

## Grasping: The Fundamental Problem

**Classical approaches**: mathematical stability formulas, geometric heuristics, motion planning algorithms. The result: slow, fragile systems that fail as soon as the object isn't exactly the one expected.

**Deep Learning**: grasping is reformulated as a prediction problem. You show an image to the network, and it predicts where and how to grasp the object. This is a paradigm shift: instead of programming rules, you learn from examples.

**2D Grasping** (planar): the model predicts a grasp rectangle (position, angle, gripper opening). Systems like GraspNet and GG-CNN achieve over 90% success on common objects. Simple and effective for pick-and-place tasks on a table.

**6-DOF Grasping**: the full 3D version, with a grasp pose at 6 degrees of freedom (3D position + 3D orientation). **Contact-GraspNet** works on 3D point clouds to predict stable grasp poses. **AnyGrasp** is a universal grasping system capable of working on completely unknown objects, even in cluttered scenes.

<Callout type="tip" title="Depth Is Crucial">
RGB-D sensors (color camera + depth sensor) are essential. A 3D point cloud gives the exact geometry of the scene: object shapes, distances, flat surfaces. A 2D image alone is ambiguous: is that cup 30cm or 60cm away? Virtually all modern grasping systems combine RGB and depth.
</Callout>

## Learning Through Interaction: 800K Grasp Attempts

One of the most iconic experiments in robotic manipulation is Sergey Levine's at UC Berkeley: 7 robots left free to grasp objects for 2 months. 800,000 grasp attempts. Lots of failures at first, then the neural network progressively learns a function that predicts the probability of success for a given grasp. In the end, the system generalizes to objects never seen during training.

This is real learning by experience, no pre-programmed formulas. The robot discovers on its own that optimal grasp angles depend on the object's shape, that some surfaces slip more than others, and that an object's center of gravity isn't always in the middle.

## Dexterity: From 2 Fingers to 5

Most industrial robots use a parallel gripper (2 fingers). It's reliable but limited: impossible to handle an egg without breaking it, impossible to flip an object in hand, impossible to use tools. **Dexterous manipulation** with multi-fingered hands (3 to 5 fingers) opens an entirely different level of sophistication.

**OpenAI Dactyl** (2019): a Shadow Hand robotic hand (24 degrees of freedom) learned to solve a Rubik's Cube purely through RL in simulation, with massive domain randomization for real-world transfer. The result was spectacular but the system was expensive, slow, and fragile. Very controlled conditions were required.

**DexMV**: learning dexterous manipulation from videos of human hands. The model watches a human manipulate an object and learns to do the same. **DexPoint**: uses 3D point clouds as representation for dexterous manipulation, capturing the fine geometry of fingers and objects.

<Callout type="warning" title="The Gap Between Research and Industry">
In industry, the vast majority of robots use simple grippers (parallel or suction cup). Dexterous manipulation remains confined to research labs. The reasons: high cost of robotic hands, maintenance complexity, and above all a lack of reliability. When a production line needs to run 24/7, simplicity wins over sophistication.
</Callout>

## Learning by Imitation: Large-Scale Teleoperation

**Learning from Demonstrations (LfD)**: a human teleoperates the robot (via a controller, haptic gloves, or a force-feedback device), and the observations (camera images) and actions (arm movements) are recorded simultaneously. A model then learns to reproduce these demonstrations.

**Diffusion Policy**: a recent and elegant approach that applies diffusion models (the ones that generate images) to robotic action generation. Instead of predicting a single deterministic action, it models a distribution of possible actions. This is important because there are often multiple valid ways to do the same thing (grasp from the left or the right), and diffusion handles this multimodality naturally.

**Action Chunking** (ACT): instead of predicting a single action at each time step, you predict a chunk of 10 to 20 future actions. Movements are smoother, errors accumulate less, and behavior is more natural. It's like planning an entire sentence rather than building it word by word.

## Industrial Applications and Trends

**Bin picking**: grasping objects in bulk from a bin (logistics, automotive). **Assembly**: inserting components, screwing, wiring. **Palletizing**: stacking boxes and crates. **Inspection**: manipulating objects to examine them from all angles. **Sorting**: automatic object sorting (recycling, distribution centers).

The 2024-2025 trend is clear: foundation models (VLA) combined with large-scale teleoperation are producing the first truly versatile manipulation robots. Physical Intelligence (Pi), Covariant, and Figure AI are all betting on this convergence.

<Callout type="tip" title="Vision-Language-Action Models">
A VLA model takes the scene image as seen by the robot and a natural language instruction, and directly generates the arm's actions. It's the end of complex pipelines with dozens of separate modules. A single model that understands, reasons, and acts.
</Callout>
