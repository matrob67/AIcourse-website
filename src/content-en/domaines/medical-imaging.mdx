## Radiologists Facing Millions of Scans: Humanly Impossible

A radiologist examines between 30 and 50 imaging studies per day. A brain scan is 500 slices to analyze. A chest CT, 300 slices. Searching for a 2mm tumor across 300 slices at 200-micron resolution is like looking for a grain of sand of a particular color on a beach. And after the tenth exam of the day, fatigue sets in. Human error isn't a flaw, it's a statistical certainty.

AI examines every pixel with the same attention, from the first exam to the thousandth. It's consistent and tireless. In a typical scenario, the AI identifies suspicious anomalies and the radiologist validates them with clinical expertise. It's a partnership, not a replacement.

<Callout type="info" title={"Why Radiology Is the Ideal Playground for AI"}>
Medical imaging checks all the deep learning boxes: standardized digital data (DICOM format -- Digital Imaging and Communications in Medicine), well-defined tasks (detect, classify, segment), and enormous volumes (a large hospital produces millions of exams per year). It's the medical domain where AI has made the most progress.
</Callout>

## Imaging Modalities

Each modality has its own specificities and challenges for AI:

**Radiography (X-rays)**: 2D images, the most common in medicine. AI detects pneumonias, fractures, pulmonary nodules, and cardiomegaly. Public datasets like **CheXpert** (224K images) and **MIMIC-CXR** (400K chest radiography images) have enabled rapid advances. The best models rival radiologists on 14 thoracic pathologies, with the advantage of never getting tired.

**CT scanner**: 3D images obtained by reconstruction from multiple X-ray projections. AI excels at detecting pulmonary nodules (lung cancer), pulmonary embolisms (life-threatening emergency), and cerebral hemorrhages. The **LUNA16** benchmark is the reference for nodule detection. AI's advantage is particularly clear here: systematically analyzing 300 slices where a human might miss a subtle detail on slice 247.

**MRI**: high-resolution 3D images, without ionizing radiation. Major applications in brain segmentation, tumor detection, and cardiac analysis. The **fastMRI** project (Meta/NYU) is remarkable: AI reconstructs diagnostic-quality images from undersampled acquisitions, reducing MRI exam time by a factor of 4 to 10. For the claustrophobic patient in the tunnel, it's a revolution.

**Mammography**: breast cancer detection, the most common cancer in women. Adding AI as a supplementary tool alongside the radiologist reduces false negatives (missed cancers) by 9%. That's potentially thousands of lives saved each year.

<Callout type="tip" title={"AI as a Second Reader"}>
In clinical practice, AI analyzes images in parallel with the radiologist and flags potentially missed anomalies. This is the principle of automated "double reading." You combine the strengths of the human (clinical context, nuanced judgment) and the machine (thoroughness, consistency, tirelessness).
</Callout>

## Architectures

**Classification**: classic CNNs (ResNet, DenseNet, EfficientNet) remain solid for determining whether an image contains a pathology. **Vision Transformers (ViT)** are gaining ground, capable of capturing global relationships in the image that CNNs, limited by their local receptive fields, might miss.

**Anomaly detection**: YOLO, Faster R-CNN, and RetinaNet locate and box anomalies. Attention maps allow visualization of the regions the model focuses on, offering a first step toward interpretability.

**Segmentation**: **U-Net** is the gold standard architecture of medical imaging. Its encoder-decoder design with skip connections preserves fine spatial details, essential for precisely delineating a tumor or organ. **nnU-Net** pushes automation further: it's a self-configuring framework that adapts U-Net to each dataset (resolution, patch size, augmentation). **SAM** (Segment Anything, Meta) is a promising generalist model showing encouraging results after adaptation to the medical domain.



## Foundation Models in Medical Imaging

**BiomedCLIP**: a CLIP model trained on biomedical image-text pairs, enabling image search by textual description. **RadImageNet**: an ImageNet alternative specifically built with 5 million radiological images for pre-training.

**Medical Vision-Language Models**: **LLaVA-Med** is a medical VLM capable of answering questions about medical images in natural language. **Med-Gemini** (Google) integrates medical vision and clinical reasoning in a single multimodal model. You can show an X-ray and ask "what's wrong?" and get a structured, well-reasoned response.

## Persistent Challenges

**Lack of annotated data**: pixel-by-pixel annotation by expert radiologists is extremely expensive in time and money. Self-supervised learning and data augmentation help, but the annotation bottleneck remains real.

**Cross-site generalization**: a model trained at hospital A can fail spectacularly at hospital B. Differences in protocols, machines, and populations create a major **domain shift**. This is one of the main barriers to clinical deployment.

**Interpretability**: radiologists need to understand why the AI predicts something in order to trust it. Techniques like Grad-CAM and attention maps provide a glimpse, but remain insufficient for a complete and rigorous medical explanation.

<Callout type="warning" title={"Automation Bias: A Real Danger"}>
When a radiologist uses AI daily, they can develop excessive confidence in the model, even when it's wrong. Studies have shown that radiologists sometimes change a correct diagnosis to align with an incorrect AI prediction. The human-AI interaction must be carefully designed to avoid this trap: AI should assist, not dictate.
</Callout>

## Real Clinical Impact

More than 500 medical imaging algorithms are authorized by the FDA (United States) or carry the CE marking (Europe). The most advanced domains are mammography, diabetic retinopathy, and chest radiography. Real clinical adoption is progressing, but remains slowed by practical obstacles: integration into existing hospital workflows, questions of legal liability in case of error, and reimbursement issues by healthcare systems.
