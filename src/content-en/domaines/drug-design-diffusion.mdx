## From Generative Images to Generative Molecules

Stable Diffusion creates photorealistic images starting from pure noise. **DiffDock** creates 3D molecules using the same principle. The idea is identical: iteratively add noise to data, then learn to remove that noise step by step. Except that instead of working with pixels on a 2D plane, you work with atoms in 3D space.

Molecules are fundamentally three-dimensional objects: the exact positions of atoms in space determine everything (shape, chemical properties, ability to bind to a biological target). Diffusion models are naturally suited to this problem because they generate continuous 3D coordinates, exactly what's needed to position atoms.

<Callout type="info" title="Why Diffusion for Molecules?">
Discrete approaches (SMILES as text, 2D graphs) lose crucial 3D information. A network working on SMILES strings doesn't know whether a molecule will fold back on itself or extend in space. Diffusion on 3D coordinates captures spatial geometry, which is essential for biological function: a drug must have exactly the right shape to fit into its protein target.
</Callout>

## Equivariance: A Molecule Stays the Same Regardless of Viewing Angle

Consider a molecule in space. If you rotate it 90 degrees, it's still exactly the same molecule with the same properties. If you move it 3 meters to the right, it hasn't changed either. An ordinary neural network would see different inputs for each orientation. **E(3)-equivariant networks** (EGNN, GemNet, SE(3)-Transformers) build this symmetry into their architecture: they "understand" that a rotation or translation is merely a change of viewpoint, not a change of molecule.

The advantage is considerable in terms of efficiency. Without equivariance, the network would need to see the same molecule in thousands of different orientations to learn it's always the same. With equivariance, a single demonstration implicitly contains the information of all possible orientations. It's like the difference between learning to recognize a cat photo by photo, and understanding the concept of a cat.

## DiffDock: Molecular Docking Reinvented by Diffusion

A ligand (a small drug candidate molecule) must insert into the binding pocket of a target protein, like a key in a lock. Predicting the exact position and orientation of the ligand in this pocket is called **molecular docking**, and it's a fundamental step in drug design.

**DiffDock** (MIT) reformulates docking as a diffusion process on the SE(3) group (the set of rotations and translations in 3D space). Instead of using expensive physical simulations like AutoDock (which can take hours per molecule), DiffDock uses an iterative denoising process: you start from a random ligand position and progressively guide it toward its optimal position.

The result is both faster and more accurate than classical docking methods for many protein targets.

<Callout type="tip" title="The Flexibility of Diffusion Docking">
DiffDock doesn't give a single deterministic answer. It generates a set of possible poses with associated confidence scores. This is more realistic than classical methods, because in reality, molecules can adopt multiple conformations within the same binding pocket, and some are more probable than others.
</Callout>

## Structure-Based Drug Design (SBDD): Designing in the Binding Pocket

You have the 3D structure of a pathogenic protein's active site. The challenge: generate a molecule that fits into it like a puzzle piece, with the right shape, the right size, and the right chemical interactions.

**TargetDiff** generates entire molecules conditioned on the 3D geometry of the binding pocket. The model learns to fill the void optimally. **Pocket2Mol** takes an atom-by-atom approach: it places atoms one by one, like a sculptor gradually adding material to fill a mold. These models produce molecules that are not only geometrically compatible with the target, but also synthesizable in the lab and potentially biologically active.

## RFdiffusion: Designing Therapeutic Proteins de Novo

What if you used diffusion not for small molecules, but to design **entire proteins**? That's what **RFdiffusion** (Baker Lab, University of Washington) does. The model generates a protein backbone (the protein's spine) that folds to adopt a specific shape: binding to a given protein target, adopting complex symmetry, or filling a cavity.

This is experimentally validated: proteins designed by RFdiffusion fold correctly in the lab and function as predicted. The success rate significantly exceeds traditional protein design methods.

<Callout type="paper" title="From Computational Design to Real Protein">
RFdiffusion designed binders (proteins that bind specifically) for targets like the coronavirus spike protein. Validated in the lab: the designed proteins fold into the right shape and bind to their target as predicted. This is proof that computational protein design by diffusion works in the real world.
</Callout>

## Chroma and EvoDiff: Modularity and Flexibility

**Chroma** (Generate Biomedicines) pushes protein design toward more flexibility. You can specify complex constraints: "I want a protein with octahedral symmetry, a central cavity of size X, and that binds to target Y." The interface is programmable, allowing researchers to express their needs in a language close to design.

**EvoDiff** takes a complementary approach: instead of working on 3D structures, it directly generates **amino acid sequences** by capturing evolutionary patterns learned from millions of natural proteins. The idea: evolution has already explored a vast space of functional proteins, and EvoDiff learns to navigate this space.

## Flow Matching: The Elegant Alternative to Diffusion

Diffusion models add noise progressively through a stochastic process. **Flow matching** proposes an alternative: continuous and deterministic trajectories, a straight line (or nearly so) from noise to signal, rather than a stochastic spiral.

**AlphaFold 3** uses a diffusion module for structure prediction. **FrameFlow** uses flow matching on SE(3) for protein design. The advantages of flow matching: faster inference (fewer steps needed) and more direct trajectories (therefore easier to optimize).

## Outlook

Drug design by generative models is converging toward integrated systems: simultaneous design of small molecules, therapeutic proteins, and nucleic acids. Integrating pharmacokinetic constraints (the molecule must be absorbable, non-toxic, stable) directly into the generative process is an active research area. The ultimate goal: fully automated closed-loop design-synthesis-test cycles, already underway in some cutting-edge laboratories.

<Callout type="warning" title="Experimental Validation Is Non-Negotiable">
A molecule generated by diffusion must be chemically synthesized and biologically tested before it can become a drug. AI generation is fast (seconds). Chemical synthesis takes days to weeks. Biological testing takes months. There's no shortcut around experimental chemistry and biology.
</Callout>
