## Synthesizing Medical Knowledge: 50 Million Articles and Growing

A doctor can only read a tiny fraction of the articles published each year in their specialty. PubMed contains over 50 million articles, and that number grows by several thousand per day. More than 500 new phase III clinical trials are published each year. How can a practitioner stay up to date when the volume of medical knowledge doubles every 73 days?

This is where **medical LLMs** come in. The idea: train a model on the entire medical literature, clinical guidelines, and anonymized patient records. Then ask it a question like "What is the optimal treatment for atrial fibrillation in a 65-year-old patient on anticoagulants?" and get an answer that synthesizes tens of thousands of relevant articles into a coherent, structured response. It's an assistant that has read everything you haven't had time to read.

<Callout type="info" title="Why Medical LLMs?">
Medicine is fundamentally a textual domain: textbooks, research articles, clinical practice guidelines, patient records. An LLM can synthesize and navigate this mass of information, provided it's reliable and accurate. And that's precisely where the challenge lies.
</Callout>

## Med-PaLM 2 (Google)

Google developed a series of medical models with steady progression: **Med-PaLM** (2022, first model to surpass the USMLE passing threshold), **Med-PaLM 2** (2023, expert-level performance), then **Med-Gemini** (2024, multimodal with medical images).

**Performance**: Med-PaLM 2 achieves 86.5% on MedQA, a benchmark based on the American medical exam (USMLE) questions. For context: Med-PaLM 1 scored 67%, untuned GPT-4 around 60%, and expert physicians around 90%. The gap is narrowing. Even more impressive, when physicians evaluate Med-PaLM 2's responses in a blind comparison, they rate them comparable in quality to those of experienced clinicians.

**Key techniques**: instruction fine-tuning on question-answer pairs carefully curated by physicians. **Ensemble refinement**: generating multiple candidate answers and selecting the best. **Chain-of-thought**: forcing the model to reason step by step, like a doctor working through a differential diagnosis before concluding.

<Callout type="tip" title={"The Importance of Explicit Reasoning"}>
In medicine, the answer alone isn't enough. The doctor must be able to verify the logic behind it. Med-PaLM 2 generates detailed explanations of its diagnostic reasoning: "The patient presents with X and Y, which suggests Z because..." This makes the model useful and verifiable in clinical practice.
</Callout>

## BioGPT (Microsoft)

BioGPT takes a fundamentally different approach: rather than fine-tuning an existing giant model, it's **pre-trained from scratch** on biomedical literature.

**Data**: 15 million PubMed articles. **Architecture**: GPT-2 (355M parameters, so modest). **Strengths**: excellent at relation extraction (which gene causes which disease), question-answering on the literature, and structured biomedical text generation. **Limitations**: much smaller than Med-PaLM 2, it lacks the general knowledge of a large LLM. It knows biochemistry but not geography.

BioGPT excels on pure biomedical NLP tasks (entity extraction, gene-disease relations) but is less powerful for complex clinical reasoning that requires broader world understanding.

## Other Notable Medical Models

**PMC-LLaMA**: LLaMA fine-tuned on open-access medical literature from PubMed Central. **Clinical Camel**: LLaMA fine-tuned on simulated clinical conversations. **Meditron**: an open-source medical model available in 7B and 70B parameters, developed by EPFL. **BioMistral**: Mistral fine-tuned for biomedical applications. **GatorTron**: an 8.9 billion parameter model pre-trained directly on real medical records, providing access to authentic clinical language (with its abbreviations and jargon).

## MultiMedQA Benchmark: Measuring Progress

The evaluation standard includes several complementary benchmarks: **MedQA** (USMLE questions), **MedMCQA** (Indian medical exam), **PubMedQA** (article-based questions), and real patient questions. Together, they cover different aspects of medical reasoning.

But these benchmarks have important limitations. Multiple-choice questions don't capture the complexity of real medicine: a patient with multiple comorbidities, the need for iterative dialogue to get the full history, integrating imaging and lab data, and the nuanced judgment needed to weigh treatment risks and benefits.

<Callout type="paper" title="The Benchmark and Its Limits">
MultiMedQA is the current standard but is fundamentally limited. Multiple-choice questions don't capture real medicine: a complex patient with multiple comorbidities, the need for dialogue to get the full history, multimodal data integration. Passing an exam is necessary but far from sufficient for practicing medicine.
</Callout>

## Critical Challenges

**Hallucinations**: the model can invent a medication dosage, an incorrect diagnosis, or cite an article that doesn't exist. In medicine, a hallucination can be catastrophic. Imagine an LLM recommending double the toxic dose of a medication with 98% confidence. Factual verification and confidence calibration are vital issues, in the literal sense.

**Bias**: LLMs inherit the biases of the literature they're trained on. Medical research historically overrepresents certain populations (white Western males) and underrepresents others. Medical LLMs risk amplifying these existing disparities rather than correcting them.

**Confidentiality**: training on patient records raises major questions (GDPR in Europe, HIPAA in the United States). On-premise solutions (the model runs in the hospital, nothing leaves) or locally deployed open-source models are generally preferred for this reason.

**Liability**: who is responsible if an LLM gives erroneous medical advice that harms a patient? The doctor who followed it? The hospital that deployed it? The developer who created it? The legal framework is still under construction, and this uncertainty slows adoption.

<Callout type="warning" title={"A Support Tool, Not a Replacement"}>
Medical LLMs are decision-support tools, not replacements for the doctor. Clinical examination, empathy, contextual judgment, and medical responsibility remain the human's domain. The LLM synthesizes the literature and suggests leads. The doctor listens to the patient, integrates the context, and decides.
</Callout>

## Outlook

The strong trend is toward multimodal models that integrate text, medical images, and physiological signals in a single model, like Med-Gemini. Integration into real clinical workflows, with robust safeguards against hallucinations and bias, is the major challenge for the coming years. The goal isn't an autonomous AI doctor, but a partner that makes every doctor more informed and more effective.
