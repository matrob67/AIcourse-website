## Doctors Spend 49% of Their Time... Typing

A patient comes in with arm pain. The doctor examines, palpates, and interviews for 10 minutes. Then they sit down at their computer and type a detailed report for 20 minutes. Later, they search for the patient's history across scattered notes written by other doctors for 15 minutes. Typing, searching, copying and pasting. Almost half of a doctor's time is devoted to administrative documentation rather than patient care. It's a massive waste of scarce expertise.

Clinical data is overwhelmingly textual: hospitalization reports, consultation notes, annotated lab results, discharge letters, prescriptions, correspondence between specialists. These texts contain the patient's complete history, all the information needed to treat them. But it's free text: chaotic, incomplete, riddled with non-standardized abbreviations, often hastily written between patients.

**Clinical NLP** (natural language processing applied to medical text) aims to teach machines to read and structure this textual chaos. Extract useful information, summarize a 500-page record in minutes, generate a clean, structured clinical note from a doctor-patient conversation.

<Callout type="info" title="The Volume of Medical Text Is Overwhelming">
A large teaching hospital generates millions of text documents per year. A cancer patient's record can contain over 200 documents accumulated over years of treatment. When a new doctor takes over this patient, it's physically impossible to read everything. AI that intelligently summarizes this record isn't a luxury, it's a necessity.
</Callout>

## Extracting Medical Entities: Clinical NER (Named Entity Recognition)

The first task of clinical NLP is to identify and label medical entities in free text. Machines read and highlight:

- **Diseases and symptoms**: "type 2 diabetes," "radiating chest pain," "exertional dyspnea"
- **Medications**: "metformin 500mg," "acetaminophen 1g x3/day," "atorvastatin 20mg"
- **Procedures**: "brain MRI," "liver biopsy," "coronary angiography"
- **Anatomy**: "right upper lobe," "common femoral artery," "left ventricle"
- **Lab values**: "HbA1c at 7.2%" (diabetes marker), "creatinine at 1.2 mg/dL"

Models like **ClinicalBERT** (a BERT trained on 2 million clinical notes from Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center) can identify and label 14 types of pathologies with over 90% accuracy. It's like having an assistant that reads each document in seconds and highlights important information by category.

<Callout type="tip" title="Controlled Terminologies: Speaking a Common Language">
After extraction, normalization is needed. A doctor writes "hypertension"? That's ICD-10 code I10. Another writes "HTN"? Same code. Normalization to standardized terminologies like SNOMED-CT, UMLS, or ICD-10 allows other systems to understand and leverage the information: comparing patient cohorts, feeding epidemiological registries, facilitating clinical research.
</Callout>

## Extracting Relations: Who Takes What, Where, When?

Beyond isolated entities, clinical NLP must understand the **relationships** between them. "Metformin 500mg twice daily" establishes a medication-dosage-frequency relationship. "Pain in the left arm" links a symptom to an anatomical location. "Discontinuation of atorvastatin due to myalgias" describes a medication, its discontinuation, and the reason.

**Negation** is a classic trap in the medical context. "No signs of diabetes" is the opposite of "diabetes." "The patient denies any chest pain" does not mean they have chest pain. Specialized models like **NegBERT** and systems like NegEx detect these negative constructions to avoid false diagnoses. It's a problem that seems simple but is fiendishly difficult in real medical language, with its double negatives and convoluted formulations.

## Summarizing a Record: Turning 500 Pages into 2

Medical summary approaches come in three families:

**Extractive summary**: selecting the most important sentences from the record and assembling them. Simple and faithful to the original text, but may lack coherence.

**Abstractive summary**: generating a summary in natural language, by rephrasing and synthesizing. More readable but risk of hallucination (inventing information absent from the record).

**Structured summary**: organizing information by standardized categories (medical history, allergies, current treatments, recent results, care plan). The most useful format for clinicians.

Recent LLMs show promising results: automatic generation of discharge letters from hospitalization notes, consultation summaries from audio transcriptions of doctor-patient conversations, and chronological synthesis of a patient's evolution over several years.

<Callout type="warning" title="Hallucinations Kill in a Medical Context">
An LLM can invent a medical history, a lab result, or a medication dosage that exists nowhere in the record. In medicine, this isn't a harmless error: a false allergy history can prevent administration of a life-saving treatment, a false lab result can lead to a misdiagnosis. Any AI-generated summary **must be verified by a human** before any clinical use.
</Callout>

## Automatic Coding: From Record to ICD-10 Code

**Medical coding** involves assigning the correct standardized codes (ICD-10 for diagnoses, CPT for procedures in the US) to each hospital stay. It's unglamorous but crucial work: hospital billing, national epidemiology, and clinical research all depend on it. Currently, specialized human coders re-read each record and assign the codes.

AI models achieve 70-80% F1-score (combination of precision and recall) on this task, compared to 80-90% for human experts. The gap is narrowing, and even an imperfect model is useful: it can pre-code records and let humans only validate and correct ambiguous cases, considerably reducing work time.

## Transcription and Ambient Documentation

The ideal scenario: a doctor and patient talk naturally, without the doctor touching a keyboard. AI transcribes the conversation in real time, extracts the relevant medical information, and automatically generates a structured clinical note.

**DAX** (Dragon Ambient eXperience, by Nuance/Microsoft) is already deployed in numerous hospitals and medical practices in the United States. Studies show a 30 to 50% reduction in documentation time. This is one of the rare cases in medical AI where the return on investment is immediate and measurable: the doctor saves time, sees more patients, and is less exhausted by the administrative burden.

<Callout type="paper" title="Reducing Administrative Burden: A Direct ROI">
Clinical studies show that AI documentation assistants reduce documentation time by 30 to 50%, allowing doctors to devote significantly more time to patients. This is one of the rare cases where medical AI has a direct, fast, and measurable return on investment.
</Callout>

## Challenges Specific to Clinical Language

- **Non-standardized abbreviations**: "MI" = myocardial infarction in one hospital, "AMI" in another. Each department, each doctor invents their own
- **Typos**: the doctor types fast between patients. "Diabtes" instead of "diabetes." Models must tolerate a significant level of noise
- **Ultra-specialized jargon**: the vocabulary of cardiology differs from neurology and dermatology. A single model must master all these sub-languages
- **Confidentiality**: records contain sensitive personal data (PII). On-premise solutions (the model runs within the hospital) are often preferred over cloud solutions for regulatory reasons (GDPR in Europe, HIPAA in the US)
