## Learning by Trial and Error: Like Learning to Walk by Falling

A child learns to walk by falling hundreds of times, getting back up, adjusting posture, and trying again. They don't consult a biomechanics manual. They try, fail, and learn. A **reinforcement learning** (RL) agent does exactly the same thing: it observes its state, attempts an action, receives a reward (or a penalty), and adjusts its behavior to maximize future rewards.

In robotics, RL allows a robot to learn complex behaviors (walking, grasping, manipulating) through trial and error, without a human having to explicitly program every movement. The decisive advantage of simulation: you can run a million attempts in a few hours on a GPU, while a physical robot can only manage a few hundred per day. Simulation multiplies experience by a factor of 1,000 or more.

<Callout type="info" title="The RL Framework in Robotics">
The agent observes the world state (images, position sensors, forces), executes an action (motor movements), receives a reward (task success, penalty for failure), and adjusts its policy to maximize cumulative long-term reward. In robotics, states are continuous and high-dimensional, actions are continuous, and rewards are often sparse: the robot is only rewarded at the end of the task.
</Callout>

## The Three Dominant Algorithms in Robotics

### PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization)

Developed by OpenAI in 2017, PPO has become the reference algorithm for robotics in simulation. It's a **policy gradient algorithm** that limits the size of policy updates at each iteration to guarantee stability.

**Key mechanism**: the "clipped" objective function. PPO computes the ratio between the new policy and the old one, and clips it within an interval $[1-\epsilon, 1+\epsilon]$ (with $\epsilon = 0.2$ typically). This guarantees that the policy doesn't change too abruptly at each iteration. It's like putting guardrails on a mountain road: you move forward, but you don't fall off the cliff.

**Advantages for robotics**:
- Remarkable stability during training (few catastrophic divergences)
- Works well with high-dimensional action spaces (a humanoid has 30+ motors)
- Excellent in massively parallel simulation (NVIDIA's Isaac Gym)
- Relatively simple to implement and tune

**Applications**: quadruped and bipedal locomotion, simple manipulation, high-level policies (planning).

<Callout type="tip" title="PPO + Parallel GPU = Revolution">
PPO excels when you can massively parallelize training. NVIDIA's Isaac Gym runs 4096+ simulation environments in parallel on a single GPU, generating millions of transitions per second. The PPO + parallel GPU combination has literally revolutionized robotic locomotion: what used to take weeks now takes hours.
</Callout>

### SAC (Soft Actor-Critic)

Developed by Tuomas Haarnoja at Berkeley in 2018, SAC introduces a subtle idea: maximize not only the reward but also the **entropy** of the policy, which encourages exploration.

**Architecture**:
- **Actor**: a neural network that generates actions (the policy)
- **Critic**: two Q-networks (double Q-learning) that estimate the value of actions. Using two critics and taking the minimum reduces overestimation, a classic RL problem
- **Entropy coefficient** (alpha): automatically adjusted to balance exploration and exploitation. When the model is uncertain, it explores more. When it's confident, it exploits

**Advantages**:
- **Sample efficiency**: off-policy (it reuses old experiences stored in a replay buffer), so it learns faster with less data than PPO
- **Natural exploration**: entropy maximization encourages varied behaviors and avoids getting stuck in local optima
- **Robustness**: high-entropy policies are often more robust to perturbations and environment changes
- **Real world**: particularly suited when data is limited (simulation isn't available)

**Applications**: dexterous manipulation, tasks where exploration is crucial, learning directly on a real robot.

### TD3 (Twin Delayed Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient)

Developed by Scott Fujimoto in 2018, TD3 is an improvement on DDPG that fixes three major problems:

1. **Twin critics**: two Q-networks whose minimum is taken, reducing systematic value overestimation
2. **Delayed policy updates**: the policy is updated less frequently than the critics (typically once for every two critic updates), allowing value estimates to stabilize
3. **Target policy smoothing**: noise is added to the target policy, smoothing estimates and making learning more robust

**Advantages**: more stable than DDPG, performant on continuous control, sample-efficient thanks to its off-policy nature.

**Applications**: robotic arm control, relatively simple continuous control tasks.

## Robotics-Specific Techniques

**Reward Shaping**: in robotics, natural rewards are often sparse. The robot only receives a positive signal when it completes the entire task, which can take hundreds of actions. Reward shaping designs intermediate rewards that guide learning: reducing distance to the goal, aligning the gripper with the object, giving a bonus for first contact. It's like putting road signs along a route instead of only marking the finish line.

**Curriculum Learning**: training on tasks of increasing difficulty. You start with large, easy-to-grasp objects, then reduce the size. You start on flat terrain, then add slopes and obstacles. The robot progresses like a student: easy exercises first, hard exams later.

**Sim-to-Real and Domain Randomization**: train in simulation then deploy on a real robot. For the transfer to work, simulation parameters are massively randomized: friction, masses, stiffness, textures, lighting, sensor and actuator noise, latencies. If the agent works across all these varied conditions in simulation, it has a good chance of working in the real world, which is just one instantiation among many.

<Callout type="warning" title="The Reward Problem: Critical and Underestimated">
Designing a good reward function is the most critical and most underestimated aspect of robotic RL. A poorly designed reward leads to pathological behaviors. The robot finds tricks to maximize the reward without accomplishing the actual task (reward hacking). Classic example: a robot supposed to walk instead learns to fall in a way that maximizes horizontal displacement. The reward rewarded displacement, not walking.
</Callout>

## Comparative Summary

**PPO**: on-policy, very stable, requires a lot of data but easily parallelizable on GPU. Ideal for large simulations.

**SAC**: off-policy, sample-efficient, naturally explores well. Ideal when data is limited or expensive to obtain.

**TD3**: off-policy, simple, stable. Good trade-off for continuous control when SAC is too complex.

## Recent Trends

- **RL from Human Feedback (RLHF)**: using human feedback (preferences, corrections) to define the reward rather than programming it manually
- **Foundation Models + RL**: an LLM automatically generates reward functions from a natural language task description
- **Offline RL**: learning from pre-collected data without real-time interaction with the environment, reusing existing demonstrations
- **Multi-task RL**: a single agent that learns many tasks simultaneously and can transfer its skills between them
