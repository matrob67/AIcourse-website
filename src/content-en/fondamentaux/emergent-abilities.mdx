## When you scale up a model, weird things happen

Imagine a scale. You add weights, the scale tips gradually. Nothing surprising: more weight, more tipping. But what if, beyond a certain amount, the scale suddenly started to **fly**? That would be a qualitatively new property -- something that didn't exist at all before and appears all at once past a certain threshold.

This is what seems to happen with certain LLM capabilities. Below a certain size, the model is completely incapable of performing a task: its performance is indistinguishable from random guessing. It just doesn't know, period. Beyond a certain size threshold, the capability "clicks" and performance takes off sharply. This phenomenon is called **emergent abilities**, and it was systematically documented by Wei et al. in 2022 at Google Research.

It's one of the most fascinating and most debated phenomena in AI research. And understanding this debate is essential to grasping the current stakes of LLMs.

## What makes a capability "emergent"?

Three criteria define an emergent capability in the strict sense:

1. **Absent in small models**: below a size threshold, performance is close to random. The model doesn't "know" how to do it at all, not even partially.
2. **Present in large models**: beyond the threshold, performance increases significantly and the task is solved reliably.
3. **Not predictable by extrapolation**: if you plot the performance curve of small models and extend it, you don't predict the appearance of the capability. It seems to arise "out of nowhere."

It's this third criterion that makes the phenomenon so intriguing. You'd expect improvements to be gradual: a model 2x larger is a bit better, a model 10x larger is even better. But for emergent capabilities, it's as if the curve stays flat for a long time, then makes a sudden vertical jump.

<Callout type="info" title={"The physics analogy of phase transitions"}>
In physics, a "phase transition" describes a sudden qualitative change: water freezing at 0 degrees, a magnet losing its magnetism beyond a certain temperature (the Curie temperature). Just before the threshold: nothing visibly changes. Just after: everything is different. For LLMs, the analogy is appealing -- but the underlying theory remains much less well understood than in physics. We observe the phenomenon, but we don't fully explain it yet.
</Callout>

## Concrete examples of emergent capabilities

### Multi-digit arithmetic

Ask a 1-billion-parameter model to calculate 347 + 892. Result: essentially random, the model gets it wrong most of the time. At 10 billion parameters, it starts working for 3-digit additions. At 100 billion, the model handles 4-5 digits reliably.

The concrete numbers are striking:
- **Before emergence**: 23% correct answers on 3-digit additions (close to random for this type of task)
- **After emergence**: 85%+ correct answers for the same additions

The transition isn't gradual. You don't go from 23% to 30% to 40% to 50%. You go from 23% to... 23%... 25%... then suddenly 75%, 85%. The model seems to "understand" addition all at once.

### Chain-of-thought reasoning

Ask a small model to "reason step by step before answering." It will produce incoherent steps, non-sequiturs, or simply ignore the instruction and answer directly (often wrong). Starting at around 100 billion parameters, the same instruction produces structured, logical, and often correct reasoning.

This is fascinating because the chain-of-thought (CoT) technique is **useless** below the threshold. It doesn't help at all. And above the threshold, it radically transforms the model's performance. The exact same prompt, word for word, goes from "completely useless" to "game changer" solely based on the model's size.

### Unplanned multilingual capabilities

A model trained primarily on English and French starts, beyond a certain size, to show capabilities in Swahili or Tamil -- languages very marginally represented in its training data. As if the universal linguistic structures learned from the main languages spontaneously transferred to barely-seen languages.

This cross-lingual transfer is one of the most spectacular examples of emergence: the model has barely seen any Tamil, but it has "understood" enough general linguistics to get by.

<Callout type="paper" title={"The systematic study by Wei et al."}>
Wei et al. (2022) documented more than 130 different tasks across the GPT-3, LaMDA, PaLM, and Chinchilla families. For each one, the same pattern: near-random performance below a threshold, then a sharp takeoff. This work had an immense impact on industry strategy -- if key capabilities only appear at large scale, you need to build very large models to unlock them. This study partly justified the massive investments in models with 100+ billion parameters.
</Callout>

## The great debate: does emergence really exist?

This is where things get controversial. The observed phenomenon is real, but its interpretation is the subject of intense debate in the community.

### The argument for: circuits that form

Proponents of "strong" emergence argue that these transitions reflect real structural changes inside the model. At a certain scale, new **computational circuits** form: connections between neurons that didn't exist in smaller models and that enable a new type of reasoning.

Research in **mechanistic interpretability** (understanding the internal mechanisms of models) has indeed found specialized structures, like **induction heads**: attention circuits that learn to copy patterns ("if A follows B once, then the next time B appears, predict A"). These circuits only appear from a certain model size and seem to play a crucial role in in-context reasoning capabilities.

### The argument against: the metric creates the illusion

In 2023, Schaeffer, Miranda, and Koyejo published a provocative paper that calls everything into question. Their thesis: emergence is largely a **measurement artifact**. The phenomenon is real in the data, but it's the way we measure that creates the appearance of a sudden jump.

The problem is subtle but important. Most studies measure capabilities with a **binary metric**: is the answer exactly correct (score = 1) or not (score = 0)? This metric hides the gradual improvement.

<Callout type="warning" title={"The illusion of the binary metric"}>
Example: a model must calculate 347 + 892 = 1239. If the model answers 1238, it gets a score of 0 (wrong). Yet it has the first 3 digits correct -- it has significant *partial* understanding. If the model goes from "1100" to "1230" to "1238" to "1239," each step represents real progress. But with a binary metric, you see 0, 0, 0, 1 -- a sudden "jump" that is actually just a gradual threshold crossing.
</Callout>

The researchers show that if you replace binary metrics with **continuous metrics** (Brier score, log-probability of the correct token, distance to the correct answer), the improvements are **smooth and gradual** at all scales. The "phase transition" disappears. Apparent emergence is often an illusion created by the choice of metric.

### The truth, somewhere in the middle

The reality is probably nuanced, and both sides are partly right:

The *underlying* improvements are gradual -- continuous metrics show this clearly. The model improves little by little, not by quantum leaps. But some capabilities need a **functional threshold** to be truly useful. A calculator that's wrong 30% of the time isn't a functional calculator -- it's unusable in practice, even if it's "a little less wrong" than random.

Emergence in the **functional** sense -- the moment when a capability becomes reliable enough to be usable in practice -- is therefore very real, even if the underlying mechanisms evolve continuously. It's the difference between "the model understands addition a little better" (gradual) and "the model can now be used as a calculator" (functional threshold).

## Practical implications

### For AI safety

If potentially dangerous capabilities can appear unexpectedly at a certain scale, how do you anticipate them before deployment? This is one of the main arguments for systematic safety **evals** (evaluations).

<Callout type="tip" title="Evals as a safety net">
Before deploying a new model, labs test it on hundreds of potentially problematic tasks: biosecurity (can it help create pathogens?), cybersecurity (can it write exploits?), manipulation (can it persuade someone to do something dangerous?). The goal is to detect dangerous emergent capabilities *before* they end up in the hands of the general public. This has become standard practice at Anthropic, OpenAI, Google DeepMind, and others, motivated in part by the discoveries about emergence.
</Callout>

### For the "small but performant model"

The emergence debate has also stimulated research on **efficient small models**. If underlying improvements are gradual, then a small model well-trained (with high-quality data and optimized training techniques) can capture a significant fraction of a large model's capabilities.

Phi-2 (Microsoft, 2.7 billion parameters) and Mistral-7B brilliantly validated this intuition: with careful training on high-quality data, you can surpass models 10 times larger on many benchmarks. Raw size isn't the only lever -- data quality and training techniques matter enormously.

## Emergence beyond model size

Emergence isn't solely tied to the number of parameters. Other factors can trigger capability "jumps":

- **Data quality**: careful filtering of the training corpus can unlock capabilities without making the model bigger. Llama 2 is better than larger models trained on less clean data.
- **Fine-tuning**: instruction tuning makes the ability to follow instructions "emerge" in a base model that didn't do it before. The model had the knowledge, but not the behavior.
- **Prompting**: chain-of-thought makes multi-step reasoning emerge. A model capable of CoT reasoning doesn't use it spontaneously without being prompted -- you have to trigger it through the prompt.
- **Test-time compute**: giving the model more computation time at inference (via multiple attempts, beam search, or longer reflection) significantly improves reasoning. It's a form of emergence through computation rather than size.

Emergence, whether real in the strong sense or a measurement artifact in the weak sense, remains one of the most influential phenomena in LLM research. Understanding when and why new capabilities appear -- whether through size, data, or prompting -- is at the heart of what defines modern LLMs and guides investment decisions across the entire industry.
