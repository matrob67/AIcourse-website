## Introduction: Descending a Mountain in the Fog

Imagine: you're at the top of a mountain in thick fog. You can't see the village down below. But you feel a slope under your feet. You take a step in the direction where it goes down the steepest. Then another. Little by little, you descend.

This is exactly what **gradient descent** does. It measures the "slope" (gradient) and moves in the opposite direction to reduce the error.

## The Fundamental Equation

$$
\theta_{\text{new}} = \theta_{\text{old}} - \eta \cdot \nabla L(\theta)
$$

Where:
- $\eta$ **(learning rate)** = the size of your step
- $\nabla L(\theta)$ **(gradient)** = the direction and magnitude of the slope

If the gradient says "error rises sharply to the right," you move left (negative gradient).

## The Learning Rate: Your Biggest Lever

It's **the hyperparameter that matters the most**.

<ImageWithSource
  src="/images/admin/img-1774006226773.png"
  alt="Comparison of learning rates in gradient descent"
  sourceUrl="https://pub.aimind.so/gradient-descent-best-optimization-technique-bfcb392b95e9"
  size="medium"
/>

### Too High

Learning rate = 0.1. You take huge leaps. You jump over the minimum. Your weights oscillate. Or worse, they diverge (explode toward infinity). The error goes up instead of down.

### Too Low

Learning rate = 0.00001. You advance at a snail's pace. Training takes **days**. And you risk getting stuck in a small local minimum.

### The Goldilocks Zone

You need to find the sweet spot. Not too much, not too little. In practice, you start high then reduce over time (learning rate scheduling).

| Epoch | Learning rate | Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| 1-10 | 0.01 | Big steps to explore |
| 10-50 | 0.001 | Medium steps |
| 50+ | 0.0001 | Tiny steps to fine-tune |

## Three Ways to Compute the Slope

  src="/images/admin/img-1774006499553.png"

  sourceUrl="https://www.geeksforgeeks.org/machine-learning/différent-variants-of-gradient-descent/"
  size="medium"
/>

### Batch Gradient Descent

You compute the gradient on **all** your data before making **a single weight update**.

Advantage: very precise.

Disadvantage: ultra slow. With 1 million examples, you wait a long time before the first update.

In practice: almost never used.

### Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD)

You compute the gradient on **a single example** at a time. Update after each example.

Advantage: very fast.

Disadvantage: noisy, unstable. Gradients from one example can be weird.

In practice: sometimes for streaming data.

### Mini-batch SGD (the standard)

You compute the gradient on **32, 64, or 128 examples** at a time.

Advantage: good trade-off. Fast enough to use GPUs. Stable enough to learn correctly.

Disadvantage: none really.

In practice: **this is what's always used**. When someone says "SGD," they actually mean mini-batch.

<Callout type="info" title="Batch size?">
32-256 is standard. On GPU, you can go 512 or more. On a small computer, 32 is enough. Larger = more stable but fewer updates per epoch.
</Callout>

## Momentum: Avoiding Oscillations

Problem with plain SGD: if the error surface is a "ravine" (very steep in one direction, almost flat in another), the gradient oscillates in a zigzag instead of going straight.

It's like a ball in a narrow bowl. It bounces from one side to the other, slowing down at each turn. Frustrating.

Solution: **accumulate velocity**. Remember past gradients.

$$
v_t = \beta \cdot v_{t-1} + \nabla L(\theta)
$$

$$
\theta = \theta - \alpha \cdot v_t
$$

Intuition: it's like a ball rolling. It gains momentum. Small oscillations cancel out, but the overall acceleration accumulates.

Coefficient $\beta = 0.9$ = typical. You can try $0.99$ (more inertia) or $0.5$ (less).

### Nesterov Accelerated Gradient (NAG)

Clever trick: instead of computing the gradient where you are **now**, compute it where you **would be after the momentum step**.

It's like taking a step forward, looking at the slope there, then adjusting your direction. This helps avoid overshooting minima.

Faster than classic momentum. Used in modern optimizers.

## The Error Landscape in High Dimensions

In 2D, you imagine a mountain. Local minima, plateaus. Simple.

In millions of dimensions (millions of weights), it gets weird:

### Saddle Points vs Local Minima

In very high dimensions, true "trap" local minima are rare. Most stationary points are **saddle points**: one direction goes down, another goes up.

When descending, you naturally pass through the saddle point (continue in the descending direction).

<ImageWithSource
  src="/images/admin/img-1774006696679.png"
  alt="3D surface with saddle point"
  sourceUrl="https://www.bibmath.net/dico/index.php?action=affiche&quoi=./p/pointcol.html"
  size="medium"
/>

### Low-Error Valleys

Good minima (with low error) are often connected by "valleys" of similar error. There isn't **one** minimum, there are thousands that are all good.

This means: you don't need to find **the** best weights. You can stop anywhere in the right valley.

### Skip Connections for a Smoother Landscape

**Residual networks** (ResNets) use "skip connections": data bypasses certain layers. Result: the error landscape is **much smoother**.

<ImageWithSource
  src="/images/admin/img-1774006914578.png"
  alt="Comparison of CNN vs ResNet with skip connections"
  sourceUrl="https://www.ultralytics.com/fr/blog/what-is-resnet-50-and-what-is-its-relevance-in-computer-vision"
  size="medium"
/>

This is why we can train very deep networks (100+ layers) now. Before, they would diverge. Skip connections make gradient descent more robust.

## Instability in Depth

Problem: in backpropagation, gradients must traverse all layers. In deep layers, they amplify (exploding gradients) or vanish (vanishing gradients).

Solutions:

**Gradient clipping**: cap the gradient if too large.

  src="/images/admin/img-1774006995353.png"

**Smart initialization**: Xavier, He initialization. Initialize weights so that gradients pass through layers without exploding/vanishing.

## Practical Summary

Start with SGD + momentum ($\beta = 0.9$) and batch = 64-256.

Learning rate? Use a "learning rate finder": try 0.0001 up to 0.1, see where the error goes down.

If you want something more modern, use **Adam** (next section).

Add a learning rate schedule: start high, reduce progressively.

Simple as that, and it works for the majority of cases.
