## Introduction: Two Ways to Fail

Your model can fail in **two completely opposite ways**.

**Underfitting**: the model is too simple for the data. It doesn't capture the true patterns. Training error is **high**.

**Overfitting**: the model is too complex. It memorizes the noise and specifics of the training data. Training error is **low** but validation error is **very high**.

This is the **bias-variance** trade-off: the foundation of all statistical learning.

## Bias vs Variance

  caption="This diagram illustrates the four possible combinations of bias and variance in a machine learning model. The black dots represent the training data, while the concentric circles show the model's ability to fit the data."
  size="large"
/>

### Bias

This is the model's **systematic error**. It makes overly simplistic assumptions and sticks with them.

Example: you predict that all Paris apartments cost 400k euros (your average), regardless of square footage. That's biased: you're systematically off.

Cause: model too simple for the data.

### Variance

**Sensitivity to small variations** in the data. The model changes drastically if you slightly change the data.

Example: a very deep decision tree learns individual noise. Change one training sample = completely different tree.

Cause: model too complex, overfitting.

### The Error Decomposition

$$\text{Total Error} = \text{Bias}^2 + \text{Variance} + \text{Irreducible Noise}$$

Ideal: small bias + small variance.

Underfitting: high bias + low variance (systematically wrong but stable).

Overfitting: low bias + high variance (fits the training data but unpredictable).

<Callout type="info" title="The target-shooting analogy">
High bias, low variance = all shots to the left (grouped but misplaced).
Low bias, high variance = shots scattered around the target.
Ideal = all shots in the center.
</Callout>

## Recognizing Underfitting

**Signs**:
- High training error. The model can't even learn its own data well.
- Validation error similar to training error. No gap.
- Poor overall performance. 50% accuracy on a 2-class classification?

**Solutions**:
- Increase complexity: more layers, more neurons
- Add richer features
- Reduce regularization (the "brakes" on complexity)
- Train longer
- Try a better architecture

## Recognizing Overfitting

**Signs**:
- Large gap between training and validation error. Train = 2%, Validation = 15%.
- Validation error that goes back up over epochs. At first it goes down, then up.
- Perfect in the lab, terrible in production. Your numbers are excellent, but the user sees a model that doesn't work.

<Callout type="warning" title={"A little overfitting is normal and desirable"}>
Zero overfitting = probably underfitting. You want a **small** amount of overfitting: complex enough for the real patterns, not too much for the noise. It's an **art**, not a science.
</Callout>

## Learning Curves: Your Best Diagnostic Tool

Plot two curves: training error and validation error over epochs.

### Underfitting

Both curves stay high and close together. Adding more data? It won't help (there isn't enough capacity to learn).

### Overfitting

Training error goes down (the model is learning). Validation error goes back up (overfitting to noise). Growing gap = classic signal.

### Ideal

Both curves go down together and stabilize low, with a small gap (0.5-5% max).

## Train/Validation/Test: The Three Sacred Sets

Never train and evaluate on the same data. That's cheating.

<ImageWithSource
  src="/images/admin/img-1774008183135.png"
  alt="Splitting data into training, validation, and test sets"
  caption="This image illustrates the need to divide the available data into three distinct sets: the training set for learning the model, the validation set for tuning hyperparameters, and the test set for evaluating final performance. This prevents evaluation bias and overfitting."
  size="large"
/>

### Training Set (70-80%)

Data on which the model learns. Weights are adjusted on this data.

### Validation Set (10-15%)

Data you look at **during** development. Used to:
- Choose hyperparameters
- Select the architecture
- Decide when to stop training (early stopping)

The model doesn't train on it, you just **observe** it.

### Test Set (10-15%)

Data you **only touch once** at the end. It's your honest estimate of true performance. Sacred. Never used for choosing hyperparameters.

<Callout type="warning" title="Why three and not two?">
If you use the same set to choose hyperparameters AND estimate performance, you'll bias optimistically. You end up choosing the model that wins on *that* set, not the best one. The untouched test set gives an honest estimate.
</Callout>

## Cross-validation: Using 100% of the Data

When you have little data (< 10k), fixed splits are wasteful. Cross-validation uses everything.

**k-fold cross-validation**:
1. Split into k parts (typically 5 or 10)
2. For each part i: train on the other k-1, evaluate on i
3. Final performance = average of k evaluations

Example: 5-fold
| Fold | Training | Evaluation |
|------|----------|------------|
| 1 | Parts 2-5 | Part 1 |
| 2 | Parts 1, 3-5 | Part 2 |
| 3 | Parts 1-2, 4-5 | Part 3 |
| 4 | Parts 1-3, 5 | Part 4 |
| 5 | Parts 1-4 | Part 5 |

Performance = average of the 5 results

Advantage: every data point is used for learning (k-1 times) and for evaluation (1 time).

Disadvantage: k times slower.

## Practical Solutions Against Overfitting

### 1. More Data

The best solution if you can. More diversity makes memorization impossible.

### 2. Data Augmentation

Create variants of your data without actually creating truly new data.

**Images**: rotation, horizontal flip, zoom, random crop, noise, brightness changes.

**Text**: paraphrase, synonym substitution, back-translation (translate then translate back to keep the meaning while varying the phrasing).

**Tabular data**: add Gaussian noise, use SMOTE (Synthetic Minority Oversampling Technique) to oversample rare classes.

Smart augmentation (RandAugment, AutoAugment) automatically chooses the best augmentations.

### 3. Regularization

Add penalties that force the model to stay simple.

**L2 (weight decay)**: penalizes large weights.

**Dropout**: randomly deactivates neurons.

**Early stopping**: stop before the model starts overfitting.

(Details in the next section.)

### 4. Early Stopping

Super simple and super effective. Monitor the validation error during training. As soon as it stops going down (or starts going back up), stop and keep the best weights.

- **Epoch 1-10**: validation error decreases (keep the weights)
- **Epoch 11-15**: validation error goes back up (stop, use epoch 10 weights)

Zero computational overhead. Almost always helps.

### 5. Reduce Complexity

Fewer layers, fewer neurons, simpler model.

Warning: too simple = underfitting.

### 6. Ensembles

Combine multiple models, by voting (majority vote) or stacking. This reduces variance automatically.

Example: 10 decision trees instead of 1. The random variation of one is canceled out by the others.

## Double Descent: Counter-intuitive

Recent research: very large models (over-parameterized) can generalize **better** than intermediate-sized models. This is the "double descent" phenomenon:

The error increases then decreases again. This phenomenon explains why giant LLMs (Large Language Models) with billions of parameters work so well despite their enormous over-parameterization.

## Quick Diagnostic Checklist

1. Plot training error vs validation error
2. Large train/validation gap? -> **Overfitting**. Regularize, add data, reduce complexity
3. High training error? -> **Underfitting**. Increase capacity, train longer
4. Both low and close? -> **Good fit**. Validate on test set
5. Check your data: labeling errors, weird outliers create an error ceiling

Quickly diagnosing the problem = the skill that separates experienced practitioners from beginners.

## Why This Matters

Overfitting and underfitting are the two main enemies of applied ML. Until you master how to recognize and fix them, you're building blind. It's the most important thing after having data.
