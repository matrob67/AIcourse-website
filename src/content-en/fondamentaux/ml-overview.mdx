## Introduction: The Dream of a Machine That Learns

Imagine: you need to filter 10,000 emails a day to catch spam. You could write rules: "If the subject contains URGENT AND make money, it's spam." But spammers adapt quickly. Tomorrow, they'll use different words. You'll spend your life writing rules that don't work.

Now imagine an alternative: you show 1 million emails to a system and tell it "this one is spam, this one isn't." Without writing a single rule, the system learns to recognize patterns. A new spam arrives? The system adapts. That's Machine Learning.

<Callout type="info" title="Key Definition">
Machine Learning is about letting the data speak for itself. Instead of writing rules by hand, you provide examples and the algorithm discovers the rules on its own. The more examples you give it, the better it gets.
</Callout>

Tom Mitchell, in 1997, put it more formally: a system learns from experience E on a task T with a performance measure P, if P improves with E. In plain English: the more examples you show me (E), the better I do the job (T), and you can measure it (P).

## Traditional Programming vs Machine Learning

### Traditional Approach: The Rule-Coding Developer

You're a developer. You need to detect spam. You start writing:
- If the subject contains "URGENT" AND "make money" -> spam
- If the sender is unknown AND more than 3 links -> spam
- If it's signed by a bank but the address looks sketchy -> spam

It works... for 2 months. Then spammers change their tactics. They stop using "URGENT." Now they use "IMPORTANT!!!" You need to write a new rule. And another one. And another one. It's an endless race: you're chasing the spammers, but you never catch up.

### Machine Learning Approach: The Automatic Learner

Different approach: you give 100,000 emails to the system. "Here are 50,000 spam, 50,000 legitimate." The system looks at patterns: common words in spam, structures, length, suspicious senders, sketchy links... It learns. A new spam arrives with a never-before-seen tactic? If it resembles what it's seen (even loosely), it recognizes it.

The system adapts without you rewriting the code. These aren't hard-coded rules: they're learned patterns.

## The Three Main Types of Learning

Just as there are several ways to learn (with a teacher, by exploring on your own, through trial and error with rewards), ML has three major "schools."

### 1. Supervised Learning: Learning with a Teacher

This is the most common one. You show pairs: "Here's an image of a cat. It's a cat." "Here's a price for this apartment: $420,000." The model learns to predict: "For an image that looks like this, it's a cat." "For an apartment with this area and this neighborhood, the price is around $400,000."

**Concrete Examples:**
- Apartment prices: you show 10,000 sold apartments + their prices. The model learns the relationship.
- Recognizing cats in photos: 100,000 photos labeled "cat," 100,000 labeled "not cat."
- Banking fraud: 10 million transactions: 99.9% legitimate, 0.1% fraud. The model learns to spot suspicious transactions even among an immense majority of normal ones.

<Callout type="tip" title={"The Teacher Analogy"}>
It's exactly like school. The teacher shows you the answers (labeled data). You study them. Then you take the exam (new data) without the answers. If you've understood the patterns well, you pass.
</Callout>

### 2. Unsupervised Learning: Learning by Exploring

No teacher here. No labels. Just raw data. "Here are 100,000 customers. Organize them into groups. Figure it out."

It's as if you arrived in an unknown city with no map, no one to tell you where to go. You explore and notice patterns: people live near the center, there are wealthier neighborhoods, poorer ones. You create your own categories.

**Concrete Examples:**
- E-commerce: 1 million customers. The model finds: "customers who buy often in small amounts," "occasional customers who spend big," "visitors who never take the plunge." Each group receives a different marketing strategy.
- Images: show 1 million photos without labels. The model learns: "these photos are visually similar," "these groups are totally different."
- Detecting outages: 1,000 servers, 10 years of logs. Detects anomalies: "this server is doing something weird, even if we don't have a 'failure' label."

### 3. Reinforcement Learning: Learning by Trial and Error

A robot or agent learns by doing. It tries an action. If it works, it receives a reward. If it fails, a penalty. It learns to maximize the cumulative reward over time.

It's like a child learning to walk: nobody tells them "contract muscle A then B then C." The child falls (penalty), succeeds in taking a step (reward), and learns through repetition.

**Concrete Examples:**
- AlphaGo plays Go. With each move, it receives a reward (win) or a penalty (loss). By playing millions of games, it discovers the best strategy.
- A robot learning to grasp an object: "Great, you grabbed it! +10 points." "Oops, you dropped it. -5 points."
- ChatGPT: the model generates text. Humans say "I like this answer" or "this one is terrible." The model learns to generate what humans prefer (this is RLHF: reinforcement learning from human feedback).

## A Brief History of Machine Learning

ML wasn't born yesterday. Here are the major milestones that led to the current revolution.

### The Origins (1950-1980)

- **1943**: McCulloch and Pitts propose the first mathematical model of an artificial neuron.
- **1957**: Frank Rosenblatt invents the **perceptron**, one of the first learning algorithms.
- **1969**: Minsky and Papert publish *Perceptrons*, showing the limitations of the simple perceptron (XOR problem). This triggers the first "AI winter."

### Renaissance and Foundations (1980-2000)

- **1986**: Rumelhart, Hinton, and Williams popularize **backpropagation**, enabling the training of multi-layer neural networks.
- **1995**: Vapnik introduces **Support Vector Machines** (SVM), which dominate the field for a decade.
- **1997**: Deep Blue (IBM) beats Garry Kasparov at chess using a combination of tree search and heuristic evaluation.

- **2006**: Geoffrey Hinton shows how to pre-train deep networks layer by layer.

## Why is ML Exploding Now?

Three factors converge to explain the recent explosion of Machine Learning:

### 1. Massive Data

The internet, smartphones, connected devices, and social media generate phenomenal amounts of data. In 2025, humanity produces about 400 million terabytes of data per day. ML needs data to learn, and we've never had this much.

### 2. Accessible Computing Power

GPUs (Graphics Processing Units), initially designed for gaming, turned out to be perfectly suited for the matrix computations of ML. NVIDIA has transformed its business around AI computing. Cloud computing (AWS, GCP, Azure) has made this power accessible to everyone.

### 3. More Powerful Algorithms

Theoretical advances (Transformers, attention mechanisms, optimization techniques) have made it possible to build more powerful and efficient models. The open-source community (Hugging Face, PyTorch, TensorFlow) has democratized access to these tools.

<Callout type="info" title="The ML Trinity">
Data + Compute + Algorithms = the recipe for modern ML success. The absence of any one of these three ingredients was the main bottleneck in previous decades.
</Callout>

## Essential Vocabulary

Before moving on, let's establish some fundamental terms:

- **Model**: a parameterized mathematical function that transforms inputs into predictions.
- **Training**: the process by which the model adjusts its parameters based on the data.
- **Inference**: using the trained model to make predictions on new data.
- **Features**: the input variables used by the model (area, age, color, etc.).
- **Label**: the output variable you're trying to predict in supervised learning.
- **Dataset**: the collection of data used for training and evaluation.
- **Overfitting**: when the model memorizes the training data and doesn't generalize well.
- **Underfitting**: when the model is too simple to capture the patterns in the data.

## Real-World ML Applications Today

ML is everywhere, often invisibly:

- **Healthcare**: early cancer detection on medical images, pandemic prediction, drug discovery.
- **Finance**: fraud detection, algorithmic trading, credit scoring.
- **Transportation**: autonomous vehicles, route optimization, traffic prediction.
- **Commerce**: product recommendations (Netflix, Spotify, Amazon), price optimization.
- **Language**: machine translation, conversational assistants, text generation.
- **Science**: protein structure prediction (AlphaFold), climate simulation.

## What ML Can't Do (Yet)

It's important to keep a critical eye. Current ML has its limitations:

- **Causal reasoning**: models find correlations, not causations.
- **Out-of-distribution generalization**: a model trained on certain data can fail when faced with very different situations.
- **Explainability**: deep models are often "black boxes" that are hard to interpret.
- **Common sense**: despite the progress of LLMs, common-sense reasoning remains a challenge.

<Callout type="warning" title="Watch Out for Bias">
An ML model is only as good (or bad) as the data it's trained on. Biased data produces biased models. This is a major ethical issue in AI.
</Callout>
