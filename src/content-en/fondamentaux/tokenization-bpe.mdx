## The Story: From Text to Numbers

Neural networks only understand numbers. You read "Hello world". Your brain understands language.

A language model sees: [15234, 18203, 91028]. Numbers.

How do you convert "Hello world" into numbers in a way the model can understand? That's the role of **tokenization** (i.e., splitting text into elementary units).

This invisible step is **critical**. Bad tokenization = model goes haywire, high costs, poor performance. Good tokenization = everything works.

Example: if "Hello" becomes 5 tokens instead of 1, your context shrinks by 80%. Catastrophe.

### Approach 1: Word-level Tokenization

Split on spaces. "The cat sleeps" = 3 tokens.

**Huge problems**:

1. **Infinite vocabulary**: there are infinitely many words. "Antidisestablishmentarianism", "supercalifragilisticexpialidocious", proper nouns, slang, neologisms. Impossible to store them all.

2. **Unknown words**: any word outside the vocabulary becomes `[UNK]` (unknown). The info disappears. The model just sees noise.

3. **No morphological understanding**: "eat", "eating", "eaten" are 3 different tokens. The model doesn't see the common root "eat-".

Concrete example: a model trained on 50k English words encounters "antidisestablishmentarianism" (rare). [UNK]. Information lost.

### Approach 2: Character-level Tokenization

Each letter = 1 token. "chat" = 4 tokens (c, h, a, t).

Advantage: small vocabulary (26 letters + accents + punctuation = ~50-100 tokens).

Problem: "Hello world" = 11 tokens instead of 2. Sequences explode. The model becomes very slow, and attention becomes O(11^2) instead of O(2^2).

### The Compromise: Subwords

Between these two extremes, there's a **sweet spot**:

Intermediate vocabulary (32k-100k tokens). Frequent words = 1 token. Rare words = multiple coherent sub-tokens.

"antidisestablishmentarianism" -> ["anti", "dis", "establish", "ment", "arian", "ism"]

Each piece is understandable. The model generalizes. No unknown words.

<Callout type="info" title="The dilemma">
Too much vocabulary = huge embedding matrix, sparse, inefficient.
Too little = long sequences, slow attention, unknown words.
Subwords = the elegant and universally used compromise.
</Callout>

## BPE: Byte Pair Encoding

**BPE** (Byte Pair Encoding) is **the dominant algorithm**. Invented in 2016 (Sennrich et al.), used by GPT-2, GPT-3, GPT-4, and virtually all popular LLMs (Large Language Models).

### The Core Idea: Merge Frequent Pairs

The algorithm is elegantly simple:

1. Start with all characters in the corpus as tokens
2. Count adjacent pairs (a,b), (b,c), etc.
3. The most frequent pair becomes a new token
4. Replace all occurrences
5. Repeat until reaching the desired vocabulary size

Concrete example:

**Initial corpus**: "bas bas bas hug hug pug bug" -- Vocabulary: `{b, a, s, h, u, g}`

**Iteration 1**:
- Pair (b, a) appears 4 times, pair (a, s) appears 4 times, pair (h, u) appears 3 times
- -> Merge (a, s): create token "as"

**Iteration 2**:
- Pair (b, as) appears 3 times, pair (h, u) appears 3 times
- -> Merge (b, as): create token "bas"

... continue until 50k or 100k tokens

### The Detailed Algorithm

**Phase 1: Initialization**

You start with a text corpus. Represent each word as a sequence of characters with a special end-of-word symbol:

| Word | Character sequence |
|---|---|
| "the" | [t, h, e, &lt;/w&gt;] |
| "cat" | [c, a, t, &lt;/w&gt;] |
| "sleeps" | [s, l, e, e, p, s, &lt;/w&gt;] |

**Phase 2: Training (once)**

Count all adjacent pairs and merge the most frequent ones:

**Round 1**: Pair (c, a) in "cat" = frequent -> create token "ca" -- corpus becomes: [t, h, e], [ca, t], [s, l, e, e, p, s]

**Round 2**: Pair (ca, t) = frequent -> create token "cat" -- corpus becomes: [t, h, e], [cat], [s, l, e, e, p, s]

... continue until 50k merges

Result: an ordered list of 50k merges (a merge strategy).

**Phase 3: Inference (used afterwards)**

When you have new text "cat":
1. Decompose into characters: [c, a, t]
2. Apply merges in the trained order
3. [c, a] -> [ca], [ca, t] -> [cat], etc.
4. Final result: [cat]

A common word = 1-2 tokens. An unknown word = several tokens. Perfect.

<Callout type="tip" title="Training vs inference">
**Training**: analyze a corpus to find common merges (done once, offline).
**Inference**: apply the merge list to new text (done every time, online).
Same algorithm, two different roles.
</Callout>

### Real Example: "unconstitutionally"

Take this long and rare English word.

English training corpus: BPE discovers that "un-" (prefix), "-tion" (suffix), "-ally" (suffix) are frequent. The word decomposes intelligently:

"unconstitutionally" -> **["un", "constitu", "tion", "ally"]**

Each piece is meaningful. The model learns:
- "un-" = negation prefix (un, unknown, unable, ...)
- "-tion" = nominal suffix (action, nation, ...)
- "-ally" = adverbial suffix (ally, finally, ...)

Result: the model generalizes. Understanding "un-" in "unconstitutional" helps it understand "unable", even if it has never seen that word.

This is the magic of BPE: **automatic** morphological decomposition, without supervision.

## Vocabulary Size: The Big Trade-off

The **single parameter** of BPE is the final vocabulary size (50k? 100k?).

| Vocabulary | Example | Sequence length | Memory | Generalization |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 8k | Very small | Long | Small | Excellent |
| 32k-50k | Standard | Medium | Medium | Good |
| 100k+ | Large | Short | Huge | Medium |

**Why does large vocabulary = short sequence?** It's counter-intuitive at first. The analogy: count in base 2 vs base 10. In base 2 (2 symbols), `42` is written `101010` -- 6 digits. In base 10 (10 symbols), `42` -- 2 digits. The larger the "symbol vocabulary", the fewer you need to represent the same information.

Same logic for tokens: with an 8k vocabulary, `"tokenize"` breaks down into `["to", "ken", "ize"]` = 3 tokens. With a 100k vocabulary, `"tokenize"` is probably a single token = 1 token. Large vocab -> each token encodes more text -> fewer tokens per sentence -> shorter sequences.

**Sizes in real models**:
- GPT-2: 50k tokens
- GPT-4 (tiktoken): 100k tokens
- LLaMA: 32k tokens

The choice depends on the corpus, languages, and computational budget.

<Callout type="info" title="The trade-off">
Vocab 2x bigger = embedding 2x bigger = more memory and compute. BUT shorter sequences = fewer tokens to process, less O(n^2) attention. The benefit can offset in the right conditions.
</Callout>

## Byte-level BPE: The Trick for Universality

**Important variant**: BPE can start from Unicode characters (language-sensitive), or from **bytes** (256 universal values).

Byte-level BPE = starting from the 256 bytes of the UTF-8 representation.

Result: **any human text**, in any language, any encoding, can be tokenized. Zero unknown `[UNK]` tokens.

Chinese "你好"? Tokenize.
Arabic "السلام عليكم"? Tokenize.
Emojis "🚀"? Tokenize.
Python code `def foo():`? Tokenize.

Used by GPT-2, GPT-3, GPT-4, LLaMA. The universal solution.

## Limits of BPE (Honestly)

BPE is excellent, but not perfect:

**1. Deterministic but suboptimal**
A single tokenization strategy per word. But maybe a different split would be better? BPE never explores it. It's just the greedy choice: at each step, merge the most frequent pair without ever going back.

**2. Dependent on the training corpus**
A tokenizer trained on 99% English and 1% French tokenizes French poorly. The French word can become 2 to 3 times more tokens. Your context shrinks.

**3. Not probabilistic**
BPE gives one and only one split. No alternatives. No uncertainty. The Unigram algorithm (an alternative) assigns probabilities to multiple splits.

<Callout type="tip" title="Real-world debugging">
When your model goes haywire in weird ways, check the tokenization **first**. Use the OpenAI or Hugging Face playgrounds. That's often where the invisible bugs hide: a rare word tokenizes badly, blows up your context, and the model gets confused.
</Callout>

## Why This Matters

Tokenization is **invisible but omnipresent**. It affects:
- Your context length (a rare word = multiple tokens = reduced context)
- The model's arithmetic (numbers are tokenized inconsistently)
- Performance in non-English languages (2 to 3 times more tokens)
- Mysterious bugs (a poorly tokenized word = the model collapses)

Understanding BPE helps you understand why your model behaves strangely. It's often the tokenization.

## Key Takeaways

1. **The problem**: how to encode text as numbers?
2. **The naive approaches**: by words (infinite vocab) or characters (long sequences)
3. **The solution**: subwords via BPE
4. **The algorithm**: iteratively merge the most frequent pairs
5. **Byte-level BPE**: zero unknown tokens, works for all languages
6. **Vocabulary size**: 32k-100k, critical parameter
7. **The limits**: deterministic, corpus-dependent, not probabilistic
8. **The reality**: tokenization is the #1 source of invisible errors
