## You built a Ferrari... and put lawnmower gas in it

Imagine spending millions to build a Formula 1 race car. Titanium engine, perfect aerodynamics, 1000 horsepower. You put it on the track... and it loses to a well-tuned Honda Civic.

That's exactly what happened with large language models (LLMs) before 2022. The industry was building bigger and bigger models -- hundreds of billions of *parameters* (the model's "internal settings") -- but feeding them too little data. In March 2022, a team at DeepMind published a study that would change everything.

<ArxivLink id="2203.15556" title="Training Compute-Optimal Large Language Models (Hoffmann et al., 2022)" />

The paper is named after **Chinchilla**, the small model they built to prove their theory. And this small model would humiliate giants.



## First, some definitions

Before going further, let's clarify the terms we'll use throughout:

- **Parameters**: the millions (or billions) of internal numbers that define a model's behavior. Think of them as the "neurons" of the artificial brain. GPT-3 has 175 billion of them.
- **Tokens**: the small chunks of text the model trains on. A token is roughly 4 characters or 3/4 of a word. "hello" = 1 token, "artificial intelligence" = 2 tokens.
- **Compute** (or *compute budget*): the amount of computing power used to train the model, measured in *FLOPs* (Floating Point Operations). It's like measuring effort in calories burned.
- **Loss**: the model's error. The lower the loss, the better the model. The goal of training is to bring it down.

## The Kaplan study: the first scaling guide

In 2020, a team at OpenAI (Kaplan et al.) published the first "scaling laws." Their message: if you double your compute budget, invest primarily in more *parameters*, not more data. The industry followed this advice... and started building increasingly massive models.

GPT-3: 175 billion parameters. Gopher (DeepMind): 280 billion. Megatron-Turing (Microsoft/NVIDIA): 530 billion.

But Kaplan may have been wrong.

## What DeepMind discovered

The DeepMind team approached the problem differently. Instead of just "scaling up" models, they trained **over 400 models** of very different sizes -- from 70 million to 16 billion parameters -- systematically varying the ratio between parameters and data.

It's like testing 400 cake recipes by changing the flour/sugar proportions to find the perfect recipe.

They used three different methods to confirm their results. All three reached the same conclusion:

<Callout type="paper" title="The Chinchilla rule">
For **compute-optimal** training (meaning the best possible model for a given compute budget), the number of training tokens should be approximately **20 times** the number of parameters. A 10-billion-parameter model should be trained on 200 billion tokens.
</Callout>

In formula terms, it's simple:

$$
\text{tokens}_{\text{optimal}} = 20 \times N
$$

Example: 70-billion-parameter model $\rightarrow$ required tokens $= 20 \times 70 \times 10^9 = 1{,}4 \times 10^{12}$ (1.4 trillion tokens)

## Kaplan vs Chinchilla: clash of the titans

Here's the concrete difference between the two approaches, if you multiply your compute budget by 10:

| | Kaplan (2020) | Chinchilla (2022) |
|---|---|---|
| Parameters | Multiply by 5.5 | Multiply by 3.2 |
| Data | Multiply by 1.8 | Multiply by 3.2 |
| Token/parameter ratio | about 5:1 | about 20:1 |
| Philosophy | Larger model, little data | Balance between both |

According to Kaplan, data didn't matter that much. According to Chinchilla, they matter *just as much* as parameters. It's a radical paradigm shift.


<Callout type="info" title={"The Gopher example: the giant with feet of clay"}>
Gopher, DeepMind's large model, had 280 billion parameters trained on 300 billion tokens -- a 1:1 ratio. According to the Chinchilla rule, it would have needed either 5,600 billion tokens for that number of parameters, or a model of only 15 billion parameters for 300 billion tokens. Gopher was massively "underfed" on data.
</Callout>

## The proof: little Chinchilla crushes the giants

To validate the theory, the team trained their test model: **Chinchilla**.

| | Gopher | Chinchilla |
|---|---|---|
| Parameters | 280 billion | 70 billion |
| Tokens | 300 billion | 1,400 billion |

Same total compute budget. 4x smaller size. 4x more data.

Result? Chinchilla **beats Gopher on almost every benchmark**. It even surpasses GPT-3 (175 billion) and Megatron-Turing (530 billion) on many tests -- with only 70 billion parameters.

The 4x smaller model, better fed, wins. The well-tuned Civic beats the starving Ferrari.

And that's not all. A smaller model also means:

- Cheaper to run in production (less expensive *inference*)
- Less GPU memory needed for deployment
- Faster responses for users
- Easier to *fine-tune* (adapt to a specific task)

## The impact on the entire industry

### The data race begins

After Chinchilla, AI labs pivoted to massive data collection. If data matters as much as parameters, then having better data than your competitors becomes a huge competitive advantage.

Entire open-source projects were born to build trillion-token corpora: **RedPajama**, **The Pile**, **FineWeb**, **Dolma**. The obsession was no longer "how many GPUs" but "how many quality tokens."

### LLaMA: the direct child of Chinchilla

**LLaMA** (Meta, 2023) directly applies the Chinchilla philosophy. LLaMA-1 7B was trained on 1 trillion tokens -- well beyond the recommended ratio. Result: a model of "only" 7 billion parameters that rivals much larger models.

LLaMA-3 8B pushes even further: 15 trillion tokens for 8 billion parameters.

| | Token/parameter ratio |
|---|---|
| LLaMA-3 8B | 15,000B / 8B = **~1,875** |
| Chinchilla recommended | **20** |

LLaMA-3 is **~94x** beyond the Chinchilla recommendation!

<Callout type="tip" title={"Beyond Chinchilla-optimal: optimizing for inference"}>
The industry realized that it's often smart to go *well beyond* the Chinchilla ratio. Why? Because Chinchilla optimizes for training cost. But in practice, a model can be trained once and used billions of times. A smaller but over-trained model costs more to produce, but much less to deploy. And it's the deployment cost that dominates in the long run.
</Callout>

## The limits: not everything is rosy

### The data wall

If you need 20 tokens per parameter, a one-trillion-parameter model would require 20 trillion high-quality tokens. Problem: the quality text available on the internet is *finite*.

| Text type | Estimate |
|---|---|
| High-quality English | ~5 to 10 trillion tokens |
| Total (variable quality) | A few tens of trillions of tokens |

We're approaching a ceiling. This is one of the great challenges of scaling for the years ahead.

### Quality vs quantity: not all tokens are equal

Chinchilla counts tokens but doesn't distinguish their quality. Yet a token from a peer-reviewed scientific article doesn't have the same value as a token from a sketchy forum comment. Recent research on data filtering (like HuggingFace's FineWeb) shows that improving quality can partially compensate for a lack of quantity.

<Callout type="warning" title="An empirical guide, not a law of physics">
The Chinchilla ratios are recommendations based on experiments, not mathematical truths set in stone. They vary depending on data quality, model architecture, training techniques, and especially what you're optimizing for: training cost or inference cost. It's a valuable compass, not an absolute GPS.
</Callout>

## In summary

Chinchilla changed a fundamental industry belief: "bigger = better." The real lesson: **bigger AND better fed = better**. Data matters as much as parameters. A well-trained 70-billion model beats an underfed 280-billion model. And with smaller models, inference costs less -- which changes everything when you want to deploy at scale.
