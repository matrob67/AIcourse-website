## The revolutionary idea: making two networks fight

In 2014, Ian Goodfellow invented the GAN during a discussion at a bar (according to legend). The approach is **radical**: instead of training a single network to generate data, you use **two** that train **against** each other in an adversarial process.

The classic metaphor: a **counterfeiter** (the generator) producing fake bills versus a **detective** (the discriminator) trying to spot them. The counterfeiter improves to fool the detective, the detective improves to unmask the counterfeiter. After millions of iterations of this game, the fake bills become indistinguishable from real ones.

What makes this idea so remarkable is that it draws from game theory rather than classical statistics. You don't explicitly define what a "good" image is -- you let the discriminator figure that out on its own, and the generator adapts accordingly.

### The generator G

The generator is a neural network that transforms **random noise** z into synthetic data (typically images):

$$x_{\text{fake}} = G(z), \quad z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$$

The vector z is sampled from a standard Gaussian distribution. This noise vector is the only "seed" from which the generator must create something realistic. Two different z values yield two different images. A slightly modified z yields a slightly modified image (in theory).

For image generation, the typical architecture starts with dense layers that transform the z vector into a small spatial tensor (e.g., 4x4 with many channels), then **transposed convolutions** progressively increase the resolution: 4x4 -> 8x8 -> 16x16 -> 32x32 -> 64x64. Each step doubles the spatial resolution while reducing the number of channels, until you get an RGB image at the desired resolution.

### The discriminator D

The discriminator is a binary classifier that answers a single question: "is this image real or generated?"

$$D(x) \rightarrow [0, 1] \quad \text{(probability that the image is real)}$$

It's a standard CNN (Convolutional Neural Network): convolutions that progressively reduce spatial resolution, followed by a dense layer and a sigmoid. The architecture is essentially the inverse of the generator: where G upscales, D downscales.

The discriminator alternately receives real images from the dataset and images generated by G, and must learn to tell them apart. At first it's easy (the fake images are noise), but as G improves, the task becomes increasingly challenging.

## The minimax game

The entire genius of GANs lies in their objective function:

$$\min_G \max_D \; \mathbb{E}_{x}[\log D(x)] + \mathbb{E}_{z}[\log(1 - D(G(z)))]$$

Let's break down this formula to understand it well:

- **$\mathbb{E}_{x}[\log D(x)]$**: on real images x, the discriminator wants $D(x)$ to be close to 1. The log of 1 = 0, that's the maximum. The discriminator **maximizes** this term.
- **$\mathbb{E}_{z}[\log(1 - D(G(z)))]$**: on fake images G(z), the discriminator wants $D(G(z))$ to be close to 0, so $\log(1 - 0) = 0$. The generator, however, wants $D(G(z))$ to be close to 1, so it **minimizes** this term (which equals $\log(1-1) = -\infty$).

It's a zero-sum game: what one gains, the other loses.

### Alternating training

Training is done by alternating two phases at each iteration:

**Phase 1 -- Train D (the detective)**:
- Take a batch of real images x from the dataset
- Generate a batch of fake images G(z) from noise
- The discriminator learns to give a high score to real images and low to fakes
- Update **only** D's weights

**Phase 2 -- Train G (the counterfeiter)**:
- Generate a batch of fake images G(z) from noise
- Pass them through D (whose weights are frozen)
- The generator learns to produce images that maximize D(G(z))
- Update **only** G's weights

Back and forth, back and forth. The balance between the two is crucial: if one gets too far ahead, everything collapses.

<Callout type="info" title={"The theoretical equilibrium"}>
At the optimum, the generator produces data indistinguishable from real, and the discriminator can no longer decide -- it answers 0.5 for any image. This is a **Nash equilibrium**: neither player can improve their situation by unilaterally changing strategy. In practice? This equilibrium is extremely hard to reach, and training is notoriously finicky.
</Callout>

## Training catastrophes

GANs are famous not only for their spectacular results, but also for the difficulty of their training. Here are the most common problems.

### Mode collapse

This is the most well-known plague. The generator learns to produce only **a few types of outputs** that effectively fool the discriminator, instead of covering the full diversity of real data.

Concrete example: you train a GAN on MNIST (handwritten digits). Instead of generating digits 0-9 in a balanced way, the generator specializes in producing perfect "9"s. Why? Because it's simpler to perfect a single mode than to master the full diversity. The generator finds a "recipe" that works and sticks to it.

It's a bit like a student who only studies one chapter for the exam: they know it perfectly, but fail everything else.

### Training instability

- **Oscillations**: instead of converging to a Nash equilibrium, D and G oscillate indefinitely. The generator finds a trick, the discriminator adapts, the generator changes strategy, etc. The loss curves form waves that never dampen.
- **Gradient vanishing**: if the discriminator is too good too quickly, it rejects all fake images with 99.99% confidence. The gradient the generator receives is then nearly zero ($\log(1 - 0.9999) \approx -9$, tiny gradient). The generator has no signal left to improve -- it's "blind."
- **Imbalance**: if one of the two networks dominates the other, the loser collapses and training never recovers.

Tuning hyperparameters (learning rates, architecture, D/G training ratio) is absolutely critical and often more art than science.

<Callout type="tip" title="Stabilization heuristics">
The community has developed many tricks to tame GANs:
- **Label smoothing**: D trains with labels of 0.9 instead of 1.0 for real images (avoids overconfidence)
- **Train D more often**: 3-5 iterations of D for every 1 of G (maintain the detective's lead)
- **Spectral normalization** in D (control the Lipschitz constant)
- **Add noise** to D's inputs (prevent discrimination from being too easy at the start)
- **Monitor FID**, not raw loss (GAN loss is notoriously uninformative)
</Callout>

### Non-convergence

Unlike classical models that minimize a single loss and converge to a minimum, GANs are a **two-player game**. There is no theoretical guarantee of convergence to a Nash equilibrium for deep networks. This is a fundamental problem of game theory applied to deep learning.

A surprising practical consequence: the loss value is often **useless** for judging quality. A model with high loss can generate excellent images, while a model with low loss can be in the middle of mode collapse. That's why external metrics like FID are used.

## Evaluating GANs

How do you know if a GAN generates good images? You can't rely on the loss. You need dedicated metrics.

### FID (Frechet Inception Distance)

This is the **standard metric**. The idea: you pass real images and generated images through a pre-trained Inception network to extract their features (internal representations). Then you measure the distance between the two feature distributions by modeling each as a multivariate Gaussian.

Low FID = the distributions are similar = the generated images are close to real ones. An FID of 0 would mean the two distributions are identical. In practice, an FID under 10 is excellent, under 50 is decent.

### IS (Inception Score)

IS evaluates two complementary aspects:
- **Quality**: each generated image individually should be clearly recognizable by a classifier (distribution concentrated on one class)
- **Diversity**: the set of generated images should cover many different classes (uniform marginal distribution)

IS is less reliable than FID because it doesn't directly compare to real data, but it remains used as a complementary metric.

<Callout type="info" title="GAN vs VAE vs Diffusion">
GANs produce sharp images because the discriminator directly optimizes perceptual quality. VAEs tend to produce blurry images because they optimize pixel by pixel (MSE, Mean Squared Error). Diffusion models produce ultra-sharp images but require hundreds of denoising steps. Historically, GANs dominated image generation. Today, diffusion models surpass them in quality and training stability.
</Callout>

## Key variants

### Conditional GAN (cGAN)

The basic GAN generates images "at random" -- you have no control over the content. The cGAN solves this by **conditioning** generation on additional information y (a class, text, a reference image).

The generator receives z + y, the discriminator receives x + y. This way, you can ask "generate me a dog" or "transform this sketch into a realistic photo." It's the foundation of pix2pix and many practical applications.

### DCGAN (Deep Convolutional GAN)

Before DCGAN (2015), GANs were extremely unstable. Radford et al. proposed a set of **architectural best practices** that changed the game:
- Strided convolutions instead of pooling
- Batch normalization in G and D
- ReLU in G, LeakyReLU in D
- No fully connected layers (except G's input and D's output)

These simple rules made GANs finally usable in a reliable way. DCGAN is the starting point for virtually all GAN architectures that followed.

### Wasserstein GAN (WGAN)

WGAN (2017) replaces the Jensen-Shannon divergence with the **Wasserstein distance** (also called "Earth Mover's Distance" -- imagine the cost of moving a pile of dirt to give it a different shape). Instead of asking the discriminator "real or fake?", you ask it to score images with a real number (it becomes a "critic" rather than a classifier). The loss is more stable, gradients are more informative, and mode collapse is reduced. WGAN was a major theoretical advance for understanding and stabilizing GANs.

## The legacy

Despite their training problems, GANs left a considerable mark:
- **First photoreal image generation** (StyleGAN, ProGAN)
- **Super-resolution**: transforming a low-resolution image into high resolution
- **Style transfer and face synthesis**: FaceApp, deepfakes, portrait aging
- **Data augmentation**: generating synthetic data for training
- **Adversarial principles** in robustness and domain adaptation

<Callout type="paper" title={"The post-GAN era"}>
Diffusion models have largely surpassed GANs for high-quality image generation. But GANs retain important niches:
- **Real-time generation**: a single forward pass vs hundreds of denoising steps for diffusion
- **Embedded applications**: when speed matters more than ultimate quality
- **Hybrid research**: some works combine GANs and diffusion for the best of both worlds
- **Distillation**: diffusion models are "distilled" into one-step generators inspired by GANs
</Callout>

## Summary

GANs embody a bold idea: two networks that compete against each other to progress together. The generator creates, the discriminator judges, and from this competition emerge images of striking realism. But this adversarial approach comes with its share of difficulties: mode collapse, instability, non-convergence. Diffusion models have taken over as the dominant method, but understanding GANs remains essential -- the adversarial principle shows up everywhere in modern deep learning, from adversarial training to model robustness.
