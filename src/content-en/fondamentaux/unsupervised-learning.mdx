## Introduction: Learning Without a Teacher

Imagine: you arrive in an unknown city with no map, no guide. Nobody tells you "this is the wealthy neighborhood" or "this is the train station." But you explore and naturally notice: "These buildings are all new and large, people are dressed fancy. This is a wealthy area." "Here it's old, noisy, smells like fish... this is the old port."

This is unsupervised learning. No labels. No teacher. Just raw data. You have to discover the structure on your own.

<Callout type="info" title={"Why does it matter?"}>
99% of the world's data is unlabeled. Labeling is expensive: it requires humans, time, and expertise. Unsupervised learning lets you exploit millions of raw data points without ever paying a human to label them.
</Callout>

## Clustering: Grouping Similar Items

**Clustering** = partitioning data into groups where each group contains similar things.

Example: an e-commerce site has 1 million customers. No labels. But clustering discovers:
- Group A: buy often, small amounts, loyal (frequent buyers)
- Group B: buy rarely, huge amounts (rare but big spenders)
- Group C: just visitors, 95% never buy (window shoppers)

Each group receives a different marketing strategy. No need to label them by hand.

### K-Means: The Easy Classic

The most popular clustering algorithm. How it works:

1. Decide the number of clusters K (2, 3, 5, whatever).
2. Place K random points (the "centers" or centroids).
3. Assign each point to the nearest center.
4. Recalculate the centers as the average of points in each group.
5. Repeat steps 3-4 until nothing changes (convergence).

The centroids move toward the "center of gravity" of each group.

**Strengths**:
- Super fast (linear complexity)
- Simple to understand and code
- Works well when clusters are roughly spherical

**Limitations**:
- You need to choose K in advance (what's the "right" number of clusters?)
- Sensitive to initialization (you can get stuck in a bad plateau)
- Assumes clusters of similar shape (spherical)

<Callout type="tip" title="Choosing K with the Elbow Method">
Plot a graph: X = number of clusters K, Y = inertia (total distance). The curve drops sharply then flattens out. The "elbow" (the transition point) is the optimal K. Visually, it's a curve that looks like a bent arm.
</Callout>

### DBSCAN: Finding Real Structures

A completely different approach: instead of looking for centers, it looks for **dense** regions.

Principle:
- A point that has many close neighbors (e.g., at least 5 points within a radius of 0.5) is a "core point"
- Connect core points that are close together -> that's a cluster
- Isolated points (not enough neighbors) -> that's noise/anomalies

**Strengths**:
- No need to specify K
- Detects clusters of **any shape** (not just spherical)
- Automatically detects anomalies (isolated points)

**Limitations**:
- Problematic if clusters have very different densities
- Sensitive to the epsilon (radius) and minPts (minimum number of neighbors) parameters

### Hierarchical Clustering: The Tree of Groups

Builds a hierarchy represented as a "dendrogram" (a tree).

**Agglomerative (bottom-up)**: each point starts alone. Iteratively merges the two closest clusters.

**Divisive (top-down)**: everything starts together. Iteratively divides.

**Advantage**: you see the structure at all scales. You can cut the tree anywhere to get whatever number of clusters you want.

## Dimensionality Reduction: Seeing the World in 2D

Your data has 1,000 dimensions? 10,000 features? That's too much to visualize or even compute efficiently.

Dimensionality reduction projects data into a smaller space (typically 2D or 3D) while **preserving the important information**.

### Why Reduce?

**Visualization**: impossible to see 1,000 dimensions. Reducing to 2D lets you explore the data visually.

**Acceleration**: fewer dimensions = faster algorithms.

**Denoising**: the least important dimensions are often pure noise. Removing them helps.

**The curse of dimensionality**: in very high dimensions, all points become very far apart from each other. K-Means, KNN, etc. become useless.

### PCA: Following Maximum Variance

**PCA (Principal Component Analysis)**: looks for the directions where your data varies the most.

Imagine: 10,000 points in 3D space. But they're all aligned along a line. The true dimension is 1, not 3. PCA finds that line.

How it works:
1. Normalize the data (subtract the mean)
2. Compute the covariance matrix (how each feature varies with the others)
3. Find the "principal axes" (the directions of maximum variance)
4. Project onto the top 2-3 axes

**Strengths**: fast, deterministic, mathematically clear.

**Limitations**: only captures linear relationships.

### t-SNE: For Beautiful Visualizations

**t-distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding**: designed **for visualization only**, not for preprocessing.

Idea: preserves **local structures** (nearby points stay nearby).

Result: very readable visualizations where you can clearly see the clusters. Magical.

**But be careful**:
- It's slow (O(n squared))
- Non-deterministic (each run is different)
- Never use t-SNE as input to an ML algorithm (it distorts the geometric properties of the data). It's purely a visualization tool.

### UMAP: t-SNE But Better

**Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection**: a more recent and better alternative.

Advantages:
- 10-100x faster than t-SNE
- Better preserves the global structure
- Scalable to millions of points
- Can even be used in preprocessing (not just visualization)

Today, UMAP is generally preferred over t-SNE for exploring large datasets.

## Anomaly Detection

The goal here is to identify observations that deviate significantly from normal behavior.

Usage examples:
- Cybersecurity: detect intrusions
- Finance: detect fraud
- Maintenance: detect machines close to failure
- Healthcare: detect rare cases/pathologies
- Monitoring: detect dying servers

Common techniques:

**Isolation Forest**: builds random trees. Measures how many splits are needed to isolate a point. Anomalies are isolated faster (fewer splits).

**Autoencoders**: a neural network that compresses then reconstructs data. Trained only on normal data. An anomaly (never seen) will be poorly reconstructed (high reconstruction error).

**One-Class SVM**: learns a boundary around normal data. What's outside = anomaly.

## Real-World Use Cases

**Customer segmentation**: an e-commerce site groups its 10 million customers into 5 to 10 segments. Each segment receives a personalized marketing campaign. The ROI (Return On Investment) increases by 40%.

**Data exploration**: Before building a supervised model, use PCA or UMAP to visualize. It helps you see: do the classes overlap? Are there weird outliers? Are my features useful?

**Compression**: PCA reduces 1000x1000 images to 50 dimensions. That takes up 1,000x less space. Then you can reconstruct the image (95% quality).

**Recommendation systems**: Matrix factorization discovers hidden preferences. Netflix groups movies into "latent features" (action-level, romance-level, etc.). Then recommends similar movies.

## Supervised vs Unsupervised: The Table

| Aspect | Supervised | Unsupervised |
|---|---|---|
| Data | Labeled (X, y) | Raw (X only) |
| Objective | Predict y | Discover structure |
| Evaluation | Clear (accuracy, MSE) | Subjective, visual |
| Examples | Classification, regression | Clustering, reduction |
| Main challenge | Obtaining labels | Interpreting results |

## Why This Matters

Unsupervised learning opens you up to analyzing raw real-world data. This is how Netflix discovers that you like science fiction. This is how banks detect fraud, and how science reveals hidden structures in data. Almost no company can label all its data. Unsupervised learning is the key to leveraging it.
