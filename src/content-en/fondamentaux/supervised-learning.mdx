## Introduction: Labeled Data for Learning

Imagine you need to teach a robot to recognize fruits. You show it 1,000 photos: "This one is an apple." "This one is an orange." "This one is a banana." After seeing hundreds of apples, the robot starts to see patterns: round shape, red or green color, particular texture. Then you show it an apple it's never seen before. It recognizes: "That's an apple!"

This is supervised learning. Each training example says "here's the input, here's the expected answer." The model learns to predict the answer for new inputs.

<Callout type="info" title={"Why 'supervised'?"}>
Because there's a "supervisor" (you or the labeled data). You tell the model: "Look, here's the right answer." The model learns. It's like having a teacher who corrects you immediately.
</Callout>

## The Fundamental Concept: Labeled Data

Each example for supervised learning contains two parts:

**Features**: the input variables that describe the problem. To predict the price of an apartment: area in sq ft, number of rooms, floor, neighborhood, year built.

**Label**: the answer you're trying to predict. For the apartment: its actual sale price ($350,000, $500,000, etc.).

A training dataset looks like a table:

| Area (sq ft) | Rooms | Floor | Neighborhood  | Price (USD) |
|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| 485 | 2 | 3 | Manhattan | 380,000 |
| 880 | 4 | 1 | Brooklyn   | 420,000 |
| 270 | 1 | 5 | Queens | 210,000 |

The model sees these patterns and learns: "Large area + central = high price. Small area + outer borough = low price."

<Callout type="warning" title="Label Quality is Crucial">
If you mislabel your data ("This spam is a legitimate email!"), the model learns garbage. We say "garbage in, garbage out": bad data in = bad model out.
</Callout>

## Classification: Predicting a Category

**Classification** is answering a yes/no question: "Cat or not cat?", "Spam or not spam?", "Sick or not sick?" The answer is a category from a finite set.

### Binary Classification: Two Choices

Only two possible categories. Examples:
- Email: spam or not spam
- Bank transaction: fraudulent or legitimate
- Medical imaging: malignant or benign tumor
- Customer review: positive or negative

The model typically produces a score between 0 and 1, interpreted as "probability of being spam." If the score exceeds 0.5, we say "spam." Otherwise, "not spam."

### Multiclass Classification: More Than Two Choices

When you have 3, 4, or 10 possible categories.
- Handwritten digit recognition (MNIST): classes from 0 to 9 (10 categories)
- Face recognition: person A, person B, person C...
- Document classification: sports, politics, economics, culture, science

### Evaluating a Classifier: The Real Metrics

The most intuitive metric is **accuracy**: it's simply the percentage of correct answers. But it's often misleading. Imagine you need to detect cancer. If you say "no cancer" for every patient and 99% are indeed healthy, you get 99% accuracy --- but you miss every sick patient, making the model completely useless.

That's why we use two complementary metrics instead.

**Precision** measures the reliability of your positive predictions: among all the cases you predict as positive, how many actually are? For example, if you label 100 messages as "spam" and 80 of them are actually spam, your precision is 80%.

**Recall**, on the other hand, measures your ability to detect all positive cases: among all the true positives in the data, how many do you manage to find? For example, if there are 1,000 actual spams in your inbox and you detect 800 of them, your recall is 80%.

<Callout type="tip" title="The Fundamental Trade-off">
Precision and recall are often in opposition. To increase precision (fewer false alarms), you say "spam" less often... but you miss more actual spams (recall drops). The context determines the right balance: in fraud detection, you want high recall (don't miss a fraud). In movie recommendations, you want high precision (don't recommend a bad movie).
</Callout>

## Regression: Predicting a Numerical Value

**Regression** is predicting a real number, not a category. Examples:
- House price: $450,000 (not "expensive" or "cheap," but an exact number)
- Tomorrow's temperature: 59.5 degrees Fahrenheit
- Revenue: $5.2 million
- A person's age from a photo: 32 years old

### Measuring Regression Quality

You can't use accuracy (these aren't categories). You use:

**MSE (Mean Squared Error)**: average of (prediction - true value) squared.
$$
\text{MSE} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} (\hat{y}_i - y_i)^2
$$
Heavily penalizes large errors. If you predict $500,000 instead of $400,000 (an error of $100,000), that counts as (100,000)^2 = 10 billion in the error. The model doesn't forgive big mistakes.

**RMSE (Root Mean Squared Error)**: the square root of MSE. It puts it back in the same unit as your variable. Easier to read.

**MAE (Mean Absolute Error)**: average of absolute errors (no squaring). More "forgiving" with outliers.

**R-squared**: measures the proportion of variance in the data that your model explains. An R-squared of 1 means the model perfectly predicts every value. An R-squared of 0 means the model is no better than just predicting the mean. In practice, an R-squared of 0.85 is often excellent for real-world noisy data.

## The Algorithms: Which Tool for Which Problem

### Linear Regression: The Basic That Works

The simplest one. Looks for a line (or a plane in higher dimensions) that minimizes the error.

In 1D: $y = a \times x + b$

Example: $\text{price} = 5{,}000 \times \text{area} + 50{,}000$. Each square foot adds $5,000 to the base price of $50,000.

**Strengths**: simple, fast, interpretable. "Each sq ft = $5,000."

**Limitations**: only captures linear relationships. If the price depends on $\text{area}^2$ (not just area), it misses it.

### Logistic Regression: The Misleading Name

Despite its name, it's a **classification** algorithm, not regression. It uses the sigmoid function to transform a linear output into a probability between 0 and 1.

$$
\sigma(z) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-z}}
$$

The sigmoid has a characteristic S-shape: when z is very large (a very confident decision), the output approaches 1. When z is very small (very negative), it approaches 0. And for values of z close to zero, the transition is smooth and continuous. This property makes it useful: it transforms any real score into a value interpretable as a probability, without ever leaving the [0, 1] interval.

If sigma(z) > 0.5, we say "class 1." Otherwise, "class 0."

**Strengths**: simple, provides probabilities, interpretable.

**Limitations**: the decision boundary is linear. For complex problems, it's not enough.

### SVM (Support Vector Machine): Maximum Separation

SVMs (Support Vector Machines) are algorithms that look for the optimal separation boundary between two classes. Imagine two groups of points (spam vs not spam) on a plane. SVMs look for the line that separates them **with the maximum margin**: the distance between the line and the closest points.

It's like wanting the widest possible highway between two groups of enemy houses.

### Decision Trees: The Human Algorithm

Divides the space hierarchically with binary questions.

```text
If Area > 650 sq ft
  If Neighborhood = Central
    -> High price
  Else
    -> Medium price
Else
  -> Low price
```

It's exactly how a human decides.

**Strengths**: very readable, no need to normalize data, handles mixed numeric/categorical features.

**Limitations**: huge tendency to overfit, unstable (small variation = completely different tree).

### Random Forests: Many Trees, One Decision

Trains 100 or 1,000 different decision trees (each on slightly different data). For a prediction, asks all trees to vote.

**100 trees vote.** 60 say "spam," 40 say "not spam." Result: **spam**.

**Strengths**: very robust, little overfitting, almost no hyperparameters to tune.

**Limitations**: less readable than a single tree, slower to train.

<Callout type="info" title={"Gradient Boosting: The Winning Alternative"}>
XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost outperform random forests. Each new tree corrects the errors of the previous one. It's often the winner of Kaggle competitions on tabular data.
</Callout>

### KNN (K-Nearest Neighbors): The Lazy Algorithm

The K-Nearest Neighbors algorithm is disarmingly simple: when you need to classify a new example, look at the K closest training examples. Majority vote.

A new customer arrives. Look at their 5 nearest neighbors in the training data. 4 bought, 1 didn't. Prediction: **will buy**.

**Strengths**: no training at all, very simple.

**Limitations**: slow at inference (must scan all the data), sensitive to the "curse of dimensionality."

## Choosing Your Algorithm: The Practical Guide

| Situation | Algorithm | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Little data, high dimension | SVM | Generalizes well |
| Tabular data, max performance | XGBoost/LightGBM | Wins competitions |
| I want to understand | Logistic regression, tree | Interpretable |
| I'm starting out | Linear/logistic regression | Fast and basic |
| Images, text, audio | Deep neural networks | They changed the game |
| Mixed data, basic | Random Forest | Robust and easy |

<Callout type="tip" title="The Secret of Real Practitioners">
You ALWAYS start with a stupidly simple baseline: linear regression for regression, logistic for classification. It takes 5 minutes. Then, only if it's not good enough, you try the complex stuff.
</Callout>

## Why This Matters

Supervised learning is the foundation of 95% of ML (Machine Learning) applications in production. This is how you predict prices, detect fraud, and diagnose diseases. It's the best understood paradigm, the most debuggable, and the most reliable. Mastering it means having the foundations of applied AI.
