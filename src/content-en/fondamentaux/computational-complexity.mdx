## Why does a GPS find your route in 2 seconds, but struggles with 1,000 cities?

You use Google Maps every day. Finding the shortest path between two points? Easy, solved in milliseconds. But now, imagine you're asked to find the shortest circuit visiting 1,000 cities exactly once (the famous **traveling salesman problem**). Even the world's best supercomputers can't solve it optimally. Why?

The answer comes down to two letters: **P** and **NP**. And this distinction is perhaps the most important question in all of computer science -- including for AI.

## Complexity classes: P, NP, and the wall

### The P class: the "easy" problems

A problem is in **P** (Polynomial time) if it can be solved in a time that grows polynomially with the input size. Concretely:

- **Sorting a list** of $n$ elements -> $O(n \log n)$
- **Shortest path** in a graph (Dijkstra) -> $O(n^2)$
- **Multiplying two matrices** $n \times n$ -> $O(n^3)$

When $n$ doubles, the computation time is multiplied by 4, 8, or 16. That's manageable. A polynomial algorithm, even a slow one, remains **tractable**: you can run it in practice.

### The NP class: easy to verify, hard to find

**NP** (Nondeterministic Polynomial) contains problems whose solution can be **verified** in polynomial time, even if **finding** it seems exponentially hard.

The classic analogy: imagine a giant Sudoku of size $n \times n$. Verifying that a filled grid is correct? Easy, you check each row, column, and block. But finding the solution from a blank grid? The number of possibilities explodes.

<Callout type="warning" title="P = NP?">
The question "P = NP?" is one of the 7 Millennium Prize Problems (one million dollar prize). It asks: can every problem whose solution can be verified quickly also be *solved* quickly? Most computer scientists think not (P ≠ NP), but nobody has managed to prove it.
</Callout>

### NP-complete: the hardest of the hard

**NP-complete** problems are the "final bosses" of NP. A problem is NP-complete if:
1. It's in NP (verifiable in polynomial time)
2. **Every** other problem in NP can be reduced to it

If you solve a single NP-complete problem in polynomial time, you solve **all of them** -- and you prove that P = NP. Famous examples:

| Problem | Description | Why it's hard |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| **SAT** (Satisfiability) | Find a variable assignment that makes a logical formula true | $2^n$ possible combinations |
| **Traveling Salesman** | Shortest circuit through $n$ cities | $n!$ permutations to explore |
| **Knapsack** | Maximize value in a bag with limited capacity | $2^n$ subsets |
| **Graph Coloring** | Color a graph with $k$ colors without conflicts | Combinatorial explosion |

## What does this have to do with AI?

You might be wondering: what does this have to do with machine learning? In fact, **the connection is deep**.

### Training is NP-hard

Training a neural network optimally (finding the weights that perfectly minimize the loss) is an **NP-hard** problem -- meaning at least as hard as NP-complete problems. In theory, gradient descent shouldn't work. Yet it works surprisingly well in practice. Why?

- **Loss landscapes** in high dimensions have many paths to good solutions (no catastrophic local minima in sufficiently wide networks)
- We don't look for the **optimal** solution, just a **good enough** one
- **Overparameterization** creates so many acceptable solutions that SGD (Stochastic Gradient Descent) finds one easily

It's like looking for an exit in a maze: in 2D it's a struggle, but in 1,000 dimensions, there are exits everywhere.

### Model complexity vs problem complexity

**Attention** in Transformers has $O(n^2)$ complexity in memory and compute, where $n$ is the sequence length. It's polynomial, so it's in P -- but in practice, for sequences of 100,000 tokens, this $n^2$ becomes a wall. That's why Flash Attention, linear attention, and all the optimizations we see in the State of the Art section were invented.

Complexity doesn't just define what's "possible" or "impossible." It dictates what's **practically feasible** with current hardware.

## Kolmogorov complexity: to compress is to understand

There's another way to think about complexity, deeper and more connected to artificial intelligence. It's **Kolmogorov complexity**.

### The fundamental idea

The Kolmogorov complexity of an object (a string of characters, an image, a dataset) is the **length of the shortest program** that can produce it.

Consider these two 1,000-character strings:

- `aaaaaaaaaaaa...` (1,000 times "a") -> Program: `print("a" * 1000)` -- about 20 characters. **Low complexity**: very compressible.
- `xK9$mP2c&Lw...` (1,000 random characters) -> No program shorter than the string itself. **Maximum complexity**: incompressible.

$$K(x) = \min\{|p| : U(p) = x\}$$

where $K(x)$ is the Kolmogorov complexity of $x$, $p$ is a program, $|p|$ its length, and $U$ a universal Turing machine.

### Compression = Prediction = Intelligence

Here's the key connection: **a good compressor is a good predictor, and vice versa**. If you can predict the next token in a sequence, you can compress it efficiently (you only encode the surprises). And if you compress well, it's because you've captured the patterns -- you've *understood* the data's structure.

This is exactly what LLMs do. GPT-4, Mistral, Claude: they are fundamentally **compressors** of the internet. They learned to predict the next token, which amounts to finding regularities (compressible patterns) in trillions of words.

<Callout type="info" title="The connection to Ilya Sutskever's reading list">
It's no coincidence that Ilya Sutskever's famous 30-paper reading list (co-founder of OpenAI) includes both the book *Kolmogorov Complexity and Algorithmic Randomness* by Shen, Uspensky, and Vereshchagin, and the paper *The First Law of Complexodynamics* by Scott Aaronson. For Ilya, understanding computational complexity is essential to understanding AI.
</Callout>

### MDL: when theory meets practice

The **MDL** (Minimum Description Length) principle directly applies Kolmogorov's intuition to machine learning: the best model is the one that **best compresses** the data, meaning the one that minimizes the sum:

$$\text{MDL} = L(\text{model}) + L(\text{data} | \text{model})$$

- $L(\text{model})$: how many bits to describe the model (its weights)
- $L(\text{data} | \text{model})$: how many bits to describe the residual errors

A model that's too simple describes the data poorly (lots of errors). A model that's too complex describes itself in too many bits. The sweet spot is **Occam's razor formalized**: the simplest solution that explains the data.

This is exactly what L1/L2 and regularization do: penalize the "description length" of the model. Weight decay? That's MDL in disguise.

## Emergent complexity: the coffee paradox

One last fascinating concept, directly from the papers recommended by Sutskever: **emergent complexity**.

Scott Aaronson and Sean Carroll studied a curious phenomenon with their "coffee automaton": when you pour milk into black coffee, there's a brief moment where the swirls form **extremely complex** patterns -- neither the uniform black coffee from the start (simple), nor the blended latte at the end (also simple). Complexity **emerges in the middle**, between initial order and final chaos.

It's a powerful metaphor for AI: the most interesting models are neither trivially simple nor chaotically complex. They capture the **intermediate structure** of the world -- patterns that are neither obvious nor random. That's precisely where intelligence resides.

## Why all this matters for you

| Concept | Practical impact in AI |
|---------|----------------------|
| **P vs NP** | Explains why optimal training is impossible, but SGD works anyway |
| **$O(n^2)$ complexity** | Dictates the limits of attention and motivates Flash Attention, linear attention |
| **Kolmogorov** | Compression = prediction = intelligence. LLMs are compressors |
| **MDL** | Theoretical foundation of regularization and model selection |
| **Emergent complexity** | The best models capture the intermediate structure of the world |

Computational complexity isn't just an abstract math topic. It's the **theoretical framework** that explains why AI works, where its limits are, and how to push them. When you optimize a model, when you choose an architecture, when you decide the size of your context window -- you're doing complexity theory, whether you know it or not.
