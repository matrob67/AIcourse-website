## The Story: Two Problems, Two Revolutions

Word2Vec was brilliant, but incomplete. Two teams identified its flaws and proposed complementary solutions.

**GloVe** (Global Vectors for Word Representation, 2014, Stanford): "Word2Vec looks locally, just a window of 5-10 words around. Yet there are **global statistics** in the entire corpus that contain enormous amounts of information. Why ignore them?"

**FastText** (2017, Facebook): "Word2Vec and GloVe give one vector per word. But a word never seen before? They don't know what to do with it. Why not decompose words into small pieces to handle the unknown?"

Two flaws, two elegant solutions. Let's look at them in detail.

## GloVe: The Big Picture Against Myopia

### The Word2Vec Problem That GloVe Fixes

Word2Vec slides a window (5-10 words) over the corpus and learns local contexts. It's effective, but it poses a subtle problem: the model **forgets** what it saw a few sentences ago. It only retains the current window.

Yet the global structure of the corpus contains valuable information. Let's take a concrete example with the words "ice" and "steam":
- Across the entire corpus, "ice" appears with "solid" 500 times
- "steam" appears with "solid" only 5 times
- The ratio is 100:1

This ratio **contains powerful semantic information**: it directly encodes the fact that ice is a solid and steam is not. If you look at the ratio for "gas": "steam" + "gas" is much more frequent than "ice" + "gas". These co-occurrence ratios capture fine relationships between concepts.

Word2Vec doesn't exploit them, because it never looks at the corpus as a whole. It just sees the local window, sentence by sentence.

### The Strategy: Build and Factorize a Matrix

GloVe proceeds in two steps.

**Step 1: Count.** You build a **gigantic co-occurrence matrix**. The cell (i, j) contains the number of times word i and word j appear near each other across the entire corpus. For a vocabulary of 400,000 words, it's a 400k x 400k matrix. Huge, but you don't store it entirely: it's sparse (most word pairs never appear together).

**Step 2: Compress.** Instead of using this raw matrix (far too large to be directly useful), GloVe looks for dense vectors of small dimension (typically 300) that **reproduce** the co-occurrence statistics:

$$
\vec{w}_i \cdot \vec{w}_j \approx \log\bigl(\text{co-occurrence}(i, j)\bigr)
$$

The idea: the dot product of two word vectors should reflect how often these words appear together. It's a form of **matrix factorization** (decomposing the large word-by-word co-occurrence matrix into two small matrices, whose rows become the embedding vectors): you look for low-dimensional vectors that best approximate the true corpus statistics. You optimize these vectors by gradient descent, like a classic neural network.

### The Result: The Best of Both Worlds

**Old methods** like LSA (Latent Semantic Analysis) used the entire co-occurrence matrix, but required singular value decomposition (SVD), which is extremely slow on large vocabularies. **Word2Vec** was fast thanks to gradient-based training, but only looked locally. **GloVe** combines both: fast gradient-based training (like Word2Vec) + use of global statistics from the entire corpus (like LSA).

<Callout type="tip" title="GloVe embeddings in numbers">
Pre-trained on Wikipedia (6 billion words) and Common Crawl (840 billion). The 300-dimensional vectors long dominated word similarity benchmarks. Before BERT, GloVe was the industry standard.
</Callout>

### Concrete Results

GloVe embeddings pre-trained on Wikipedia were **the standard** in NLP for years (2014-2018). They rival Word2Vec on analogies (king - man + woman = queen) and often outperform Word2Vec on **word similarity**, precisely because global statistics better capture fine semantic relationships.

## FastText: Solving the Infinite Vocabulary Problem

### The Problem That Kills Word2Vec and GloVe

Imagine you've trained Word2Vec or GloVe on a standard English corpus. Both models have 300,000 words in their vocabulary. Now, the model encounters a word it has never seen: "antidisestablishmentarianism", a technical term like "geolocation", or even a simple typo "aple" instead of "apple".

The model's answer: "I don't know this word. No embedding."

This is a fatal problem in several contexts:
- **Morphologically rich languages**: in Turkish, a single verb can have 20+ conjugated forms. In Finnish, it's virtually infinite. Storing every form is impossible.
- **Specialized corpora**: medical terms, legal jargon, molecule names. Rare but crucial words.
- **Social media**: typos, abbreviations, neologisms that appear and disappear every day.
- **Proper nouns**: names of people, regional places, brands.

Word2Vec and GloVe crack under the unknown. And in the real world, the unknown is everywhere.

### The Genius Solution: Decomposing Words into Pieces

**FastText** (Facebook, 2017) flips the paradigm. Instead of having one vector per whole word, you have:
- Vectors for **word pieces** (character n-grams, typically size 3 to 6)
- The final embedding of a word = the **sum of all its pieces' vectors**

For "apples" (with n-grams of size 3-6), the pieces are:

&lt;ap, app, appl, apple, pples, ples, les, es&gt; (the &lt; and &gt; markers indicate the start/end of the word)

$$
\text{embedding}(\text{"apples"}) = \sum_{\text{piece} \in \text{n-grams}} \vec{v}_{\text{piece}}
$$

It's simple but powerful: each word is the sum of its parts. And these parts are **shared** across thousands of words.

### Why It Works for Unknown Words

New word never seen, "applesauce"? Let's decompose it into pieces:

&lt;ap, app, appl, pple, ples, lesa, esau, sauc, auce, uce&gt;

The pieces "ap", "app", "appl" are **shared** with "apples", "application", "appetite". Even if "applesauce" was never seen in the training corpus, its pieces exist in other words and have already-learned vectors. The resulting embedding is a combination of known pieces, giving a **reasonably relevant** vector.

It's like encountering an unknown word but recognizing its Latin roots: you don't know the exact word, but you can guess its meaning thanks to the familiar pieces.

### The Hidden Talent: Spelling Mistakes

"apples" vs "appls" (a common typo):

| Word | N-grams |
|---|---|
| apples | &lt;ap, app, appl, pples, ples&gt; |
| appls (typo) | &lt;ap, app, appl, ppls, pls&gt; |

70% of the pieces are identical! Both words will have very similar embeddings. Result: a semantic search with FastText doesn't miss results because of a typo. For a search engine or chatbot, this is a huge practical advantage.

<Callout type="tip" title="FastText excels where morphologies explode">
In Finnish or Turkish, a single verb can have 20-30 conjugated forms. Word2Vec would need to have seen each of these forms during training. FastText? A single stem decomposed into pieces is enough. Near-perfect coverage with a table 100 times smaller. This is also why FastText is particularly popular for low-resource multilingual applications.
</Callout>

## All Three in One Table

| Criterion | Word2Vec | GloVe | FastText |
|---|---|---|---|
| Looks at | Locally (window) | Globally (entire corpus) | Locally (window) |
| Unknown words? | No | No | Yes (via subwords) |
| Morphology | Weak | Weak | Excellent |
| Training speed | Fast | Moderate | Moderate |
| Frequent words | Good | Very good | Good |
| Rare words | Weak | Weak | Good |
| Typo resistance | None | None | Good |

## The Gap That Will Remain: Static vs Contextual Embeddings

Here, we need to mention a fundamental limitation that Word2Vec, GloVe, and FastText all share: they produce a **single vector per word**, regardless of the context in which that word appears.

"Bank" (the financial institution) and "bank" (the riverbank) = **same embedding**. The model blends both meanings into a single vector, a kind of "average" of the two meanings. This is a major problem for all tasks where polysemy matters (that is, almost all of them).

### The Contextual Revolution

Between 2018 and 2024, NLP shifted toward **contextual embeddings**, which give a different vector for each occurrence of a word based on its context:

- **ELMo** (Embeddings from Language Models, 2018): uses the hidden layers of a bidirectional LSTM network. Each occurrence of a word produces a slightly different embedding depending on the surrounding words. First step toward contextual.
- **BERT** (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers, 2018): uses bidirectional Transformer attention. "The bank lends money" and "The river bank is muddy" generate **completely different** vectors for "bank". Context radically transforms the representation.
- **GPT** (Generative Pre-trained Transformer, 2018+): same idea with causal (unidirectional) attention, for generation.

The result: massively better performance on almost all NLP tasks (classification, entity extraction, similarity, question answering). Static embeddings have become, for the most part, fossils.

<Callout type="warning" title="But static embeddings aren't dead">
In certain specific cases, static embeddings remain relevant:
- **Ultra-critical latency**: a table lookup = 1 millisecond. BERT = 100ms. For real-time applications (search engines, recommendations), that matters.
- **Limited memory**: a GloVe table = 1 GB. BERT = 400 MB of weights + computational cost. For edge computing (phone, IoT), static wins.
- **Data-poor languages**: for Bambara or Lao, a BERT training corpus doesn't exist. A pre-trained static embedding (even cross-lingual) can save the day.
- **Quick exploration**: visualizing 100,000 embeddings in t-SNE to explore a corpus. Fast, intuitive, still used by data scientists.
</Callout>

## Why This Matters

GloVe and FastText showed two fundamental directions for improvement:
- **GloVe**: exploiting **macroscopic** statistics (how often words co-occur across the entire corpus) produces better embeddings than only looking at local windows.
- **FastText**: decomposing words into morphemes or character n-grams solves the infinite vocabulary problem and handles complex languages with elegance.

These two insights **persist** in modern models. BERT uses a form of global vision (its bidirectional attention sees the entire text at once). Modern tokenizers (BPE, WordPiece) rely on subword decompositions, directly inspired by the FastText approach.

Together, Word2Vec, GloVe, and FastText form the **conceptual foundation** of 15 years of NLP. Understanding their strengths and limitations is understanding why modern models are built the way they are.
