## The simple formula that works: predict the next word

An **LLM** (Large Language Model) is a neural network -- almost always a Transformer -- trained on **a single simple task**:

**Predict the next token given all the previous ones.**

> **Given**: "The black cat"
> **Objective**: Predict what comes next
> **Answer**: "sleeps", "jumps", "meows" (with probabilities)

What makes it "Large"?
- **Billions of parameters**: GPT-1 (117M) < GPT-3 (175B) < PaLM (540B) < Llama 3.1 (405B)
- **Trillions of training tokens**: gigantic corpus of web text, books, code, scientific articles
- **Massive compute**: thousands of GPUs for months

### The miracle: a mundane task creates intelligence

The task is **trivial**. Predict the next word. Just that.

And yet... at sufficient scale, there emerge:
- World knowledge (knowing that Paris is the capital of France)
- Mathematical logic (solving equations)
- Multi-step reasoning (breaking down problems)
- Code (generating functional Python)
- Translation (without translation training data!)

None of these capabilities are "programmed." They simply **emerge** from the prediction task.



<Callout type="tip" title="Tokenization: not words, pieces">
LLMs don't process "whole words." They break text into **tokens** (word pieces or subwords):

| Input | Tokens |
|---|---|
| "extraordinary" | ["extra", "ordinary"] or ["ext", "raord", "inary"] |
| "the" | ["the"] |
| "!" | ["!"] |

Average ratio: 1.3 tokens per word in English.

Common algorithms: BPE (Byte-Pair Encoding), WordPiece, SentencePiece.
</Callout>

## The three levers of progress: scaling beyond limits

The entire history of LLMs (2018-2024) is: **increase three variables and watch the model become smarter**. It's not complicated, but it works every time.

### Lever 1: Parameters (network size)

| Model | Year | Parameters |
|---|---|---|
| GPT-1 | 2018 | 117 million |
| GPT-2 | 2019 | 1.5 billion |
| GPT-3 | 2020 | 175 billion |
| PaLM | 2022 | 540 billion |
| Llama 3.1 | 2024 | 405 billion |

**Observation**: bigger = smarter (with scaling and time).

### Lever 2: Data (volume of training text)

| Model | Training tokens |
|---|---|
| GPT-1 | 1 billion |
| GPT-3 | 570 billion (and much more after dedup) |
| Llama 3.1 | 15 trillion |

**Observation**: more varied text = better pattern learning.

### Lever 3: Compute (GPU power)

| Model | Compute |
|---|---|
| GPT-3 | $3.6 \times 10^{23}$ operations = \$4.6M in GPUs |
| PaLM | ~10x more operations |
| Llama 3 | estimated \$100M+ in compute |

**Observation**: more GPU-days = better training.

### The secret: it's predictable

Kaplan et al. (OpenAI, 2020) discover that **performance follows a regular mathematical curve**:

$$
\text{Performance} \propto (\text{Parameters} \times \text{Data} \times \text{Compute})^{-\alpha}
$$

In log-log, it's a straight line. No surprises, no magic thresholds. Just a power law.

**Consequence**: you can plan. "I want 85% performance. How many GPUs do I need?" -> mathematical formula.

That changed everything. Before: "Innovate and hope." After: "Scale and win."

<Callout type="info" title="The debate: can we scale infinitely?">
Scaling laws describe the pre-training loss (a statistical metric). But some tasks may have "plateaus": rapid improvement up to 85%, then flat. Open debate in 2026.
</Callout>

## "Emergent" capabilities: the scaling puzzle

At certain size thresholds, completely **new** capabilities appear. No specialized training, just: "The model is bigger and suddenly it can do X."

Examples:

### In-context learning
GPT-2 can't do this. GPT-3 (175B)? Give 3 examples in the prompt and it works.

> **Few-shot:**
> Sentiment: "I love it!" -> positive
> Sentiment: "Meh..." -> neutral
> Sentiment: "Excellent!" -> ?
>
> GPT-3 responds correctly ("positive") without fine-tuning.

### Chain-of-thought
GPT-2 gives an answer. GPT-3 + the instruction "Let's think step by step"?

> **Question**: If I have 3 apples and my friend gives me 5, then I share half with my mom...
>
> **GPT-3**: "Step 1: $3 + 5 = 8$. Step 2: $8 / 2 = 4$. Answer: 4 apples"
>
> Explicit reasoning! And it helps performance.

### Code generation
Not specifically trained on code, just a generic LLM. GPT-3.5?

> **Prompt**: "Write a Python function that checks if a number is prime"
> **Output**: Correct, efficient, commented code.

### Zero-shot translation
Never trained on Chinese to French. GPT-3 does it anyway.

> "Translate from Chinese to French: 你好 ->"
> GPT-3: "Bonjour"

### The philosophical debate

**Question**: do these capabilities truly **emerge** (radically new behavior), or do they **reveal themselves** (were latent, become visible with scaling)?

**Active debate in 2026**:
- "Emergence" side: some tasks go from 0% to 80% at once when you double the size
- "Revelation" side: maybe it's just a metric thing. Making visible ≠ emerging

We don't know. It's fascinating and debated.

<Callout type="warning" title="Capabilities are sometimes unstable">
GPT-3 can do translation. GPT-4 much better. But "can do" may mean "sometimes correct," not "always." Emergent capabilities can be fragile.
</Callout>

## The training pipeline: 4 steps to go from nothing to an assistant

An LLM isn't born intelligent. It goes through **4 successive steps** that progressively transform it from a neural network with random weights into an assistant capable of reasoning and following instructions. Each step builds on the previous one:

### Step 1 -- Pre-training (Base Training)

**Objective**: learn language and world knowledge.

The model ingests **trillions of tokens** of text (web, books, code, articles) and learns to predict the next token. This is the most expensive phase: thousands of GPUs for months, at a cost of tens of millions of dollars.

**Result**: a "base" model that can complete text coherently. It knows facts, speaks multiple languages, can write code. But it doesn't yet know how to *answer questions* -- if you ask "What is the capital of France?", it might complete with "What is the capital of Germany?" rather than answering "Paris."

-> Detailed in the GPT section (section 41) and scaling laws

### Step 2 -- SFT (Supervised Fine-Tuning)

**Objective**: learn to follow instructions.

The base model is trained on **thousands of examples** of conversations (question -> quality answer), written by humans or generated by other LLMs. The model learns the *format*: when asked a question, you should answer (not keep asking questions).

**Result**: a model that knows how to respond to instructions. It's already usable, but it can still produce dangerous, misleading, or inconsistent quality responses.

-> Detailed in the Instruction Tuning section (section 109)

### Step 3 -- RL Alignment (RLHF, DPO, GRPO)

**Objective**: learn human preferences -- be helpful, honest, and safe.

The model is refined so that it **prefers** good answers over bad ones. Several algorithms exist for this step:
- **RLHF/PPO** (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback / Proximal Policy Optimization): a reward model scores answers, PPO optimizes (section 52)
- **DPO** (Direct Preference Optimization): direct training on preference pairs, without RL (section 53)
- **GRPO** (Group Relative Policy Optimization): compares a group of answers against each other, without a critic model (section 111)

**Result**: an aligned assistant -- the model refuses dangerous requests, gives nuanced answers, and is generally more pleasant to use.

<Callout type="info" title={"Visualizing the pipeline"}>
Imagine fitting a function to a data distribution (red vs blue points):

1. **Untrained** -- the decision boundary is random (a simple straight line)
2. **Pre-training** -- the boundary starts to follow the data (approximate curve)
3. **SFT** -- the boundary refines (complex curve that separates better)
4. **RL (DPO/GRPO)** -- the boundary is optimized to perfectly separate good answers from bad ones

Each step makes the decision more precise and more aligned with what we expect from the model.
</Callout>

### Step 4 (optional) -- Specialization

Depending on the final use, you can add a specialization step:
- **RLVR** for mathematical/code reasoning (section 175) -- this is what produces models like DeepSeek-R1
- **Domain fine-tuning** to adapt the model to a specific sector (medical, legal, finance)
- **Distillation** to compress a large model into a smaller one that retains its capabilities

The complete pipeline typically costs **$10M-$100M+** for a frontier model. But thanks to fine-tuning (LoRA, QLoRA), you can adapt an existing model for a fraction of the cost.

## How an LLM generates text (the concrete mechanics)

Step by step, when you ask a question to an LLM:

### 1. Tokenization

> **Input**: "Explain gravity"
> **Tokens**: ["Expl", "ain", "grav", "ity"]

The tokenizer breaks text into manageable pieces (not whole words, sub-units).

### 2. Embedding

> Token "Expl" -> numerical vector $[0.2, -0.5, 0.8, \ldots]$ (2048 dimensions)
> Token "ain" -> numerical vector $[0.1, 0.3, -0.2, \ldots]$

Each token becomes a dense vector in a continuous space.



### 3. Passing through the Transformer

- **Layer 1**: self-attention + MLP
- **Layer 2**: self-attention + MLP
- ...
- **Layer 32**: self-attention + MLP (for GPT-3.5)

**Output**: updated vector for each token.

At each layer, each token "talks" to other tokens via attention. The network refines the representations.

### 4. Predicting the next token

Last token's vector -> linear layer -> 50,000 scores (vocabulary size)

$$
\text{softmax}() \rightarrow \text{probabilities that sum to 1}
$$

**Result**: $P(\text{"the"}) = 0.6$, $P(\text{"in"}) = 0.2$, $P(\text{"between"}) = 0.1$, ...

### 5. Sampling
You **draw a token** according to this probability distribution.

| Temperature | Behavior |
|---|---|
| 0 | Always the most probable token ("the") -- reproducible answers |
| 1 | Draw according to exact probabilities |
| 2 | Less deterministic, more creativity |

**Temperature = 0** -> reproducible answers. **Temperature >= 1** -> varied and creative answers.

### 6. Auto-regression (the loop)

**Initial prompt**: "Explain gravity"

| Iteration | Input | Output | New prompt |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | "Explain gravity" | "It's" | "Explain gravity It's" |
| 2 | "Explain gravity It's" | "a" | "Explain gravity It's a" |
| 3 | "Explain gravity It's a" | "force" | "Explain gravity It's a force" |

Continues until `</s>` or `max_tokens`.

Each generated token becomes the input for the next prediction. It's **auto-regressive**: the model feeds on its own output to keep generating.

<Callout type="tip" title="Prediction != understanding">
Technically, an LLM doesn't "understand" anything. It:
1. Transforms vectors via weight matrices
2. Produces a probability distribution
3. Draws a token

Is that understanding? Philosophically open. Practically: it works.
</Callout>

## Hard limitations

- **Hallucinations**: the model generates false facts with apparent confidence, because it optimizes for statistical plausibility, not factual truth
- **Context window**: limit on tokens it can process at once (improving, but remains a constraint)
- **No persistent memory**: each conversation starts from scratch (unless an external memory system is added)
- **Fragile reasoning**: can fail on minor variations of the same problem
- **Computational cost**: enormous training, and inference also consumes significant resources

## Why LLMs win

- **Architecture**: the Transformer scales well
- **Data**: text is abundant on the web
- **Compute**: massive GPUs are available
- **Flexibility**: one model for 100 tasks (via prompting)

## Why this matters

An LLM = a network trained on **a trivial task** (predict the next token) at **an insane scale** (billions of parameters, trillions of tokens).

This simple combo produces a tool of **remarkable flexibility** that can:
- Answer questions
- Generate code
- Translate
- Summarize
- Debate
- And much more (almost anything that can be framed as "generate text")

The three scaling axes (parameters, data, compute) directly govern progress. No magic breakthrough. Just: bigger = smarter.

Emergent capabilities (in-context learning, chain-of-thought, code) reveal that the patterns of human language contain much more than we thought -- logic, reasoning, world knowledge -- all implicitly encoded in text statistics.
