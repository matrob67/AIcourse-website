## Introduction: Learning by Trial, Error, and Reward

Your child wants to learn to walk. Nobody programs their muscles. They try, fall (ouch, negative), get back up, succeed in taking a step (yes, positive!). Little by little, they learn the sequence of muscles they need to contract.

This is **Reinforcement Learning** (RL). An agent interacts with an environment. It tries an action. The environment says "well done, here's a reward" or "missed, penalty." After thousands of attempts, the agent finds the strategy that maximizes cumulative reward.

<Callout type="info" title={"It's not supervised, it's real feedback"}>
Supervised: "Here's the right answer." RL: "Here's a reward, figure out on your own where it came from."
</Callout>

## The Actors of RL

### Agent

The entity that learns. It's the "brain" being trained. Can be a robot, an AI playing chess, a chatbot.

### Environment

Everything the agent interacts with. Reacts to actions. Determines rewards.

### The Interaction Loop

1. Agent observes the state (position, situation)
2. Agent chooses an action
3. Environment changes and produces a reward
4. Back to step 1

Think of AlphaGo: agent = the AI, environment = the Go board. AlphaGo plays a move. The environment validates whether it's legal and produces a reward (better position? eventual victory?)

## Key Concepts

### State

The current description of the situation.

Examples:
- Chess: position of all pieces
- Walking robot: positions and velocities of its joints
- Video game: screen pixels, or player position + enemies + items

### Action

What the agent can do.

**Discrete**: go left, right, jump, shoot (video games)

**Continuous**: apply a continuous force on a motor (robotics)

### Reward

Immediate feedback from the environment, a number.

Examples:
- Chess: +1 for a win, -1 for a loss, 0 otherwise
- Walking robot: +1 for each meter advanced, -100 for a fall
- Pac-Man: +10 for eating a pellet, -10 for getting caught

<Callout type="warning" title="Designing the Reward is an Art">
A bad reward = weird agent. Classic example: agent tasked with "picking up objects." It learns to spin in circles next to the respawn point instead of exploring (reward hacking). It maximizes the reward, but not what you actually wanted.
</Callout>

### Policy

The agent's strategy. A function that says "in this state, take this action."

**Deterministic**: same state = same action always.

**Stochastic**: same state = probability distribution over different actions.

The goal of RL: find the **optimal policy** that maximizes the long-term reward.

### Value Function

Answers: "How much is this state worth in the long run?"

$$
V(s) = r + \gamma \cdot V(s')
$$

The **discount factor** ($\gamma$, between 0 and 1) controls the importance of the future.
- $\gamma$ close to 0: the agent is short-sighted, focused on immediate reward
- $\gamma$ close to 1: the agent plans for the long term

### Q-Function

A variant of the value function. Answers: "If I take this action in this state, what reward do I expect?"

$$
Q(s, a) = r + \gamma \cdot \max_{a'} Q(s', a')
$$

If you know the optimal Q, the optimal policy is simple: in each state, take the action with the highest Q value.

## Exploration vs Exploitation: The Fundamental Dilemma

You know a good restaurant (exploitation). But maybe there's a better one nearby (exploration). If you only exploit, you never discover the best one. If you only explore, you never enjoy your discoveries.

This is the central trade-off of RL.

**Exploitation**: do what you know to be good

**Exploration**: try new things to potentially discover something better

Strategies:

**Epsilon-greedy**: with probability $\epsilon$ (say 10%), choose a random action (exploration). Otherwise, choose the best known action (exploitation). You reduce $\epsilon$ over time.

**Softmax**: choose actions proportionally to their estimated Q value. Good actions = more likely, but others also have a chance.

**UCB (Upper Confidence Bound)**: balances between value and uncertainty. Favors poorly known actions.

## Q-Learning: The Foundational Algorithm

One of the most famous RL algorithms. Learns the optimal Q function without a model of the environment.

How it works:

1. Observe current state s
2. Choose action a (epsilon-greedy for example)
3. Environment gives reward r and new state s'
4. Update Q:

$$
Q(s, a) \leftarrow Q(s, a) + \alpha \left[ r + \gamma \cdot \max_{a'} Q(s', a') - Q(s, a) \right]
$$

Where $\alpha$ is the learning rate.

The update pushes Q toward the target value "immediate reward + best future value."

### Deep Q-Network (DQN): When State Spaces are Huge

When the state space is infinite (the pixels of an Atari game = billions of states), a Q table doesn't work.

Solution: replace the Q table with a **neural network** that approximates Q(s, a).

DQN allowed an AI to learn Atari games at superhuman level. Two key innovations:

**Experience Replay**: stores transitions (s, a, r, s') in a buffer. Randomly re-samples for training. This breaks temporal correlations.

**Target Network**: uses a frozen copy of the network to compute the target. Stabilizes training.

## Policy Gradient: Optimizing the Policy Directly

Instead of learning Q and deriving the policy from it, optimize **the policy directly**.

Policy gradient theorem:

$$
\nabla J(\theta) = \mathbb{E} \left[ \nabla \log \pi_\theta(a|s) \cdot Q(s, a) \right]
$$

In plain English: increase the probability of actions with high Q value.

Variations:

**REINFORCE**: the simplest, very basic.

**Actor-Critic**: uses two networks. The actor = the policy. The critic = the value function. One learns to act better, the other learns to evaluate.

**PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization)**: the most popular variant currently. Stable, robust. Used for RLHF (aligning ChatGPT).

### Robotics

RL makes it possible to train robots to walk, grasp objects, and navigate their environment. **Sim-to-real transfer** is a key technique: you train the agent in simulation (fast and risk-free), then transfer the learned policy to a real robot.

### RLHF: Aligning LLMs with Humans

RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) is one of the most impactful applications of RL. ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini all use it.

<ArxivLink id="2203.02155" title="Training language models to follow instructions with human feedback (InstructGPT)" />

Process:

1. **Supervised pre-training**: LLM trained classically on text (already useful)

2. **Reward model**: humans compare pairs of responses, indicate which is better. Train a model to predict these preferences.

3. **PPO optimization**: use PPO to optimize the LLM. Maximize the reward model score, while staying close to the original model (avoids "reward hacking").

Result: ChatGPT capable of following instructions, being helpful, avoiding harmful content.

<Callout type="info" title="DPO: A Modern Alternative to RLHF">
DPO (Direct Preference Optimization) simplifies the process. No need for an explicit reward model or PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization). Just a direct classification problem on preference pairs. Simpler, often just as good.
</Callout>

## The Challenges of RL

**Sample efficiency**: RL often requires millions of interactions. In simulation, it's fast. With a real robot? Very expensive.

**Stability**: RL training is notoriously unstable. A slight hyperparameter change = collapse.

**Reward design**: designing the right reward is very difficult. It's easy to get fooled by an agent that finds loopholes.

**Exploration vs exploitation**: finding the right balance takes experience.

**Generalization**: an agent trained on one environment can fail if the environment changes slightly.

<Callout type="warning" title={"The Alignment Problem with LLMs"}>
If you optimize an LLM to please an imperfect reward model, it can "hack" it. It generates text that a human wouldn't actually like but that scores well on the reward. This is a central challenge of AI safety.
</Callout>

## Why This Matters

RL is how modern systems learn from real interaction. AlphaGo, ChatGPT, autonomous vehicles, robots: everywhere you need to learn from real-world feedback (not just static data), RL dominates. It's also the most difficult paradigm, but the most powerful.
