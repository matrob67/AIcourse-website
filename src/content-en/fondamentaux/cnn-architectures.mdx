## Why tell the history of CNNs?

Imagine you're asked to recognize handwritten digits. You'd simply see the shapes, the curves, effortlessly. For a computer, it's complicated. Traditional neural networks see giant lists of numbers and lose the notion that the image is "an image". Convolutional networks (CNNs) preserve the spatial structure. Understanding how CNNs improved since 1998 means understanding how we progressed from "it kind of works" to "it rivals humans".

### The pioneer that changed the game

Yann LeCun created LeNet-5 to recognize handwritten digits on bank checks. Simple? Yes. Revolutionary? Absolutely. It was the proof that convolutions (filters that slide over the image) capture visual patterns better than a standard network.

### How does it work?

LeNet-5 is tiny compared to today's standards: 32x32 pixels (black and white images), 2 layers looking at 5x5 patterns, then 3 layers that classify: **~60,000 parameters**. For 1998, that was a feat.

The key ideas:
- **Weight sharing**: the same filter is applied everywhere in the image, reducing parameters
- **Convolutions**: capture edges, textures, spatial patterns
- **Hierarchical learning**: low layers see simple strokes, high layers combine them

<Callout type="info" title={"Why CNNs didn't explode immediately"}>
LeNet worked! But computers were slow and data was scarce. GPUs didn't exist. Deep learning slept for 15 years. Until smartphones with built-in GPUs and the Internet explosion (data everywhere).
</Callout>

### 2012, it explodes

AlexNet crushed the ImageNet competition with an **error of 15.3%** instead of the 26% from previous methods. Suddenly, everyone said: "oh, deep learning is actually useful now?"

### Why AlexNet won

It was 1,000 times larger than LeNet: **224x224 color pixels, 5 convolutional layers, 60 million parameters**. But above all, three major innovations:

- **ReLU**: instead of slow sigmoid, we use max(0, x). Training becomes **10 times faster**
- **Dropout**: during training, 50% of neurons are randomly deactivated. This forces the network to distribute information redundantly, reducing overfitting
- **Data augmentation**: cropping, flipping, blurring images to virtually get many more

And: **2 GPUs** for parallelization. The computing revolution had begun.

<Callout type="tip" title={"The domino effect"}>
ImageNet 2012 changed the trajectory of AI. By 2015, deep learning was no longer fringe: it was the norm everywhere.
</Callout>

## VGGNet (2014): the simplicity of depth

<ImageWithSource
  src="/images/admin/img-1774010006099.png"
  alt="Detailed VGGNet architecture"
  source="https://viso.ai/deep-learning/vgg-very-deep-convolutional-networks/"
  caption="This image illustrates the VGGNet architecture, a deep convolutional neural network (CNN). It shows the different convolution layers (conv1 to conv5), pooling layers (max pooling), and fully connected layers (fc6 to fc8) with their respective dimensions."
  size="large"
/>

### A simple idea: stack deep

VGG said: "what if we took **tiny filters (3x3 only)** and stacked a lot of them?" Two 3x3 filters side by side have the same "field of view" as a 5x5, but more expressive capacity. VGG-16 and VGG-19 (16 and 19 layers) became stars.

### The secret: regularity

The architecture is **dead simple**:
- 3x3 filters, **nothing else** for convolution
- Blocks: 2-4 3x3 convolutions, then max pooling
- The number of filters doubles each block (64 → 128 → 256 → 512)
- ~138 million parameters for VGG-16

### The cost of simplicity

VGG is enormous. The last 3 dense layers: 100+ million parameters. It's a brick wall, not a feather. Despite this, VGG remained a standard for "extracting features" via transfer learning (reusing a model pre-trained on a large dataset like ImageNet and adapting it to a specific task, rather than training from scratch), because its representations in the low layers are universal.

## GoogLeNet / Inception (2014): doing multiple things in parallel

<ImageWithSource
  src="/images/admin/img-1774010048309.png"
  alt="GoogLeNet architecture with Inception layers"
  source="https://viso.ai/deep-learning/googlenet-explained-the-inception-model-that-won-imagenet/"
  caption="Diagram of the GoogLeNet architecture illustrating the Inception modules that combine multiple convolution and pooling operations in parallel to extract features at different levels."
  size="large"
/>

### The inverted idea

Google said: "instead of stacking deep, let's go **wide**: apply multiple convolutions **in parallel** on the same input."

An Inception block simultaneously performs:
- 1x1 convolution (inexpensive, reduces the number of channels)
- 3x3 convolution (captures medium-scale patterns)
- 5x5 convolution (captures large-scale patterns)
- 3x3 max pooling

All results are concatenated. It's like looking at the same scene with multiple camera lenses of different focal lengths at the same time, then combining what each one sees.

The result is striking: GoogLeNet rivals VGG but with only **5 million parameters** (27 times fewer). This isn't magic -- it's proof that a well-designed architecture can do more with much less. Where VGG brute-forced with 138 million parameters, Inception achieved the same performance through more efficient design.

## ResNet (2015): the connections that save

<ImageWithSource
  src="/images/admin/img-1774010099704.png"
  alt="Comparison of ResNet, plain ResNet, and VGG-19 architectures"
  source="https://sh-tsang.medium.com/review-resnet-winner-of-ilsvrc-2015-image-classification-localization-detection-e39402bfa5d8"
  caption="The image illustrates ResNet architectures (with residual connections), plain ResNet (without residual connections), and VGG-19, highlighting the impact of residual connections on the depth of convolutional neural networks."
  size="large"
/>

### The weird problem of 2015

Deeper = better? No! Beyond ~50 layers, adding layers **degraded** results, even during training. Not overfitting, just... optimization became impossible. Gradients were vanishing.

### The elegant solution: skip connections

Kaiming He had a simple idea: instead of asking a block of layers to directly compute the desired output $F(x)$, ask it to compute the **difference** from the input (the "residual"), then add:

$$
y = F(x) + x
$$

This tiny addition changes everything. The gradient can flow **directly** through the skip, without being diluted by the layers. Boom, 152 layers of depth become possible.

<ArxivLink id="1512.03385" title="Deep Residual Learning for Image Recognition" />

### ResNet variants

ResNet-18/34/50/101/152: the numbers = the depth. The deeper ones use "bottleneck blocks" (1x1 → 3x3 → 1x1: first reduce the number of channels with a 1x1 to cut computation cost, apply the expensive 3x3 convolution on this compressed version, then re-expand with another 1x1) to stay efficient.

<Callout type="paper" title="ResNet surpassed humans">
ResNet-152 in 2015: 3.57% error on ImageNet. Humans? ~5%. For the first time, a machine saw better than a person on this dataset. And skip connections became **the** foundation of almost all modern architectures, well beyond CNNs.
</Callout>

## Summary table: the visible evolution

| Architecture | Year | Depth | Parameters | Top-5 Error |
|--|--|--|--|--|
| LeNet-5 | 1998 | 7 | 60K | — |
| AlexNet | 2012 | 8 | 60M | 15.3% |
| VGG-16 | 2014 | 16 | 138M | 7.3% |
| GoogLeNet | 2014 | 22 | 5M | 6.7% |
| ResNet-152 | 2015 | 152 | 60M | 3.6% |

## The trend

This table tells a clear story: depth and size alone aren't enough. VGG is 28 times larger than GoogLeNet for slightly inferior performance. ResNet is deep but smart -- 152 layers become possible thanks to residual connections, not despite them. Architectural efficiency has become a criterion as important as raw accuracy.

After ResNet, the same logic continued: DenseNet extended residual connections to all preceding layers, EfficientNet systematically balanced width, depth, and resolution, and Vision Transformers finally abandoned convolutions in favor of attention. Each one solves the previous one's problem. All build on what came before.

<Callout type="tip" title={"Today"}>
In practice, you'll never train these models from scratch. You'll download them pre-trained on ImageNet and adapt them to your task in a few hours. That's **transfer learning** (reusing a pre-trained model and adapting it to your specific task, rather than training from scratch), and it has been the global standard since 2015.
</Callout>
