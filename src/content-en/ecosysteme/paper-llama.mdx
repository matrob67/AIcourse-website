## The Equalizer: David Beats Goliath

LLaMA 13B vs GPT-3 (175B). Same performance. Or better.

13 billion parameters against 175 billion. How can a model 13x smaller rival a giant?

The answer fits in one word: **data**. LLaMA directly applies the lessons of Chinchilla. Instead of stacking parameters and feeding them sparingly (like GPT-3: 175B parameters, 300 billion tokens), LLaMA does the opposite: a compact model, stuffed with data (13B parameters, **1 trillion tokens**).

GPT-3's token/parameter ratio? About 1.7. LLaMA's? About 77. That's 45x more data per parameter. And it changes everything.

<Callout type="info" title={"The impact: open-source becomes real"}>
Before LLaMA, the best LLMs were proprietary. OpenAI, Google, and Anthropic kept their models behind paid APIs. LLaMA made open-source viable for world-class models. Thousands of researchers, developers, and startups could experiment. Innovation accelerated overnight. The industry's structure changed.
</Callout>

## Public Data Only: Reproducibility First

A remarkable aspect of LLaMA: Meta only uses **publicly accessible data**:

- **Common Crawl** (67%): web pages filtered and cleaned with a quality classifier
- **C4** (15%): Common Crawl cleaned following Google's methodology
- **GitHub** (4.5%): open-source code
- **Wikipedia** (4.5%): in 20 languages
- **Books** (4.5%): Gutenberg corpus and Books3
- **ArXiv** (2.5%): scientific papers in LaTeX
- **StackExchange** (2%): technical Q&A

No proprietary data. No inaccessible secret corpora. This matters for **reproducibility**: in theory, anyone could reconstitute the training dataset (even if in practice, it's considerable work).

## Architectural Choices: Nothing New, but the Best of Everything

LLaMA didn't invent any new technique. Its strength lies in the **judicious combination** of existing best practices:

- **Pre-norm with RMSNorm**: normalize the input of each sub-layer (instead of the output). More stable for large-scale training.
- **SwiGLU** (activation function): replaces ReLU in feed-forward layers. Improves performance without significant overhead.
- **Rotary Positional Embeddings (RoPE)**: a relative positional encoding that allows generalization to sequence lengths never seen during training.
- **No bias** in linear layers: a simplification that doesn't affect performance.

This exact recipe has become **the industry standard**. Mistral, LLaMA 2/3, Gemma, Qwen, Yi: they all copy this architecture, sometimes identically. LLaMA defined the archetype of the modern LLM.

## Results: Data Beats Parameters

| Model | Parameters | Training tokens | Performance |
|--------|-----------|--------|-------------|
| LLaMA-7B | 7B | 1T | Outperforms GPT-3 (175B) on some benchmarks |
| LLaMA-13B | 13B | 1T | Outperforms GPT-3 overall |
| LLaMA-33B | 33B | 1.4T | Competitive with Chinchilla (70B) |
| LLaMA-65B | 65B | 1.4T | Rivals PaLM (540B) on several tasks |

The evidence is overwhelming: brute size doesn't reign. What matters is the **parameter/data ratio** and training quality. A well-sized and properly trained model outperforms a giant model that's poorly calibrated.

## The Leak and the Ecosystem Acceleration

LLaMA was initially reserved for academic researchers, with an access request form. But a few days after publication, the weights **leaked** onto the internet. Torrents, forums, GitHub: everyone had instant access.

Meta was "surprised." Or maybe not so upset. The leak massively accelerated adoption. And the resulting ecosystem gave Meta enormous influence over the direction of open-source AI.

## The Explosion of Derivatives: One Week Is Enough

The ecosystem of LLaMA-derived models exploded in a matter of weeks:

**Alpaca** (Stanford, March 2023): LLaMA-7B fine-tuned with 52,000 instructions generated by GPT-4. Total fine-tuning cost: **$600**. Performance close to GPT-3.5 for certain tasks. Revelation: you can create a competent conversational assistant for the price of a restaurant dinner.

**Vicuna**: fine-tuned on ShareGPT conversations (users were sharing their ChatGPT dialogues). 90% of ChatGPT's quality, as estimated by GPT-4.

**WizardLM**: uses Evol-Instruct, a technique that progressively increases instruction complexity. The model learns to handle increasingly sophisticated requests.

**Code Llama**: specialized for programming. Fine-tuned on source code, supports infilling (completing code in the middle of a file).

Dozens of other variants followed. LLaMA became the "Linux" of AI: a foundation on which everyone builds.

## The Evolution of the LLaMA Family

**LLaMA 2** (July 2023): going commercial. Permissive license (unless you have more than 700 million users). Sizes 7B/13B/70B. Chat versions fine-tuned with RLHF. 4,096-token context.

**LLaMA 3** (April 2024): the qualitative leap. 8B and 70B parameters. Trained on 15 trillion tokens (15x LLaMA 1). 8K token context. Performance close to GPT-4 for the 70B.

**LLaMA 3.1** (July 2024): the major extension. Addition of the 405B model (the largest open-weight model ever published). 128K token context. 8 languages. The 405B rivals GPT-4 and Claude 3.5 Sonnet.

**LLaMA 4** (2025): adoption of the Mixture of Experts (MoE) architecture. Scout (10M token context) and Maverick models. Routing efficiency joins open-source.

Each generation progresses significantly. LLaMA remains the undisputed king of open-source.

<Callout type="paper" title={"The industry's structural turning point"}>
LLaMA demonstrated that open-source is viable for cutting-edge LLMs. Before, AI was a proprietary monopoly. After, it's a thriving open ecosystem. The industrial structure of AI has changed irreversibly. Proprietary models must now justify their price against open-source alternatives of comparable quality.
</Callout>

<Callout type="warning" title="Open-weight, not open-source in the strict sense">
Let's be precise: LLaMA is **open-weight** (public weights), not **open-source** in the strict sense. The training data remains secret. The full training code isn't shared. Infrastructure and engineering details are absent. Reproducibility is therefore partial. Projects like **OLMo** (Allen AI) and **Pythia** (EleutherAI) go further by publishing data, code, and intermediate checkpoints.
</Callout>
