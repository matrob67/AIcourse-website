## Predicting Performance Before Training

LLM performance doesn't improve randomly. It follows **power laws**: regular and predictable mathematical relationships between invested resources and the quality of the resulting model.

Double the model size? You gain about 5% on loss. Triple? About 7%. It's predictable. Mathematical. Extrapolatable.

It's the paper by Kaplan et al. (OpenAI, January 2020) that formalized this observation. And it changed everything: AI went from an experimental discipline ("let's try and see") to a predictive engineering discipline ("here's how much to invest to reach a given performance").

<Callout type="info" title={"Why this is fundamental"}>
Scaling laws allow you to **predict a model's performance before training it**. You have a budget of X million dollars? You can calculate the optimal model size, the required amount of data, and the expected performance. This has become the basis for billion-dollar investment decisions in the AI industry.
</Callout>

## The Three Fundamental Relationships

Loss (the model's prediction error -- lower is better) follows power laws with respect to three factors:

1. **Number of parameters (N)**: $L \sim N^{-0.076}$
2. **Dataset size (D)**: $L \sim D^{-0.095}$
3. **Compute budget (C)**: $L \sim C^{-0.050}$

Concretely, what does this say?

- Multiply parameters by 10 -> loss decreases by a factor of $10^{0.076} \approx 1.19$ (about 19% improvement)
- Multiply data by 10 -> loss decreases by a factor of $10^{0.095} \approx 1.25$
- Multiply compute by 10 -> loss decreases by a factor of $10^{0.050} \approx 1.12$

The striking finding: these relationships are **valid across 6 orders of magnitude**. From a model with a few million parameters to one with hundreds of billions, the same curves apply.

<Callout type="tip" title="The magic of log-log">
On a log-log scale (both axes in logarithm), power laws become **straight lines**. That's why you can extrapolate: measure performance on small models (cheap), draw the line, extrapolate to a large model. Expected performance follows the line. It's like predicting a planet's position by knowing its orbit.
</Callout>

## Key Conclusions from Kaplan et al.

### 1. Parameters Matter More Than Data

This is the most striking (and most retrospectively controversial) conclusion. For a fixed compute budget, Kaplan et al. conclude that it's more efficient to **increase model size** than data quantity. The optimal ratio would be to increase parameters much faster than data.

This conclusion directly motivated the race to giant models: GPT-3 (175B), PaLM (540B), etc. "Bigger = better" became the industry mantra.

### 2. Architecture Matters Little

Performance depends primarily on the **total number of parameters**, not the specific architecture. Whether you use a wide and shallow model or a narrow and very deep one, if the parameter count is the same, performance is similar. This is surprising and simplifying: parameter count is the universal proxy for capacity.

### 3. Large Models Are More "Data-Efficient"

A larger model learns more efficiently from each token. It reaches the same performance with **less data** than a smaller model. This is an important property: it means scaling parameters isn't wasteful, it's an efficiency gain.

### 4. Performance Is Predictable Across Orders of Magnitude

The most practically useful discovery: by measuring performance on 10M, 100M, and 1B parameter models, you can predict with a few percent accuracy the performance of a 100B model. This is what allows planning billion-dollar investments without flying blind.

## Impact on the Industry

### Justification for Massive Scaling

This paper provided the **theoretical justification** for investing massively in ever-larger models. Before, increasing model size was a bet. After, it was a calculation. "If we spend X on compute, we'll get Y in performance." Investors understand calculations, not bets.

This paper directly influenced the development of GPT-3 (175B parameters) and the scaling race that followed.

### Optimal Resource Allocation

Scaling laws answer the practical question: "I have a $10 million GPU budget. What model size to train? How much data to use? How long to train?" Before, it was trial and error. After, it's engineering.

### Strategic Planning

AI labs use scaling laws to plan **future generations** of models. If GPT-4 requires X compute and reaches a given performance, GPT-5 with 10X compute will reach another performance level. This guides infrastructure purchase decisions, hiring, and fundraising.

<Callout type="paper" title="Kaplan vs Chinchilla: the major correction">
Kaplan's most important conclusion (prioritize model size over data) was **revised** by the Chinchilla paper (Hoffmann et al., DeepMind, 2022). Chinchilla shows that in reality, model and data must be scaled **proportionally** (ratio of about 20 tokens per parameter). The correction is major: GPT-3 (175B parameters, 300B tokens) was undertrained on data according to Chinchilla. The entire industry pivoted toward smaller but better-trained models (LLaMA, Mistral).
</Callout>

## Limits and Criticisms

Scaling laws aren't without flaws:

- **Compute-optimal only for training**: they don't consider inference cost. In production, a smaller overtrained model can be more economical than a large compute-optimal model.

- **Loss vs capabilities**: better loss doesn't always translate to better performance on practical tasks. Loss is a proxy, not a final objective. A model with slightly better loss may show no perceptible difference on benchmarks.

- **Emergent capabilities**: power laws don't predict the sudden appearance of qualitatively new capabilities. The transition from "can't code" to "can code" doesn't follow a smooth curve. These phase transitions are unpredictable.

- **Empirical observations, not physical laws**: the exponents (-0.076, -0.095, -0.050) are measured, not derived from fundamental theory. They could change with new architectures, new data, or beyond a certain scale. Nothing guarantees that the log-log line won't eventually flatten.

<Callout type="warning" title="Scaling laws aren't eternal">
Scaling laws are empirical observations, not fundamental physical laws. They could plateau (a "scaling wall"), change slope, or stop applying beyond a certain scale. The debate over the existence of such a wall is one of the most important in current AI. If the laws continue, AGI is a matter of compute. If they stop, new ideas are needed.
</Callout>
