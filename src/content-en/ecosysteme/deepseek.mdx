## The Surprise That Shook Silicon Valley

January 2025. DeepSeek releases **R1**. A reasoning model that rivals OpenAI's o1. Open-source. Announced training cost: **$5.5 million** (compared to hundreds of millions, even billions for o1).

The impact was immediate: stock prices plummeted. NVIDIA lost 17% in a single day. The assumptions of the entire industry were called into question. Do you really need billions in investment to create competitive AI?

The term "Sputnik moment" was used by Marc Andreessen, Sam Altman, and dozens of analysts. Like the Soviet satellite in 1957, DeepSeek shows that competition comes from where you least expect it.

<Callout type="info" title={"What is DeepSeek?"}>
Founded in 2023 by **Liang Wenfeng**, a quantitative hedge fund founder (High-Flyer Capital). Based in Hangzhou, China. The goal: create competitive LLMs without depending on American GPUs. The context: the US banned the export of H100s to China. DeepSeek has to innovate with H800s (a downgraded version, with less inter-GPU bandwidth).
</Callout>

## Innovation Under Constraint: When Necessity Is the Mother of Invention

American export restrictions ban H100s from China. DeepSeek has H800s (reduced interconnect, less bandwidth for parallelism). Instead of complaining, they **innovated to work around the limitations**.

### DeepSeek V2 (May 2024): Architectural Efficiency

The key innovation: **Multi-Head Latent Attention (MLA)**. It's an intelligent compression of the attention mechanism. Instead of storing full keys and values for each token (the infamous KV-cache that explodes in memory), MLA compresses these representations into a much smaller latent space.

- 236 billion total parameters, only **21 billion activated** (Mixture of Experts architecture)
- **10-30x cheaper** to use than the OpenAI API
- Competitive performance with the best models of the time

The low prices aren't a marketing argument. Users immediately see the difference on their bill.

### DeepSeek V3 (December 2024): The Shock

- **671 billion total parameters**, 37 billion activated
- Performance similar to GPT-4o and Claude 3.5 Sonnet
- Announced training cost: **$5.5 million**. 2,048 H800 GPUs for 2 months.
- **FP8 training**: training in 8-bit precision instead of 16-bit. Half the memory, faster training. An engineering feat.
- **Multi-token prediction**: predicting multiple tokens at once, not just one. Speeds up training.

<Callout type="tip" title={"$5.5 million vs billions: how is that possible?"}>
American giants pay for **brute force**: more GPUs, more memory, more time. DeepSeek innovates on **architecture and engineering**. KV-cache compression (MLA). Highly efficient MoE (5-6% activated/total ratio). Low-precision training (FP8). Each innovation saves compute. Accumulated, they're game-changing. It's proof that smart engineering can compensate for a lack of brute force.
</Callout>

## R1: Learning to Think Without a Teacher

DeepSeek R1 is the reasoning model. A direct competitor to o1. But the way it was trained is even more fascinating than its results.

**R1-Zero**: the foundational experiment. Training through **pure RL** (Reinforcement Learning). No prior supervised fine-tuning. No examples of "good reasoning" provided by humans. Just a simple reward: "is the final answer correct?"

The model explores. It tries approaches. Some work, others don't. It learns.

And spontaneously, behaviors emerge that nobody programmed:
- **Chain-of-thought**: the model starts thinking step by step before answering
- **Verification**: it double-checks its results, like a careful student
- **Exploring alternatives**: when an approach fails, it tries another one

Reasoning **emerged** from RL, without being explicitly taught. It's a profound discovery.

**R1 (refined version)**: an additional step. You filter the best reasoning traces produced by R1-Zero, use them as supervised learning data, then do RL again. Result: record performance, cleaner and more reliable reasoning.

**Distilled versions**: R1 is greedy with reasoning tokens (it "thinks" for a long time). DeepSeek distilled the knowledge into smaller models:
- R1-Distill-Qwen 32B, 14B, 7B, 1.5B
- R1-Distill-Llama 70B, 8B

The small versions retain much of the reasoning capabilities. A 7B model that reasons like a 600B+ model? That's the promise of distillation.

<Callout type="paper" title={"The emergence of reasoning: why this is a major discovery"}>
Before DeepSeek R1, it was thought that reasoning had to be **explicitly taught**. Constitutional AI supervises it through principles. RLHF guides it through human feedback. DeepSeek shows that **pure RL** with a simple reward (right/wrong) is enough. Reasoning emerges spontaneously. It's a profound discovery about the nature of learning and how cognitive capabilities can appear without explicit process supervision.
</Callout>

## The Shockwave

### In the Industry

Financial markets react violently. Questions pop up everywhere:

- Do we really need clusters of 100,000 GPUs?
- Are billion-dollar investments justified, or can you do just as well for 10x less?
- Is the American technological advantage as solid as we thought?

DeepSeek challenges the fundamental assumption that AI competitiveness requires superhuman investments.

**DeepSeek V4** is expected in 2026 with 1T parameters and native multimodal capabilities (2026 update).

### Open-Source Contributions That Accelerate Everyone

DeepSeek publishes its innovations, and they're adopted quickly:

- **Multi-Head Latent Attention**: other labs implement it to reduce memory costs
- **Auxiliary-loss-free load balancing**: a better technique for MoE architectures
- **FP8 training methodology**: recipes for stable low-precision training

These contributions benefit the entire ecosystem, including DeepSeek's competitors.

## The Questions That Remain Open

**Censorship.** Chinese models refuse to answer certain politically sensitive questions in China (Tiananmen, Tibet, Taiwan, etc.). This is a limitation for international users who expect an unrestricted model. The question is broader: every AI reflects the values of whoever builds it.

**Access to compute.** Chip export restrictions could tighten further. If DeepSeek can no longer obtain performant GPUs, its progress could slow down. But history suggests that constraints stimulate innovation.

**Cost verifiability.** The $5.5 million figure is hard to verify independently. Does it include failed experiments? Preliminary trials? The total cost of the research program? Skeptics are asking for more transparency.

**Geopolitics.** Using Chinese models raises questions about technological sovereignty and data security. For some applications (defense, government, sensitive data), it's an obstacle. For others (research, personal use), it's a non-issue.

<Callout type="warning" title={"Geopolitics has caught up with AI"}>
DeepSeek isn't just a startup. It's a piece in the US-China competition over AI. Chip export restrictions haven't stopped China. They've **motivated it to innovate**. DeepSeek's architectural efficiency is a direct consequence of sanctions. The debate over the effectiveness of these restrictions is just beginning. The irony: by limiting access to compute, the US may have accelerated Chinese algorithmic innovation.
</Callout>
