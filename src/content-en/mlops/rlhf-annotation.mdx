## ChatGPT's Secret: 350,000 Humans Who Voted

ChatGPT isn't just an LLM that was left to train on its own. It's an LLM that was **fine-tuned with human feedback**. Hundreds of thousands of response pairs where humans said "this one is better than that one."

This process is called **RLHF** (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback). Think of it as educating a child: you don't write a 10,000-page rule manual. You show examples, you say "this is good, that's not as good," and they learn to generalize. Except the child is a 175-billion-parameter model.

## How It Works: The Raw Data of Alignment

The idea is simple: humans evaluate the model's responses. Not just "good" or "bad." They **compare** two responses and say "this one is better."

### The Three Types of Data

**1. SFT Data (Supervised Fine-Tuning)**

The human writes the ideal response to a question:
- Question: "Explain how plants produce oxygen"
- Response: [the human writes a great, clear, accurate response]

This is the starting point. You give the model examples of "good responses" and it learns through imitation.

**2. Preference Data**

You show two model responses to the same question and the human chooses the better one:
- Question: "Write a job application email"
- Response A: [model response - somewhat generic]
- Response B: [model response - more personalized]
- Vote: "I prefer B"

This is more subtle than SFT. You don't have the "true" response. You just have a preference.

**3. Scoring Data**

The human assigns a score from 1 to 7:
- 7 = excellent response, honest, helpful, safe
- 4 = correct but somewhat generic
- 1 = dangerous or wrong

<Callout type="info" title={"The scale goes crazy: millions of annotated responses"}>
OpenAI collected hundreds of thousands of preference pairs to align their successive models. And then millions more to keep improving. That's **human workers over years**. The total cost? Probably tens of millions of dollars, split between annotation, annotator training, and quality control.
</Callout>



## The RLHF Annotation Pipeline

### Step 1: Generate Good Questions

You need questions that are **diverse** and **realistic**:
- Factual questions ("What is the capital of France?")
- Creative writing ("Write a poem about autumn")
- Reasoning ("How would you find the solution to this problem?")
- Code
- Sensitive topics (to test safety)

Easy questions aren't interesting. Very hard questions aren't either (the model gets them wrong). You need a balanced distribution: 20% easy, 50% medium, 30% hard.

### Step 2: Generate Multiple Responses

For each question, you ask the model to respond **multiple times** in different ways:
- Temperature=0.8 (random, diverse)
- Temperature=1.0 (even more random)
- With different seeds

This creates diversity. You have 4 different responses to the same question. Annotators will compare them.

### Step 3: Preference Annotation

Here comes the human. The annotator sees:

> **Question**: "Why are people afraid of the dark?"
>
> **Response A**: "It's an evolutionary fear. Human ancestors who were afraid of the dark survived better because they avoided nocturnal predators. We inherited this fear."
>
> **Response B**: "It's an irrational fear that people could treat with behavioral therapy."
>
> **Which one is better?**

The annotator has **detailed guidelines**:
- **Helpfulness**: does the response help the person?
- **Honesty**: is the response factually correct?
- **Harmlessness**: is the response safe and respectful?
- **Instruction following**: does the response follow what was asked?

In this case, Response A is better: it explains why (honesty and helpfulness). Response B just throws out a psychology term without really explaining.

<Callout type="paper" title="The dilemma: helpfulness vs safety">
Sometimes criteria conflict. A very helpful response can be dangerous (e.g., explaining how to do something illegal). The guidelines need to be **very precise** about how to resolve these tensions. Because the annotators' preferences become the **encoded values of the final model**.
</Callout>

### Step 4: Train a Reward Model

The hundreds of thousands of preferences are used to train a **reward model**: a separate model that learns to predict "which response a human would prefer." It's a quality classifier. Then, this reward model guides the main LLM via reinforcement learning (PPO). For more recent methods (DPO), you skip the reward model step and use the preferences directly to fine-tune the LLM.

## Annotation Tools

### Argilla

The best-suited open source tool for RLHF:
- Pairwise comparison interface (Response A vs Response B)
- Multi-criteria scoring
- Easy to integrate with HuggingFace Datasets
- Data goes directly to HuggingFace Hub

### Scale AI / Surge AI

For major labs (OpenAI, Meta):
- **Scale AI**: you provide the questions and responses, Scale manages the annotators, quality control, and sends back the annotated preferences
- **Surge AI**: specializes in NLP, slightly more expensive, but more qualified annotators

Cost: several million dollars for a large dataset.

### LMSYS FastChat / Chatbot Arena

LMSYS (Berkeley) created a community platform where users compare models:
- You ask a question
- You see the response from model A (anonymous)
- You see the response from model B (anonymous)
- You vote for the best one
- The votes become training data

It works surprisingly well. Users who vote aren't paid but are motivated by curiosity.

<Callout type="tip" title="DPO simplifies everything">
DPO (Direct Preference Optimization) is a new method that simplifies the pipeline. Before, you needed:
(1) Collect preferences
(2) Train a reward model on them
(3) Use the reward model with RL (PPO, very complex)

With DPO:
(1) Collect preferences
(2) Fine-tune the model directly on them

It's 10x simpler. No RL needed. No reward model needed. Small teams can now do alignment.
</Callout>

## The Challenges: Subjectivity Kills

### The Problem: People Don't Agree

With classic annotation (labeling whether an email is spam or not), two annotators agree 95% of the time. With preferences, they agree **60-75%**. Sometimes it's impossible to say one response is objectively better.

Question: "What are the best movies of the 90s?"
- Response A: "Forrest Gump, Pulp Fiction, etc." (popular)
- Response B: "Chungking Express, Underground, etc." (arthouse)

Which is better? It's subjective. Two annotators will prefer different things.

### Annotator Biases

Annotators bring their biases:
- Cultural (a French person and a Japanese person don't prefer the same things)
- Verbosity (tendency to prefer long responses)
- Expertise (an expert judges differently than a novice)
- Fatigue (after 8 hours, quality drops)

If all your annotators are French, the model will have French values encoded. That's not necessarily bad, but it is a bias.

### Annotator Safety

Annotating dangerous content (violence, porn, harassment) is **traumatizing**. Best practices include:
- Rotation: don't annotate tough content every day
- Psychological support
- Time limits: max 2 hours per day on sensitive content
- Mandatory breaks

<Callout type="warning" title={"The ethics question"}>
There's a serious ethical question: who annotates? Often precarious workers in developing countries, paid $5 an hour to look at traumatizing content. Investigative reports have revealed problematic working conditions. Responsible organizations must guarantee decent conditions: fair wages, psychological support, the right to refuse content that's too difficult.
</Callout>

## Alternatives: Annotation Without Humans

### Constitutional AI (Anthropic)

Instead of humans comparing, the **AI itself** evaluates its responses according to a list of principles:

> **Constitution:**
> 1. The response must be honest
> 2. The response must be helpful
> 3. The response must be safe
> ...

The model reads its own response and says "this response violates principle 2." Then it corrects itself. It's self-criticism guided by human principles.

Advantage: much less need for human annotators, and it scales infinitely.
Disadvantage: it works less well than pure human feedback (but the results are still remarkable).

### RLAIF: AI Feedback Instead of Human Feedback

Using a powerful LLM (GPT-5.4, Claude Sonnet 4.6) to annotate preferences.

- You show 2 responses to the judge LLM
- The LLM says "response B is better because..."
- You train your model on these "AI-annotated" preferences

It works better than expected. A good LLM's preferences align well with human preferences (especially on objective criteria). And it's 100x cheaper.

## Best Practices

- **Create very precise guidelines** with examples. Show how to score a difficult case.
- **Train annotators** beforehand. Calibration session: everyone annotates the same 50 examples, then you discuss the disagreements.
- **Measure inter-annotator agreement**. If nobody agrees, your guidelines are bad.
- **Double-annotate 10-20%** for quality control.
- **Iterate on the guidelines**. After 100 annotations, you'll probably find ambiguous cases. Clarify the guidelines.
- **Diversify annotators**. If they're all 25-35 year old Western men, you're missing other perspectives.
- **Protect annotators**. It's real work, sometimes psychologically taxing. Treat them well.

## Why It Matters

Human alignment is the **real difference** between GPT-3 (powerful but chaotic) and ChatGPT (powerful AND useful). It's the difference between a model researchers love and a model people actually use.

All useful AI models in production (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) go through RLHF or DPO. It's not optional. It's the foundation.
