## Why Benchmarks Are Necessary

How do you know if GPT-5.4 is "better" than Claude Sonnet 4.6? If you ask each to summarize an article, both responses will be different and potentially equally good. "Quality" is subjective, contextual, and multidimensional.

**Benchmarks** solve this problem by creating a standardized playing field: thousands of questions with reference answers, where each model is evaluated under the same conditions. It's the equivalent of national exams -- imperfect, but essential for objective comparison. They let labs measure progress, and let you choose the right model for your project.

But beware: understanding what each benchmark actually measures is just as important as reading the scores. A model that dominates MMLU isn't necessarily the best for your customer support chatbot.

<Callout type="info" title="The benchmark paradox">
An LLM can excel on every academic benchmark and be mediocre for your specific use case. Conversely, a modest model on the leaderboards can become excellent after targeted fine-tuning. Benchmarks measure general capabilities -- not performance on your particular problem. Always keep that in mind when reading a leaderboard.
</Callout>

## Knowledge and Reasoning Benchmarks

These benchmarks test what the model "knows" and its ability to reason about that knowledge. It's the oldest and most cited category in new model announcements.

### MMLU (Massive Multitask Language Understanding)

The **most cited benchmark** in model announcements, and for good reason: 14,042 multiple-choice questions covering 57 subjects -- from mathematics to philosophy, including law and medicine. It's the equivalent of an encyclopedic general knowledge exam.

Why is it so popular? Because it captures an intuitive notion of a model's "general intelligence." The MMLU score has become the number every lab proudly announces. GPT-4 reached ~86% in 2023, Claude 3.5 Sonnet ~88% in 2024. In 2026, the top models (GPT-5.4, Claude Sonnet 4.6, Gemini 3.1 Pro) all exceed 90%.

But MMLU has a major flaw: with only 4 choices per question, a model that just guesses gets 25%. And a model that simply memorized the right answers (contamination) scores high without actually understanding. That's why **MMLU-Pro** was created: 10 choices instead of 4 (random chance drops to 10%), problems that require multi-step reasoning, and scores that drop 15-20 points compared to classic MMLU. It's a much better discriminator today.

### ARC (AI2 Reasoning Challenge)

ARC tests scientific reasoning at the middle/high school level, with an interesting twist: the benchmark is split into **ARC-Easy** (direct reasoning, single logical step) and **ARC-Challenge** (multi-step reasoning with plausible distractors). The Challenge version is valuable because it separates models that recite facts from those that genuinely reason. A model can know Newton's laws and fail on a problem that requires combining them.

### HellaSwag

HellaSwag evaluates **common sense** and natural language understanding through sentence completion tasks. The model must choose the most plausible continuation of an everyday scenario. It's a test of implicit reasoning -- the kind of thing humans do without thinking.

But HellaSwag is now virtually **saturated**: all top models exceed 95%, making comparison useless. It's a classic example of a benchmark that fulfilled its mission and needs to be replaced by more demanding tests.

## Programming Benchmarks

Code benchmarks are particularly interesting because they allow **objective** evaluation: the code compiles/works, or it doesn't. No subjectivity. But they vary enormously in realism.

### HumanEval

Created by OpenAI, it contains 164 Python programming problems. The model receives a function signature with a docstring and must generate the complete implementation. Think of it as a timed programming exam.

The key metric is **pass@k**: the percentage of problems solved when the model gets $k$ attempts. It's clever, because it captures two different dimensions:

- **Pass@1** = the first answer is correct. It's the most demanding metric -- and the most relevant for production, where you want the right answer on the first try.
- **Pass@10** = at least one answer out of 10 is correct. Measures the model's raw ability, regardless of its consistency.

The gap between the two tells a story. A model with pass@1=70% and pass@10=95% knows how to solve the problem, but it's inconsistent -- it needs multiple attempts to find the right solution.

<Callout type="paper" title={"The pass@1 vs pass@10 gap tells a story"}>
A model with a large gap (low pass@1, high pass@10) will benefit enormously from techniques like best-of-n sampling or self-consistency. A model with a small gap is more reliable in one-shot mode -- often preferable in production where latency matters.
</Callout>

### MBPP (Mostly Basic Python Problems)

974 beginner-to-intermediate Python problems. Why is it useful despite being easy? Because it's 6x larger than HumanEval, which gives statistically more robust results. It's a good complement to verify that a model is solid on common tasks, not just on difficult exercises.

### SWE-bench

The most realistic code benchmark, by far. The model receives a **real GitHub issue** from an actual open source project (Django, Flask, scikit-learn) and must produce a working patch. It doesn't just test the ability to write code: you have to understand an existing codebase of thousands of lines, locate the bug, and generate a fix that passes the tests. It's incomparably closer to a developer's daily work than "implement this function" exercises.

## Mathematics Benchmarks

Math is the perfect playground for testing reasoning: each problem has a verifiable correct answer, and you need to chain logical steps to get there. That's why progress in math is often the clearest signal of improvement in a model's reasoning capabilities.

### GSM8K

8,500 elementary and middle school level problems, each requiring 2 to 8 reasoning steps. Typical example: "Alice has 3 apples, she gives half to Bob, then buys 5 apples. How many does she have?" (Answer: 3 - 1.5 + 5 = 6.5, but actually 3/2 = 1 remainder 1, so she keeps 2 apples and buys 5 = 7. See the trap?)

GSM8K is the standard for **basic arithmetic reasoning**. Frontier models solve it at 95%+, but smaller models (7B-13B) still show interesting weaknesses, especially on problems with 5+ steps.

### MATH

12,500 problems at the high school to undergraduate level, classified by difficulty from 1 to 5: algebra, geometry, number theory, combinatorics. It's a qualitative leap from GSM8K. At level 5, problems demand mathematical creativity and advanced techniques that even good undergraduate students don't always master.

### AIME / AMC

American math competitions -- the equivalent of the Olympiads. The **AMC** is the qualifying round (accessible), the **AIME** is the sequel (very hard). These benchmarks test **mathematical creativity**: knowing the formulas isn't enough, you have to find the trick. The best LLMs do well on the AMC but remain clearly below the best human students on the AIME, making it an excellent indicator of the current limits of LLM reasoning.

## Conversational Benchmarks

The previous benchmarks test isolated skills. But in practice, you use an LLM in conversation. These benchmarks evaluate overall interaction quality -- the ability to be helpful, relevant, and coherent throughout exchanges.

### MT-Bench

80 questions in 8 categories (writing, reasoning, math, code, extraction, STEM, humanities, roleplay). What makes MT-Bench unique: each question has a **follow-up** that tests multi-turn coherence. The model must remember the context and go deeper. For example, after explaining a concept, the follow-up might be "What if we added this constraint?" Models that shine in one-shot but lose the thread in multi-turn are immediately exposed. A powerful LLM serves as an automatic judge and scores from 1-10 (see the LLM-as-Judge chapter).

### LMSYS Chatbot Arena

The Chatbot Arena is probably **the most reliable evaluation** that exists today, and its design is elegantly simple. Anonymous users ask questions to two anonymous models side by side, then vote for the best one. No predefined questions, no imposed criteria -- just real users with real questions choosing the response they prefer.

The resulting **Elo ranking** (borrowed from chess) captures real human preferences at scale. With hundreds of thousands of votes, the results are statistically robust. It's often the first ranking consulted by professionals to compare models, and for good reason: it measures what truly matters -- end-user satisfaction.

<Callout type="tip" title="The Chatbot Arena Elo ranking, the reference">
The Elo system captures real user preferences, not performance on artificial lab questions. That's why it's considered the most reliable measure. Check it first when you're comparing models for a real project.
</Callout>

## Specialized Benchmarks

Certain capabilities aren't captured by generalist benchmarks. These specialized benchmarks target specific skills that matter a lot in practice.

- **IFEval** (Instruction Following Eval): evaluates the following of very precise instructions -- "write exactly 3 paragraphs," "don't use the word 'the'," "respond in JSON with these exact keys." Crucial for production, where output format is often as important as content.
- **TruthfulQA**: tests whether the model resists popular misconceptions. Questions are designed to trap: they address widespread but false beliefs (urban legends, pseudo-science). A good score means the model prefers truth over popularity.
- **BBH** (Big-Bench Hard): the 23 hardest tasks extracted from BIG-Bench, targeting advanced reasoning -- logical navigation, object tracking, causal reasoning. These tasks were so hard that pre-2023 models performed at chance level.
- **GPQA** (Graduate-Level Google-Proof Q&A): doctoral-level questions in physics, chemistry, and biology. The "Google-Proof" part is key: these questions can't be solved by searching the internet. Even domain experts only get ~65% correct, making it an ambitious ceiling for LLMs.

## The Fundamental Limitations of Benchmarks

Benchmarks are essential, but they're also deeply imperfect. If you don't understand their limitations, you risk making decisions based on statistical mirages.

### Training Data Contamination

This is the most insidious problem. LLMs are trained on billions of web pages. And benchmark questions and answers are published online. Result: a model can "memorize" the correct answers without understanding them. It's like a student who got the past exams with answer keys -- their score doesn't reflect their understanding.

The problem is nearly impossible to completely eliminate. Even with decontamination techniques (duplicate filtering), close reformulations of original questions can persist in training data. Some historical benchmarks like HellaSwag or early versions of ARC are considered largely contaminated, making their scores even less reliable.

### Saturation

When all top models exceed 95% on a benchmark, it no longer discriminates anything. That's the case for HellaSwag, ARC-Easy, and soon GSM8K. The benchmark fulfilled its historical mission (measuring progress that was then difficult), but it's become obsolete for comparing current models.

The race forward is perpetual: MMLU saturates, MMLU-Pro gets created. HumanEval saturates, SWE-bench gets created. That's healthy, but it means **2024 scores are not comparable to 2026 scores** -- the benchmarks themselves have changed. Be wary of naive historical comparisons.

### Goodhart's Law

"When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." Labs are incentivized to optimize benchmark scores because those are the numbers that make headlines. Some optimization techniques are legitimate (better reasoning, more data), but others are problematic: **fine-tuning on data similar to benchmark questions**, tuning hyperparameters specifically for the MCQ format, or even training on reformulations of original questions.

The result: a model can gain 5 points on MMLU without actually becoming smarter. It just learned to better answer MCQs. Performance in production on open, unstructured tasks can remain the same, or even regress.

### The Benchmark/Production Gap

This point is rarely discussed but fundamental: benchmarks test **isolated** skills (solve a math problem, complete a Python function), while real tasks are **composite** (understand an ambiguous request, find the relevant information, synthesize it in the right format, with the right tone). A model can excel on each skill individually and fail on their combination in real conditions.

<Callout type="warning" title="Never trust a single benchmark">
No single benchmark captures the full range of an LLM's capabilities. A model excellent at math may hallucinate on factual questions. A model strong at code may be mediocre at instruction following. Always evaluate on multiple benchmarks relevant to **your** use case, and supplement with your own evaluation on your own data.
</Callout>

## In Practice: How to Choose an LLM

Benchmarks are your starting point, not your destination. Here's a concrete approach to go from leaderboard to decision:

1. **Check general rankings**: start with the Chatbot Arena Elo and the Open LLM Leaderboard to identify 3-5 serious candidates. That eliminates 90% of options in 5 minutes.
2. **Identify relevant benchmarks for your case**: code? Look at HumanEval and SWE-bench. Math and reasoning? GSM8K and MATH. General knowledge? MMLU-Pro. Instruction following? IFEval. Don't look at benchmarks that don't match your usage.
3. **Create your own benchmark**: 50-200 representative examples of your real use case, with reference answers when possible. It's the most relevant test and the one nobody could optimize for in advance.
4. **Evaluate the quality/cost ratio**: a model that's 2x cheaper but 5% less performant is often the best business choice. The difference between top 1 and top 3 on benchmarks is rarely noticeable by end users.
5. **Test in real conditions**: A/B testing with real users to measure the impact on your business metrics. It's the only evaluation that truly matters.

Benchmarks let you narrow the search space from 50 models to 3. Your own benchmark lets you go from 3 to 1. And A/B testing confirms your choice was right.
