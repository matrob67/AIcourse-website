## NVIDIA's dominance: 80% of the global market

NVIDIA controls about 80% of the AI GPU market. This is no accident: for 15 years, they've built increasingly powerful GPUs, created a dominant software ecosystem (CUDA -- we cover it in detail in the dedicated chapter), and most importantly, anticipated the AI explosion before anyone else. Today, if you want to train a massive language model, you need NVIDIA GPUs. It's effectively a de facto monopoly.

Each new GPU generation doubles (or nearly doubles) compute power. It's reminiscent of Moore's Law from the 1990s, but even faster. Let's see how.



## NVIDIA A100 (2020): the GPU that launched 1,000 AI startups

The A100 is the GPU that made LLMs possible. On A100 clusters, OpenAI trained GPT-3 (175 billion parameters). Facebook trained LLaMA. Nearly every foundational model you know came from A100s. It's a bit like if every movie had been shot with the same camera.

### What it can do

- **54.2 billion transistors** (for comparison, a desktop CPU has about 5 billion)
- **6,912 CUDA cores** (the GPU's small processors)
- **80 gigabytes of memory** (enough to store 1,000 Word documents of 80 pages each)
- **2,000 gigabytes per second of memory bandwidth** (the speed at which data can be read)
- **312 TFLOPS** in FP16 (312 trillion operations per second -- this is the basic computation when multiplying two matrices)
- **400W** power consumption (like 4 refrigerators)
- **NVLink 3.0** for talking to other GPUs (600 GB/s)

<Callout type="info" title={"The A100's secret: TF32"}>
The A100 introduced TF32, a clever numerical format that gives you the speed of FP16 (fast) with the precision of FP32 (accurate). PyTorch uses it automatically. It's like having your cake and eating it too.
</Callout>

## NVIDIA H100 (2022): the generational leap

The H100 is a massive leap forward. OpenAI trained GPT-4, Meta trained LLaMA 2 and 3, and most major 2023-2024 models were born on H100 clusters. In 2025-2026, the B200 is gradually replacing it for new clusters, but the H100 remains ubiquitous and will still be in use for years to come.

### What it can do

- **80 billion transistors** (50% more than the A100)
- **16,896 CUDA cores** (2.4x more than the A100)
- **80 gigabytes of HBM3 memory** (same amount as the A100, but faster technology)
- **3,350 gigabytes per second bandwidth** (1.7x faster than the A100)
- **990 TFLOPS in FP16** (3x faster than the A100)
- **1,979 TFLOPS in FP8** (an even smaller format for even more speed)
- **700W** power consumption (1.75x more than the A100, but much more powerful)
- **NVLink 4.0** (900 GB/s, 1.5x faster)

### The key innovation: the Transformer Engine

The H100 includes a specialized chip just for **Transformers** (the architecture behind all modern LLMs). This chip:
- Automatically converts numbers between different formats (FP8 and FP16) layer by layer
- Chooses the best format for each layer based on the actual data
- You gain a 2x speed boost without losing precision. It's like compressing a JPEG image without seeing any difference.

<Callout type="paper" title={"The production reality: 40-60% efficiency"}>
Theoretically, the H100 can do 990 TFLOPS. In practice, during real training, you only use 40-60% of that capacity (this is the **MFU** -- Model FLOPS Utilization, the actual utilization rate of compute power). The 40-60% lost comes from GPU communications, waiting, non-matrix operations. Even 50% is considered "excellent" by researchers.
</Callout>

## NVIDIA B200 (2024): the next-gen colossus

The B200 is the most powerful GPU ever created. It's the GPU for training models with 100+ billion parameters. OpenAI and Anthropic are buying thousands of them right now.

### What it can do

- **208 billion transistors** (two B200 GPUs in a single package, connected by an internal highway at 10 TB/s)
- **18,432 CUDA cores**
- **192 gigabytes of HBM3e memory** (that's 2.4x more than the H100)
- **8,000 gigabytes per second bandwidth** (2.4x more than the H100)
- **2,250 TFLOPS in FP16** (2.3x more than the H100)
- **4,500 TFLOPS in FP8** (2.3x more than the H100)
- **9,000 TFLOPS in FP4** (an ultra-small format for inference)
- **1,000W** power consumption (the electricity needs of a small house)
- **NVLink 5.0** (1,800 GB/s, 2x faster than the H100)

### The major innovation: two GPUs in one

For the first time, NVIDIA used **two internal GPUs** (called "dies") connected by an internal super-highway. It's as if your computer were two computers talking to each other at the speed of an internal connection. Technically, it's a hack to work around the size limits of modern processors. In practice? It works incredibly well.

The B200 also supports **FP4** (4 bits). That's extremely small. A normal number (FP32) = 32 bits. FP4 = 4 bits. You gain 8x in speed, but you lose a bit of precision. For inference (when you use the model to answer queries), it's almost imperceptible. You ask a question, the model responds. It calculated in 4 bits instead of 32. You can't tell the difference.

## NVIDIA GB200: the superchip (2 GPUs + 1 CPU)

The GB200 isn't just a GPU. It's a **superchip**: 2 B200s + 1 ARM "Grace" CPU on a single module.

### What it contains

- **2 B200 GPUs**: 384 gigabytes of combined memory
- **1 Grace CPU**: 72 classic ARM cores (like your phone's processor, but much more powerful)
- **NVLink-C2C**: a CPU-GPU highway at 900 GB/s (7x faster than standard PCIe)
- **2,700W** total

### Why it matters

Traditionally, GPUs are separate from CPUs. For a GPU to do anything, the CPU has to request it. That's slow. Here, they're on the same chip. The CPU can prepare data and pass it to the GPU at 900 GB/s instead of 64 GB/s (PCIe). It's like having the chef and the delivery person in the same house instead of putting them in different cities.

### The GB200 NVL72: the mega-machine

NVIDIA sells a complete rack called the **GB200 NVL72**. Inside: 36 GB200 superchips. That's **72 B200 GPUs total**.

**The numbers get insane**:
- **13.5 terabytes** of memory
- **720 PFLOPS in FP8** (720 petaflops, or 720 million billion operations per second)
- It's one of the most powerful computing machines in the world

This rack is designed to train models with **1 trillion parameters** (yes, one trillion).

## Comparison table: how much faster is it?

| What you're computing | A100 | H100 | B200 | B200 (double) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| FP16 speed | 312 TFLOPS | 990 TFLOPS | 2,250 TFLOPS | 4,500 TFLOPS |
| FP8 speed | -- | 1,979 TFLOPS | 4,500 TFLOPS | 9,000 TFLOPS |
| Memory | 80 GB | 80 GB | 192 GB | 384 GB |
| Memory speed | 2 TB/s | 3.35 TB/s | 8 TB/s | 16 TB/s |
| GPU-GPU cable | 600 GB/s | 900 GB/s | 1,800 GB/s | 1,800 GB/s |
| Power | 400W | 700W | 1,000W | 2,700W |

**In summary**: each new generation is 2-3x faster. Every 2 years, you gain a generation. That means a GPU that's 4 years old is 5-10x weaker.

<Callout type="warning" title="Energy costs are becoming the real bottleneck">
Electricity is expensive. A B200 consumes 1,000W. A GB200 NVL72 consumes 120 kW (like 120 refrigerators). Over a year, that's millions of dollars in electricity alone. Liquid cooling is no longer optional: it's mandatory.
</Callout>

## Which one should you choose? A practical guide

- **I want small-scale inference** (like a 13-billion-parameter chatbot): a single H100 is enough, or even an A100
- **I want large-scale inference** (70+ billion parameters): 2-4 H100s, or 1 B200
- **I want to train a medium model** (10-70 billion): an H100 cluster with 8-16 GPUs
- **I want to train a huge model** (200+ billion): B200 or GB200 NVL72
- **I'm on a tight budget**: A100s are still available at lower prices and are sufficient for many tasks

## Why this matters

Choosing the right GPU means **choosing whether your project will cost 1 million or 10 million dollars**. A bad choice means:
- Waiting 10x longer to train (time is money)
- Consuming 5x more electricity
- Renting hardware you'll never use at 100%

The best teams in the world (OpenAI, Google, Meta) take this decision very seriously. They measure **MFU** and optimize every detail to use 50%+ of GPU capacity. Because at this scale, every percent of inefficiency = millions of dollars.
