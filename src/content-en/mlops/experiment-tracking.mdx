## The problem: how many configurations have you tested again?

A serious ML project easily means hundreds of experiments. Learning rate of 1e-3 or 3e-4? Architecture with 6 or 12 layers? Dataset v2 or v3? Batch size 32 or 128? Each combination is an experiment. And each experiment produces loss curves, evaluation metrics, model checkpoints.

Without a structured system, you get lost very quickly. "Which model had the best results? With which hyperparameters? On which data?" -- the honest answer is often "no idea, I overwrote the results last week."

**Experiment tracking** automatically records, for each training run:

- **Hyperparameters**: learning rate, batch size, warmup steps, architecture, etc.
- **Metrics**: loss, accuracy, perplexity, F1, and their evolution throughout training
- **Artifacts**: model checkpoints, visualizations, confusion matrices, logs
- **Environment**: library versions, code Git commit, hardware used
- **Data**: dataset version, basic statistics (size, distribution)

<Callout type="info" title="The Excel spreadsheet trap">
Many start with a Google Sheets spreadsheet to track experiments. With 10 runs, it's manageable. With 100, it's painful. With 1,000, it's unusable. Tracking tools offer parameter search, metric filtering, visual curve comparison, and automatic reproduction of a past experiment. It's a qualitative leap.
</Callout>

## MLflow: the open-source standard

**MLflow**, created by Databricks, is the most widely adopted tracking tool in the world. Its main advantage: it's open source, flexible, and free of vendor lock-in.

### MLflow's 4 components

- **Tracking**: the core product. Record params, metrics, and artifacts for each experiment. Web interface for exploring, comparing, filtering.
- **Models**: a standardized format for packaging a model with its dependencies. An MLflow model deploys on any compatible platform.
- **Model Registry**: model lifecycle management. A model goes from "staging" to "production" to "archived," with a complete history of transitions.
- **Projects**: reproducible ML code packaging (conda/docker environment + entry point).

### Concrete strengths

- **Open source**: deployable on your own infrastructure, zero license cost, your data stays with you
- **Agnostic**: works with PyTorch, TensorFlow, scikit-learn, HuggingFace, XGBoost, and everything else
- **Simple to integrate**: a few lines of code are enough to start tracking
- **De facto standard**: the widest adoption in industry, which makes hiring and documentation easier

### Honest limitations

- The web interface is functional but visually basic, especially compared to W&B
- Multi-user collaboration requires deploying a dedicated MLflow server (not just a local file)
- Advanced visualizations (curve comparisons, custom dashboards) are limited

<Callout type="tip" title="MLflow + HuggingFace: the frictionless combo">
HuggingFace Transformers has native MLflow integration. Add an MLflow callback to your Trainer and you're done: hyperparameters, per-epoch metrics, learning rate schedule -- everything is auto-tracked without writing a single line of custom logging. It's the path of least resistance to get started.
</Callout>

## Weights & Biases: when the interface makes the difference

**W&B** (Weights & Biases) is the SaaS platform that won over the ML community with the quality of its interface. It's genuinely beautiful and pleasant to use.

### Features that make the difference

- **Experiment Tracking**: interactive dashboards with zoom, filtering, side-by-side curve comparison. You see at a glance which configuration performs best.
- **Sweeps**: distributed hyperparameter search with built-in Bayesian optimization. Define the search space, W&B explores intelligently.
- **Artifacts**: dataset and model versioning with automatic lineage (which artifact produced which other)
- **Tables**: interactive tabular data exploration, perfect for analyzing model errors
- **Reports**: collaborative reports mixing charts, analyses, and comments. Ideal for sharing results with the team.
- **Prompts**: specialized tracking for LLM chains (prompts, responses, evaluations)

### Strengths

- Exceptional interface: intuitive, responsive, visually polished. This is the undisputed strong point.
- Native collaboration: project sharing, comments on runs, team reports
- Deep integrations: PyTorch, TensorFlow, HuggingFace, LangChain, Keras, and more
- Active community: widely used in academic research, many papers share their public W&B logs

### Limitations

- SaaS by default: your data (prompts, metrics, artifacts) is on W&B servers. The self-hosted version exists but is expensive.
- Paid for teams: free for personal use, but ~$50/month/user for team features
- Vendor lock-in: advanced features (Sweeps, Reports, Artifacts) are proprietary

<Callout type="paper" title="W&B has become a standard in ML research">
It's now common to see links to public W&B projects in research papers. The community can explore detailed results, compare runs, and reproduce experiments. It's a real plus for open science.
</Callout>

## Neptune: the balance between the two

**Neptune** positions itself between MLflow (open source, basic) and W&B (SaaS, premium). Not as pretty as W&B, but more complete than MLflow.

### What it does well

- **Experiment Tracking**: flexible structure with hierarchical namespaces. You organize your metadata however you want, with no imposed schema.
- **Model Registry**: complete model lifecycle management
- **Experiment comparison**: a particularly ergonomic side-by-side interface for comparing dozens of runs
- **Self-hosted**: deployable on-premise, important for companies with data constraints

### Strengths

- Ergonomic and detailed comparison interface
- Flexible metadata structure (no rigid schema to follow)
- Good value-for-money compared to W&B
- Self-hosted option to keep control of your data

### Limitations

- Smaller community than MLflow or W&B
- Fewer LLM-specific features
- Less visible in academic research

## Comparison: who to choose based on your context

| Criteria | MLflow | W&B | Neptune |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Open source** | Yes, fully | No | No |
| **Self-hosted** | Yes (free) | Yes (paid) | Yes (paid) |
| **Interface** | Functional | Excellent | Good |
| **Collaboration** | Basic | Excellent | Good |
| **Cost** | Free | $$$ | $$ |
| **LLM support** | Basic | W&B Prompts | Basic |

<Callout type="warning" title="Watch out for sensitive data in traces">
Experiment traces potentially contain sensitive information: prompts with user data, responses containing PII (Personally Identifiable Information), confidential business metrics. Before sending all of that to a cloud SaaS, check GDPR compliance. Self-hosted solutions (MLflow, Langfuse) offer total control over your data.
</Callout>

## Best practices for effective tracking

- **Log automatically**: use the native callbacks of frameworks (HuggingFace, PyTorch Lightning) rather than manual logging. Less code, fewer oversights.
- **Version the code**: record the Git commit hash with each experiment. Without it, you can't reproduce.
- **Tag intelligently**: use tags to categorize your runs (baseline, ablation, final, debug). It makes searching easier later.
- **Compare systematically**: define a primary metric and always compare to the baseline. Without a reference point, numbers mean nothing.
- **Document failures**: failed experiments are just as instructive as successes. "Learning rate 1e-2 diverges after 100 steps" is valuable information for the whole team.
- **Make it accessible**: tracking only has value if the entire team can access it. A tracker nobody checks is a useless tracker.

Experiment tracking isn't glamorous, but it's the foundation of rigorous ML work. It's what separates "I'm doing ML by trial and error" from "I know exactly what I've tried, what worked, and why."
