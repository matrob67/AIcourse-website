## The backstory: where does the data that ChatGPT uses come from?

GPT-3 was trained on 300 billion tokens (small pieces of words). Each token comes from somewhere. And the vast majority comes from the web.

OpenAI crawled billions of web pages: Wikipedia, Reddit, GitHub, blogs, news sites. All of it was scraped, cleaned, filtered, then fed into training. The result? The data that powers modern LLMs.

But how do you do this at scale? You can't ask 1,000 humans to copy-paste web pages. You need an automated, scalable process, capable of handling **petabytes** of raw data.

Welcome to **web scraping for ML**.

## Common Crawl: the largest public web dataset

### What is it?

**Common Crawl** is a **nonprofit organization** that has been systematically crawling the web since 2011. Roughly once a month, it captures a snapshot of several billion web pages.

It's like taking a photograph of the entire web -- a photograph that weighs about **300 terabytes** compressed.

### The data is public and free

Common Crawl makes all its data available online for free. You can download the snapshots, search through billions of pages, use what you find.

It's already used for:
- **C4** (Common Crawl-Colossal Cleaned): the dataset that Google uses
- **The Pile**: 825 GB of high-quality text
- **FineWeb**: 15 trillion tokens, the most recent and widely used dataset

### The formats

Common Crawl distributes its data in three formats:

**WARC**: the raw HTML of each page (complete, heavy)
**WAT**: the metadata (HTTP headers, timestamps, URLs)
**WET**: the extracted text (no HTML, just plain text)

For training an LLM, you mainly need the WET (just the text).



## The pipeline: from raw web to clean dataset

### Step 1: choose the snapshots

Common Crawl produces a new snapshot each month. You pick some. The most recent (for freshness), the oldest (for a baseline), all of them.

### Step 2: extract the text

You need to **parse the HTML** and extract just the textual content. Not the menus, not the ads, not the footers. Just what a human would read.

Tools:
- **BeautifulSoup**: classic Python library for parsing HTML
- **trafilatura**: specialized in extracting the "main content" of a page

trafilatura is generally better for this use case. It uses advanced heuristics and ML to identify the editorial content of a page and ignore all the noise (menus, ads, footers, sidebars).

### Step 3: filter by language

You want a French LLM? Keep the French text, discard the rest.

Tool: **fastText** (Meta's library) for language classification.

It sounds simple but at 300 TB of data, it takes time.

### Step 4: filter by quality (THE BIG ONE)

This is the most critical step of the entire pipeline. Among billions of web pages, **the majority is garbage**: spam, auto-generated content, near-empty pages, repetitive content.

You need heuristics and classifiers to say "this page is good, this one is bad."

**Simple heuristics**:
- Length: not too short (< 100 words), not too long (> 100,000 words)
- Alphabetic character ratio: lots of symbols = probably code or spam
- Stop word ratio: too few "the," "a," "is" = probably spam
- Duplicate lines: lots of duplication = boilerplate (menus, footers)
- Unique word ratio: low diversity = repetitive content

**More advanced classifiers**:
- **Perplexity filtering**: a language model measures how "normal" the text is. Wikipedia has low perplexity. A spam page has high perplexity.
- **Quality classifier**: train a small model to distinguish "high-quality content" (Wikipedia, books) vs "low-quality content" (spam, auto-generated)

<Callout type="paper" title="FineWeb: proof that quality matters more than quantity">
HuggingFace published FineWeb, a filtered Common Crawl dataset. They applied aggressive filtering: 90 billion original pages reduced to 15 billion after filtering (82% of content eliminated).

Result: models trained on FineWeb (15 billion filtered pages) perform significantly better than models trained on a larger raw dataset.

The lesson: 15 GB of clean data beats 100 GB of dirty data. Quality always trumps quantity.
</Callout>

### Step 5: deduplication

Common Crawl has A LOT of duplicate content. Mirror pages. Syndicated content (the same article on 100 different blogs). You need to clean up.

**Exact deduplication**: compute a SHA-256 hash of each page. Pages with the same hash are identical.

**Near-exact deduplication**: two pages are 99% identical (just one line different). That's more complex.

Tools: **MinHash + LSH** (Local Sensitive Hashing) to efficiently find near-identical documents at scale.

## Alternatives to Common Crawl: when to crawl yourself

### Public APIs

When they exist, prefer APIs:
- **Wikipedia**: complete free dump updated monthly
- **ArXiv**: all scientific articles in open access
- **GitHub**: API for source code (respect the licenses)
- **Reddit**: public archives
- **Stack Overflow**: public data dump

These APIs are cleaner than raw scraping. Less spam. Better structure.

### Direct scraping

For sources not covered by Common Crawl or APIs:

**requests + BeautifulSoup**: simple, OK for small volumes

**Scrapy**: robust framework for large-scale crawls

**Playwright/Selenium**: if pages need JavaScript (single-page apps)

### Trafilatura

**trafilatura** is the most accurate tool for extracting clean text from raw HTML. It combines:
- Heuristics to identify the main content block
- Automatic cleaning (removing boilerplate, ads, menus)
- Handling of tricky cases (tables, lists, multimedia content)

## Ethical and legal considerations

### Robots.txt

Websites publish a `robots.txt` file that tells bots which pages they can crawl and which are off-limits.

Respecting `robots.txt` is a widely recognized ethical best practice. Legally, its binding value depends on the jurisdiction, but violating it exposes you to lawsuits.

### Copyright: the battleground

This is the **real question** of web scraping for AI.

**The facts**:
- Several lawsuits in progress: New York Times vs OpenAI, Getty Images vs Stability AI
- The EU AI Act requires transparency about training data and allows creators to request exclusion
- In the US, "fair use" is sometimes applicable, sometimes not
- In Asia, it varies enormously

**The takeaway**: the legal landscape **is evolving very rapidly**. What was tolerated in 2020 may no longer be in 2025. Legal risk is increasing, especially in Europe.

<Callout type="warning" title="Consult a lawyer before large-scale scraping">
If you have commercial ambitions and plan to scrape billions of pages to train a model, **consult a lawyer**. The legal risk is real, especially in the EU. You can also partner with content creators for an explicit license.
</Callout>

### Personal data: GDPR

Scraping can collect personal data (names, emails, addresses). **GDPR** and other laws impose obligations:
- **Minimization**: only collect what is necessary
- **PII scrubbing**: remove emails, phone numbers, addresses
- **Right to be forgotten**: if someone asks, you must be able to delete their data from the dataset

### Rate limiting and politeness

- Respect the server: delays between requests
- Descriptive User-Agent: identify your crawler ("MyCrawler/1.0")
- No accidental DDoS (Distributed Denial of Service): if you send 1,000 requests/second, the server goes down

## Best practices

- **Document provenance**: for each piece of data, trace its URL, date, license
- **Version the dataset**: use DVC or LakeFS for data versioning
- **Reproducibility**: the pipeline must be runnable multiple times and produce the same result
- **Quality over quantity**: 10 GB of clean data is better than 1 TB of dubious content
- **Respect licenses**: check the terms of use for each source

## Why this matters

80% of the data an LLM sees comes from the web. The quality of web scraping determines the quality of the model.

- Bad scraping -> model with lots of hallucinations
- Good scraping + good filtering -> capable and factual model

The labs that do good scraping (aggressive filtering, deduplication, cleaning) produce the best models.

It's an invisible but fundamental element of modern AI.
