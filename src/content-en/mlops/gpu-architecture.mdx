## Why GPUs dominate deep learning

Before diving into the specs, let's understand **why** GPUs are such a big deal. Deep learning, at its core, is all about **matrix multiplications**. Millions of multiply-add operations, all independent of each other. And independent means parallelizable.

<Callout type="info" title={"The analogy to get it"}>
Imagine you need to grade a million exam papers. A CPU is a brilliant professor who grades 1,000 papers per hour: it takes 1,000 hours. A GPU is 10,000 less experienced graders who each grade 10 papers per hour: together, they finish in 100 hours. For deep learning, the number of hands beats individual talent.
</Callout>

A CPU has a few ultra-powerful, versatile cores (8-64). A GPU like the NVIDIA H100 packs **16,896 CUDA cores** and **528 Tensor Cores**. Each individual core is weaker than a CPU core, but 16,000 cores running in parallel on the same matrix changes everything. In concrete terms, an H100 reaches ~67 TFLOPS (tera floating-point operations per second) in standard compute, versus ~1-2 TFLOPS for a good CPU. That's a **50x** factor in raw brute force alone.

## Internal structure of a GPU

### Streaming Multiprocessors (SM): the building blocks

Think of a GPU as an office building. Each floor is a **Streaming Multiprocessor** (SM), a nearly self-contained unit with its own resources. The H100 has 132 of these "floors."

In each SM, you'll find:
- **128 CUDA cores**: the generalist workers, handling FP32 floating-point computation
- **4 Tensor Cores** (4th generation): the matrix specialists, more on these in a moment
- **64 INT32 cores**: for integer operations
- **Shared memory** and **L1 cache**: the local desk, ultra-fast, accessible by all threads in the SM

The GPU distributes tasks across its 132 SMs, and each one works on its chunk in parallel. This architecture is what makes scaling so natural.

### CUDA Cores: the generalists

**CUDA cores** do the basic work: one floating-point operation per clock cycle (an addition, a multiplication). In deep learning, they handle everything that isn't a large matrix multiplication:

- **Element-wise** operations: activation functions (ReLU, GELU), normalization, dropout
- **Reductions**: sums, averages, softmax

They're versatile, but for the heart of deep learning (matrix multiplications, ~95% of a Transformer's compute), you need specialists.

### Tensor Cores: the real engine of deep learning

This is where it gets interesting. A CUDA core multiplies **two numbers** per cycle. A **Tensor Core** multiplies **two entire matrices** (typically 4x4 or 8x8) in a single cycle. Specifically, it computes **D = A x B + C** where A, B, C, D are matrices, not scalars.

The performance difference is dramatic on the H100:
- CUDA cores in FP32: ~67 TFLOPS
- Tensor Cores in FP16: ~990 TFLOPS
- Tensor Cores in FP8: ~1,979 TFLOPS

Tensor Cores are **15 to 30 times faster** for matrix multiplications. In practice, this means that when you train a Transformer, the vast majority of computation goes through these Tensor Cores. If you're not using a framework that leverages them (PyTorch with `torch.float16` or `torch.bfloat16`), you're leaving 95% of the power on the table.

<Callout type="paper" title="The reduced precision revolution">
The H100 supports a full range of numerical formats: FP64, TF32, BF16, FP16, FP8, INT8. **FP8** is the most radical: it doubles throughput compared to FP16, with virtually no precision loss thanks to dynamic scaling. In practice, this means you can train or serve a model **twice as fast** without sacrificing quality. This is what makes ever-larger models economically viable.
</Callout>

## HBM memory: the critical bottleneck

### Why memory matters more than compute

It might seem counterintuitive, but in deep learning, it's often **memory** that limits you, not compute power. Why? Because all your parameters, activations, gradients, and optimizer states must fit **simultaneously** in GPU memory.

Concrete example: a 70-billion-parameter model in FP16 -- its weights alone weigh **140 GB**. The H100 has 80 GB of memory. It doesn't fit. So you have to spread the model across multiple GPUs, which complicates everything (inter-GPU communication, synchronization, etc.).

### High Bandwidth Memory (HBM): stacked memory

**HBM** (High Bandwidth Memory) is the technology that makes all of this possible. Unlike traditional RAM (GDDR, soldered around the processor), HBM is **stacked vertically** in layers, right next to the GPU. Imagine pancakes of memory stacked on top of each other: the data path is much shorter, so throughput explodes.

The evolution is spectacular:
- **HBM2** (A100): 80 GB, 2 TB/s bandwidth
- **HBM3** (H100): 80 GB, 3.35 TB/s bandwidth
- **HBM3e** (B200): 192 GB, 8 TB/s bandwidth

Bandwidth **doubles with each generation**. And it's bandwidth, not TFLOPS, that determines your inference speed.

### What this concretely means for LLM inference

When an LLM generates text token by token, the GPU must re-read nearly all the weights for each token. Let's do the math: 140 GB of weights, 3.35 TB/s bandwidth on H100 = **~24 tokens/second** theoretical maximum. No matter if your GPU has 1,000 or 10,000 TFLOPS, it's the memory read speed that sets the ceiling.

That's why two techniques are absolutely critical in production:
- **Quantization**: reducing weights to INT8 or INT4 to divide their size by 2 or 4 (and therefore multiply throughput by the same factor)
- **Batching**: processing multiple requests simultaneously to amortize the cost of reading weights across multiple users

## The memory hierarchy: from fastest to slowest

A GPU has multiple levels of memory, like drawers increasingly far from your desk. The closer it is, the faster it is, but the smaller it is:

1. **Registers** (~20 TB/s): each thread's private memory, tiny but instant
2. **Shared memory / L1** (~19 TB/s): shared within an SM, a few dozen KB
3. **L2 cache** (~12 TB/s): shared across all SMs, a few MB
4. **HBM** (~3.35 TB/s on H100): main memory, 80 GB
5. **CPU RAM via PCIe/NVLink** (~64-900 GB/s): CPU-GPU transfers, the slowest

Between top and bottom, there's a **100x** factor. A well-written algorithm keeps its data in the fast levels instead of fetching from HBM every operation.

<Callout type="tip" title={"FlashAttention: the optimization that exploits memory hierarchy"}>
FlashAttention reorganizes the attention computation to keep data in the SM's **shared memory** (SRAM), instead of making round trips to HBM. Result: **2-4x faster** and much less memory-hungry. If you're using Transformers in 2024+, FlashAttention is enabled by default in most frameworks. But understanding *why* it works (the memory hierarchy) will help you spot other optimization opportunities.
</Callout>

## SIMT: the GPU execution model

One last important architectural concept. GPUs operate in **SIMT** (Single Instruction, Multiple Threads) mode: threads are grouped into **warps** of 32 that all execute the same instruction at the same time, but on different data. It's like a choir where everyone sings the same note, but each person holds a different sheet of music.

The trap: if your code has an `if/else` and some threads in the warp take the `if` while others take the `else`, the GPU executes both branches **sequentially**. The inactive threads wait. This is **warp divergence**, and it can cut your performance in half.

That's why deep learning operations are designed to be uniform: the same operation applies to every element of a tensor, without branching. This regularity is exactly what GPUs love.

## Metrics for choosing a GPU

When comparing GPUs for ML, here are the metrics to look at, in priority order:

- **Tensor Core TFLOPS**: the raw power for matrix multiplications
- **HBM capacity**: does your model fit in memory?
- **HBM bandwidth**: your actual LLM inference speed
- **Interconnect (NVLink)**: multi-GPU communication speed, critical for distributed training
- **TDP** (Thermal Design Power): power consumption, which determines your datacenter cost

The key concept here is **arithmetic intensity**: the ratio of compute performed to data read from memory. If your workload is **memory-bound** (like LLM inference), HBM bandwidth is your limit. If it's **compute-bound** (like training with large batches), it's the TFLOPS. For LLM inference, you're almost always memory-bound. Keep that in mind when choosing your hardware.
