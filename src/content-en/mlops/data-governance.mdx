## Why this directly concerns you

Are you training a model with data containing personal information? Do you have users residing in Europe? The **GDPR** (General Data Protection Regulation) applies to you. Are you deploying an AI system for recruiting, credit scoring, or healthcare? The **EU AI Act** applies too.

These aren't theoretical regulations. Non-compliance can result in fines of up to 20 million euros (GDPR) or 35 million euros (AI Act), not counting the reputational damage that can sink a startup. These penalties are not theoretical: numerous companies have already paid millions.

<Callout type="info" title={"Governance is also a competitive advantage"}>
Enterprise clients increasingly require certifications before signing: "Do you have GDPR compliance? ISO 27001? SOC2?" It's become a non-negotiable prerequisite for landing large contracts. Data governance isn't just a legal obligation, it's a business differentiator. Companies that integrate it early win client trust faster.
</Callout>

## GDPR: the fundamentals

The **GDPR** (General Data Protection Regulation) is the European framework on personal data, in effect since 2018. It applies to any organization processing data of European residents, even if the organization is based outside Europe.

### The six guiding principles

- **Lawfulness**: you must have a legal basis for processing data. Explicit consent, legitimate interest, contractual obligation, etc. "We had the data so we used it" is not a legal basis.
- **Purpose limitation**: data collected for one purpose (e.g., customer service) cannot be reused for another (e.g., training a model) without an additional legal basis
- **Minimization**: only collect what is strictly necessary. If you don't need the date of birth, don't collect it.
- **Accuracy**: data must be correct and up to date
- **Storage limitation**: no indefinite storage. Define a retention period and delete afterward.
- **Security**: protect data against unauthorized access, leaks, and loss

### Individual rights you must respect

- **Right of access**: any person can ask "what data do you have about me?" and you must respond within 30 days
- **Right to rectification**: "this data is incorrect, fix it"
- **Right to erasure** (right to be forgotten): "delete all my data." And you must do it, with limited legal exceptions.
- **Right to portability**: "give me my data in a structured, machine-readable format"

### The machine unlearning conundrum

The right to be forgotten poses a major technical challenge in ML: if someone requests deletion of their data, how do you make the model that already ingested that data "forget" it? The field of "machine unlearning" exists, but no solution is truly satisfactory today. Retraining the model without the data in question is theoretically correct but often impractical (cost and time).

**The best strategy is prevention**: anonymize or pseudonymize personal data before training, or better yet, don't include it in your training datasets at all.

<Callout type="warning" title={"The right to be forgotten is real and applies to your models"}>
This isn't a theoretical concept. Deletion requests do come in, and you must be able to respond. Prevent the problem upstream: scan your datasets to detect **PII** (Personally Identifiable Information) and remove it before any training.
</Callout>

## EU AI Act: the first global legal framework for AI

The **AI Act** (adopted in 2024, being rolled out progressively between 2025 and 2027) is pioneering legislation at the global level. It's the first law specifically regulating artificial intelligence systems.

### Classification by risk level

The AI Act classifies AI systems into four categories, each with its own obligations:

1. **Unacceptable risk** (PROHIBITED): Chinese-style social scoring, subliminal behavior manipulation, real-time biometric facial recognition in public spaces. These uses are flat-out banned.
2. **High risk**: AI systems used for recruiting, credit granting, criminal justice, healthcare, education. Strict obligations for documentation, testing, transparency, and human oversight.
3. **Limited risk**: chatbots, deepfake systems. Transparency obligations: the user must know they are interacting with an AI.
4. **Minimal risk**: spam filters, video games, etc. No specific obligations.

### Special obligations for foundation models (GPAI)

Models like GPT-4, Claude, Llama, and the like have specific obligations:

- **Technical documentation**: description of architecture, training data, capabilities, and limitations
- **Copyright compliance**: clear policy regarding training data and copyright
- **Data summary**: publication of a sufficiently detailed summary of training data used
- **Systemic risk** (models trained with >10^25 FLOPS): thorough model evaluation, adversarial testing, mandatory incident reporting

<Callout type="paper" title="The systemic risk threshold in practice">
The 10^25 training FLOPS threshold roughly corresponds to the compute level of GPT-4. It's a very high threshold that only the largest models reach. The vast majority of models (7B-70B parameters) stay below it. But if you're training a massive model, the additional obligations are substantial.
</Callout>

## Data traceability (Data Lineage)

### The concept

**Data lineage** means tracing the complete journey of each piece of data: where it came from, what transformations it underwent, and in which models it's used. It's the equivalent of food traceability, but for your data.

### What you need to trace concretely

- **Provenance**: web scraping? Internal database? Data purchased from a third party? Public API?
- **Transformations**: cleaning, filtering, augmentation, tokenization. Each step documented.
- **Consent**: what legal basis justifies using this data?
- **Access**: who accessed the data, when, and for what purpose?
- **Usage**: in which models did this data serve for training?

### Lineage tools

- **Apache Atlas**: governance and lineage for Hadoop/big data
- **OpenLineage**: open standard for data pipeline traceability
- **Amundsen / DataHub**: data catalogs with lineage features
- **dbt**: automatic lineage for SQL transformations

<Callout type="tip" title="Model Cards and Data Cards: document your models and datasets">
HuggingFace popularized Model Cards (a model's identity sheet) and Data Cards (a dataset's identity sheet). The AI Act could make them mandatory for certain high-risk systems. But even without a legal obligation, it's an excellent practice: it forces you to think about the model's limitations, potential biases, and appropriate uses.
</Callout>

## Setting up good data governance

### Inventory and classification

- Exhaustively catalog all your data sources
- Document the legal basis for each source
- Identify data containing personal information
- Classify by sensitivity level (public, internal, confidential, restricted)

### Personal data protection

- **PII detection**: automatically scan your datasets to find emails, phone numbers, names, addresses, social security numbers
- **PII removal**: remove them before any training. This is non-negotiable.
- **Pseudonymization**: replace real identifiers with artificial ones
- **Differential privacy**: a mathematical technique that guarantees it's impossible to extract an individual's data from the trained model. More complex to implement but provides formal guarantees.

### Data quality

- Automated tests on schema, statistical distributions, and completeness
- Continuous monitoring in production to detect drift
- Data Cards to document each dataset version

### Organization

- Appoint a **DPO** (Data Protection Officer) if you haven't already
- Train ML teams on compliance issues: it's not just legal's problem
- Integrate compliance checks into your CI/CD: governance must be automated
- Plan regular audits, both internal and external

<Callout type="warning" title="The fines are real and enforced">
GDPR: up to 20 million euros or 4% of global annual revenue (whichever is higher).
AI Act: up to 35 million euros or 7% of global annual revenue.

These are not empty threats. Numerous companies have already been heavily penalized for GDPR non-compliance. The AI Act is starting to be enforced. It's better to invest in compliance than to pay fines.
</Callout>

## The future: regulation is accelerating everywhere

The global trend is clear: every major economic zone is developing its regulatory framework for AI.

- **European Union**: AI Act, the most advanced and most stringent framework
- **United States**: Executive Orders, state-level legislation (California leading)
- **China**: specific regulation on generative AI, factual accuracy obligations
- **Canada**: AIDA (Artificial Intelligence and Data Act) in the adoption process
- **United Kingdom**: pro-innovation approach with a sectoral rather than horizontal framework

Teams that invest now in solid governance will be ready for any future regulatory framework, regardless of jurisdiction. Those who wait until the last minute risk having to redo everything in a rush.
