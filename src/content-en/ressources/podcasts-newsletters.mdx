## How to Not Be Obsolete in 30 Days

AI moves at a crazy speed. A revolutionary paper on Monday, a framework that makes it obsolete by Friday. If you don't keep up, you end up talking about techniques from 6 months ago as if they were cutting-edge -- and people give you weird looks.

The good news: you **don't** need to follow everything. One newsletter per week and a podcast during your commute, and you stay in the race. The key is choosing the **right** sources.

<Callout type="info" title="Why combine podcasts and newsletters">
Newsletters offer a quick, scannable summary of the news. Podcasts allow for deeper analysis and expert perspectives. Combining the two gives you coverage that is both **broad** (newsletters) and **deep** (podcasts) across the field.
</Callout>

## Newsletters

### The Batch (Andrew Ng / DeepLearning.AI)

[The Batch](https://www.deeplearning.ai/the-batch/) is Andrew Ng's weekly newsletter. It's kind of the evening news of AI:
- Summary of the week's AI news
- Personal analyses from Andrew Ng with a practitioner's point of view
- Accessible tone, no PhD required to follow
- Free, 500K+ subscribers

**Who it's for:** Everyone, from beginner to senior.

### Import AI (Jack Clark)

[Import AI](https://importai.substack.com/) by Jack Clark (co-founder of Anthropic, former policy director at OpenAI):
- Detailed analyses of the week's papers and trends
- Unique perspective from a major industry player
- Focus on the societal and political implications of AI
- Free, weekly

**Who it's for:** Those who want to understand the stakes beyond the technical.

### The Gradient

[The Gradient](https://thegradient.pub/) is both a newsletter and a publication:
- In-depth articles on technical advances
- Interviews with researchers
- Research trend analyses, often more nuanced than the surrounding hype

**Who it's for:** Intermediate to advanced level.

### Ahead of AI (Sebastian Raschka)

[Ahead of AI](https://magazine.sebastianraschka.com/) by Sebastian Raschka (author of "Machine Learning with PyTorch and Scikit-Learn"):
- In-depth monthly reviews of trends in LLMs and deep learning
- Technical but accessible analyses
- Focus on practical aspects and implementations
- Often accompanied by code and notebooks

**Who it's for:** ML practitioners who want technical depth.

<Callout type="tip" title="Technical vs mainstream newsletters">
Distinguish between technical newsletters ([Import AI](https://importai.substack.com/), [Ahead of AI](https://magazine.sebastianraschka.com/)) and mainstream newsletters ([The Batch](https://www.deeplearning.ai/the-batch/), The Algorithmic Bridge). The former assume ML knowledge, the latter are accessible to everyone. Choose based on your level.
</Callout>

### Other Notable Newsletters

- **[Davis Summarizes Papers](https://dblalock.substack.com/)**: daily summary of arXiv papers. Ideal for quickly scanning what's coming out.
- **[The Algorithmic Bridge](https://thealgorithmicbridge.substack.com/)**: AI and society, by Alberto Romero
- **[Ben's Bites](https://bensbites.beehiiv.com/)**: daily AI news, business and startup oriented
- **[Interconnects](https://www.interconnects.ai/)** (Nathan Lambert): analyses of research in RLHF, alignment, and human preferences. Nathan works at Allen AI and knows the subject inside out.
- **[AI Tidbits](https://aitechtidbits.substack.com/)** (Sahar Mor): concise weekly summary. When you have 5 minutes and no more.

## Podcasts

### Latent Space

[Latent Space](https://www.latent.space/) is **THE** go-to technical podcast for AI developers:
- Interviews with founders and CTOs of AI startups
- In-depth discussions on architectures, techniques, and engineering decisions
- Focus on engineering and putting LLMs into production
- Episodes of 1-2 hours, packed with content

**Who it's for:** Engineers and developers building with AI.

### Practical AI

[Practical AI](https://changelog.com/practicalai) is focused on real-world applications:
- How to use AI in production (not just in research)
- Interviews with ML engineers at real companies
- Discussions on everyday tools, workflows, and challenges
- Accessible format, ~45-minute episodes

**Who it's for:** Those who deploy ML for real.

### The TWIML AI Podcast (This Week in Machine Learning)

[TWIML](https://twimlai.com/) by Sam Charrington:
- Broad coverage of ML and AI topics
- Quality interviews with academic and industry experts
- Multiple episodes per week -- you'll never run out of content
- Massive archives (700+ episodes)

**Who it's for:** Those who want broad coverage of the field.

### Machine Learning Street Talk

[MLST](https://www.youtube.com/@MachineLearningStreetTalk) offers in-depth and sometimes philosophical discussions on ML:
- Tim Scarfe, Keith Duggar, and Yannic Kilcher
- Detailed analyses of controversial papers
- Debates on research directions (sometimes heated, always interesting)

**Who it's for:** Those who enjoy debates and deep questions about AI.

<Callout type="paper" title="Conference podcasts">
[NeurIPS](https://neurips.cc/), [ICML](https://icml.cc/), and [ICLR](https://iclr.cc/) publish podcasts and workshop recordings. This content is more technical but offers direct access to cutting-edge research. The "Papers Read Aloud" podcast offers audio readings of papers for those who prefer listening to reading.
</Callout>

### French-Language Podcasts

- **Underscore_ (Guillaume Chaslot)**: discussions on AI and its societal impacts in French. Guillaume is a former YouTube employee and knows recommendation algorithms well.
- **La Science, CQFD (France Culture)**: regular shows on AI within the broader scientific context
- **Lex.AI (French-language podcast)**: interviews with AI researchers and entrepreneurs

### Company Podcasts

Big AI companies also have their podcasts. Obviously, it's partly PR, but the technical content is often excellent:

- **[Google DeepMind Podcast](https://deepmind.google/discover/the-podcast/)**: conversations with DeepMind researchers
- **[NVIDIA AI Podcast](https://blogs.nvidia.com/ai-podcast/)**: industrial applications of AI, hardware and deployment oriented

## Complementary Sources

### arXiv and Aggregators

This is the **primary source** of AI research. If you want to be ahead of the curve, this is where it happens:

- **[arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/list/cs.AI/recent)**: preprint server. All AI papers come out here before being published at conferences. Categories to follow: cs.CL (NLP), cs.CV (vision), cs.LG (machine learning), cs.AI (AI).
- **[Papers With Code](https://paperswithcode.com/)**: papers + code + benchmarks. The best way to track the state of the art on a specific topic.
- **[Semantic Scholar](https://www.semanticscholar.org/)**: AI-powered search engine for scientific literature. The recommendation features are excellent.
- **[Connected Papers](https://www.connectedpapers.com/)**: visualization of connections between papers. Brilliant for exploring a field and understanding the genealogy of ideas.
- **[Hugging Face Daily Papers](https://huggingface.co/papers)**: daily selection of the most important papers, with community discussions.

### Social Media

- **X (Twitter)**: despite everything, it's still where many researchers first publish their work and analyses
- Accounts to follow: [@kaborejules](https://x.com/kaborejules), [@ylecun](https://x.com/ylecun), [@sama](https://x.com/sama), [@hardmaru](https://x.com/hardmaru), [@_jasonwei](https://x.com/_jasonwei), [@kaborejulien](https://x.com/kaborejulien)

<Callout type="warning" title="Information overload">
The flow of information in AI is **enormous**. It's impossible to follow everything, and trying will lead to burnout. Choose **2-3 newsletters** and **1-2 podcasts** that match your level and interests. Depth beats breadth. It's better to thoroughly understand 3 papers a week than to skim 30.
</Callout>
