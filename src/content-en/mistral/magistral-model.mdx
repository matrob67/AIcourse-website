## Magistral: When AI Actually Thinks

Imagine the difference between a student who answers "4" to a math question and a student who writes out their complete reasoning before giving their answer. The first one might get it right by luck. The second, by making each step explicit, is wrong far less often, and when they are wrong, you can identify exactly where and why.

That's the fundamental principle behind Magistral. Standard LLM models generate a response directly. Magistral first produces an explicit **chain of thought**, meaning its step-by-step reasoning, then the answer. The result: drastically superior performance on difficult tasks.

### The Conceptual Leap: Answering vs Reasoning

**Standard model**: "Solve 2x + 5 = 13"
--> Directly answers "x = 4"
(This works for simple cases. For complex ones, the model often hallucinates.)

**Reasoning model (Magistral)**:
1. "I see a linear equation with one unknown"
2. "I need to isolate x"
3. "I subtract 5 from both sides: 2x = 8"
4. "I divide by 2: x = 4"

This "thinking out loud" dramatically improves accuracy. Why? Because each intermediate step creates a checkpoint. If the model makes an error at step 2, the following steps have a chance to correct it. Without a chain of thought, an early error in the internal reasoning propagates silently.

<Callout type="info" title="Reasoning = explainability">
Important enterprise bonus: you see exactly how the model arrived at its conclusion. This is crucial for debugging, trust in results, and auditing. A model that says "the answer is 42" with no explanation is a black box. A model that shows its reasoning is a transparent colleague.
</Callout>

### AIME 2024: 73.6% Success Rate

The AIME (American Invitational Mathematics Examination) is one of the most difficult math exams in the world, reserved for the best American high school students. For reference: GPT-4 scores about 25 to 30% on this exam. Magistral scores **73.6%**.

This number is significant because AIME problems aren't routine exercises. They're problems that require creativity, multi-step reasoning, and often non-obvious approaches. A score of 73.6% means Magistral can solve university-level math problems that no generalist LLM can tackle.

### Flash Answers: The Best of Both Worlds

**The reasoning problem**: generating a long, detailed chain of thought is expensive in tokens and latency. For a simple question like "What's the capital of France?", deploying the full reasoning apparatus is wasteful.

**Magistral's solution**: Flash Answers automatically detects whether your question is simple or complex.

- Simple question ("Translate this word to French") --> direct answer, no chain of thought, in a few milliseconds
- Complex question ("Prove that the square root of 2 is irrational") --> full reasoning activated

**The result**: up to **10x more throughput** on simple questions, while preserving deep reasoning when needed. You don't have to configure anything: Flash Answers decides automatically.

<Callout type="tip" title="Automatic smart routing">
You don't have to do anything. Flash Answers analyzes each question and decides on its own whether full reasoning is needed. If your question requires thought, Magistral thinks. If not, it answers fast. It's the best of both worlds with no compromise.
</Callout>

### Magistral Small: Reasoning in Open-Source

Mistral published a compact version of Magistral as open-source. This is an important strategic choice:

- **Democratized access**: researchers, students, and small organizations benefit from reasoning without a cloud budget
- **Open research**: the community can study, reproduce, and improve the reasoning mechanisms
- **Fine-tuning**: companies can adapt Magistral Small to their specific domains and benefit from reasoning on their own use cases

### Multilingual and Structured

Magistral v1.2 reasons in French, English, German, and other languages. This isn't trivial: many competing reasoning models only really work well in English. Magistral maintains the quality of its reasoning in European languages.

The generated chains of thought are structured and readable, which facilitates debugging and human verification.

### Where to Use Magistral

**Mathematics and science**: physics, chemistry, differential equations. Any problem that requires multi-step reasoning with intermediate calculations.

**Legal analysis**: interpreting complex contracts, building arguments, analyzing case law. Structured reasoning is exactly what a lawyer needs to verify the model's work.

**Planning**: breaking down a complex problem into sub-problems, defining an action plan, anticipating obstacles. Magistral excels at structured thinking.

**Complex programming**: algorithm design, debugging distributed systems, designing software architectures. Tasks that require thinking before coding.

<Callout type="warning" title="The cost of reasoning">
Chain of thought = more tokens generated = higher bill. For high-volume applications where most questions are simple, the right strategy is to route simple questions to Small or Medium, and reserve Magistral for complex cases that truly benefit from reasoning.
</Callout>
