## The Problem: Your Model Completes Text, It Doesn't Follow Instructions

Take a raw LLaMA model, fresh out of pre-training. It's an excellent text completer: it has read the entire internet and knows how to predict the next word with impressive accuracy. But tell it "Translate this into English" and it will... sometimes continue your sentence as if writing an article, sometimes rephrase your instruction, sometimes give a correct answer by chance. It's random, because it has never learned to **follow instructions**.

**Instruction tuning** (or SFT, Supervised Fine-Tuning) fixes this. You show the model thousands of instruction-response pairs so it understands: "when someone asks me a question, I should answer, not continue the sentence." It's the step that transforms a completer into an **assistant**.

<Callout type="info" title={"The Complete Pipeline of a Modern LLM"}>
1. **Pre-training** on a massive corpus (internet, books, code)
2. **Instruction tuning** (SFT) on instruction-response pairs
3. **Alignment** via RLHF or DPO

Each step refines the behavior. From text completer to helpful assistant, then to aligned and safe assistant. Instruction tuning is the pivotal moment: the one where the model "understands" what's expected of it.
</Callout>

## The Pioneers: FLAN (2021)

Google launched the movement with FLAN (Finetuned Language Net). The insight was simple but powerful: fine-tuning a model on **62 diverse NLP tasks**, all phrased as natural language instructions, drastically improves its zero-shot capabilities on tasks it has never seen.

Then came **FLAN-T5** and **FLAN-PaLM**, trained on more than **1,800 tasks** with varied templates for each task. The idea: the more you diversify the instructions seen during training, the better the model generalizes to new instructions. It's like a student who practices on many different types of exercises: on exam day, they know how to adapt.

In parallel, the **T0** project (BigScience) did the same thing with a community touch: instruction templates were collaboratively generated by researchers from around the world.

## The Open-Source Revolution: Alpaca and Self-Instruct (2023)

Stanford had an idea as simple as it was audacious: use GPT-3.5 to **automatically generate** 52,000 instruction-response pairs from just 175 starting examples. Total cost: less than $600. Then they fine-tuned LLaMA-7B on it.

This technique is called **Self-Instruct**: you give a few seed examples to a powerful model, and it generates thousands more. It's **data distillation** without human annotation. Imagine an experienced professor (GPT-3.5) creating exercises to train a promising student (LLaMA).

The result triggered an avalanche: Vicuna, WizardLM, and dozens of other open-source models began rivaling ChatGPT, all at trivial costs.

### OpenHermes: Quality Above All

OpenHermes pushed the approach further with ~1M carefully selected examples:
- **Multiple sources**: GPT-4, code, math, logical reasoning
- **Rigorous filtering**: removal of poorly formulated, inconsistent, or repetitive examples
- **Result**: Hermes-2-Mistral-7B, among the best 7B models of its generation

The lesson? It's not raw quantity that matters, it's the diversity and quality of examples.

## Data Format: The Conversational Standard

```json
{
  "messages": [
    {"role": "system", "content": "You are a helpful assistant."},
    {"role": "user", "content": "Explain photosynthesis."},
    {"role": "assistant", "content": "Photosynthesis is..."}
  ]
}
```

This is the ChatML format, which has become the de facto standard. Clear, structured, compatible with all frameworks (TRL, Axolotl, LLaMA-Factory). The "system" field sets the general behavior, "user" contains the question, "assistant" the expected response.

## Task Diversity: The Real Secret

A good instruction tuning dataset doesn't just contain Q&A. It covers a broad spectrum:
- **Open-ended questions**: "Explain...", "Describe...", "Compare..."
- **Structured tasks**: "Translate...", "Summarize...", "Extract the entities from..."
- **Reasoning**: "Solve this problem...", "Analyze the pros and cons..."
- **Creativity**: "Write a poem about...", "Invent a story where..."
- **Code**: "Implement...", "Debug this code...", "Optimize this function..."
- **Multi-turn**: complete conversations with evolving context

This diversity is crucial. A model trained only on factual Q&A will be bad at creativity. A model trained only on code will be weird in conversation.

<Callout type="tip" title="Quality > Quantity: The LIMA Lesson">
The LIMA paper (Meta, 2023) showed that a model fine-tuned on just 1,000 carefully selected examples can rival models trained on hundreds of thousands of mediocre examples. Alpagasus confirmed: 5,000 excellent examples beat 500K average ones. The moral: invest in the quality of your data, not the volume.
</Callout>

## Instruction Tuning Best Practices

### 1. Respect Chat Templates
Each model has its exact conversation format. ChatML for Mistral, the Alpaca format for LLaMA, etc. A wrong template = a confused model that mixes instructions and responses.

### 2. Mask Instructions in the Loss
Compute the loss **only** on the response, not on the instruction. The model should learn to *respond*, not to *predict questions*. It's a technical detail that massively changes quality.

### 3. Aim for Detailed and Structured Responses
The model imitates the style of the responses it sees. Short, sloppy responses produce a sloppy assistant. Well-structured responses, with clear paragraphs and examples, produce a helpful assistant.

### 4. Diversify Seriously
Don't fine-tune only on factual questions. Vary the domains, response formats, and difficulty levels. The more varied situations the model sees, the better it generalizes.

<Callout type="warning" title="The Synthetic Data Trap">
Data generated by a powerful LLM (GPT-5.4, Claude Sonnet 4.6...) inherits its biases: its style, its refusals, its phrasings. Worse, training on synthetic data then regenerating new data with the resulting model can cause "model collapse": quality degrades with each generation, like a photocopy of a photocopy. The solution: always mix synthetic and human-annotated data.
</Callout>

## The Impact: The Industry Standard Pipeline

Today, absolutely every serious model follows this three-step pipeline:
1. **Pre-training** on a massive corpus (billions of tokens)
2. **Instruction tuning** (SFT) on quality instruction-response pairs
3. **Alignment** (RLHF/DPO) to refine behavior

Each step makes the model more useful and more aligned with what humans expect. And instruction tuning is the decisive moment: the one that makes the difference between a model that "knows things" and a model that "can help."

---

**Essence**: Instruction tuning is the step that transforms a raw model into a usable assistant. Without it, your LLM is an encyclopedia that can't hold a conversation. With it, it understands what you want and tries to respond.
