## LeWorldModel (LeWM): The First Stable JEPA from Pixels

For years, Yann LeCun has championed a clear vision: LLMs are not enough to achieve artificial general intelligence. We need **world models** -- models that learn an internal representation of the physical world and can **plan** in that representation space. The architecture he proposes for this is called **JEPA** (Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture).

The problem? Until now, no one could make JEPA work end-to-end from pixels without resorting to heavy technical hacks. LeWorldModel, published in March 2026 by Lucas Maes, Yann LeCun et al., changes the game.

## The Problem: Representation Collapse

When you train a world model directly from pixels, you hit a devastating phenomenon: **representation collapse**.

The model needs to learn to encode images into representation vectors, then predict the representation of the next image. But nothing prevents it from finding a trivial shortcut: encoding **all images** to the same vector (or a very reduced subspace). The prediction loss then drops to zero, but the representations are useless -- they capture no information about the world.

<Callout type="info" title={"The collapse analogy"}>
Imagine a translator who translates every book into a single word: "ok." Technically, they can perfectly predict the translation of any book (it's always "ok"). But the translation contains no useful information. That's exactly what happens with representation collapse.
</Callout>

Existing solutions rely on **complex heuristics**:
- **Stop-gradient**: you prevent the gradient from backpropagating through part of the network, which breaks the symmetry but in an ad hoc manner
- **EMA (Exponential Moving Average)**: you maintain a slow copy of the encoder as a target, adding an extra mechanism to calibrate
- **Frozen pre-trained encoders**: you use a network like DINO that's already trained and never touch its weights, which prevents end-to-end learning

These approaches work, but they introduce **numerous hyperparameters** (typically 6 or more) and prevent the system from learning its own optimal representations. DINO-WM, for example, uses a frozen DINO encoder that has never seen the target task's data.

## The LeWM Architecture

LeWM is remarkably simple. The system consists of just two networks:

### The Encoder

A network that takes a frame as input (an image) and produces a **latent token of dimension 192**. It's the compact representation of the world's state at that moment. No pre-trained backbone, no frozen features: the encoder learns **from scratch**, directly from pixels.

### The Predictor

A network that receives the current frame's embedding **and the action taken**, and predicts the next frame's embedding. This is the "world model" proper: it encodes the environment's dynamics in latent space.

### Only Two Loss Terms

Training relies on exactly **two loss terms**:

1. **The prediction loss**: the error between the embedding predicted by the predictor and the embedding actually produced by the encoder for the next frame. That's the main objective.

2. **The SIGReg regularizer**: a term that prevents representation collapse (detailed below).

That's it. No stop-gradient, no EMA, no frozen encoder. The number of hyperparameters drops from **6 (for PLDM, an alternative) to just 1**: the coefficient $\lambda$ of the regularizer.

## SIGReg: The Key to Stability

SIGReg is the component that makes the entire system possible. It's an elegant statistical regularizer based on a deep mathematical result.

### The Cramer-Wold Theorem

The principle rests on the **Cramer-Wold theorem**: a multivariate distribution is entirely characterized by the set of its one-dimensional projections. In other words, if all 1D projections of a distribution match those of a standard Gaussian, then the distribution is itself a standard Gaussian.

<Callout type="info" title={"The intuition behind Cramer-Wold"}>
Instead of directly checking that a cloud of points in 192 dimensions is well-distributed (which is extremely difficult), you project the cloud onto **random lines** and check that each 1D projection looks like a Gaussian. If all projections are good, the full distribution is good. It's much simpler and numerically stable.
</Callout>

### The Mechanism in Practice

1. You project the batch's embeddings onto $M$ **random directions** in the representation space
2. For each 1D projection, you compute the **Epps-Pulley test statistic**, which measures how much the projected distribution deviates from a standard Gaussian
3. You average these statistics to get the SIGReg regularization term

The result: embeddings are pushed toward an isotropic distribution (well spread in all directions), which makes collapse **mathematically impossible**. If embeddings become redundant or collapse to a subspace, the 1D projections will deviate sharply from a Gaussian, and the penalty will increase.

### A Single Hyperparameter

The practical advantage is immense. The only hyperparameter to tune is $\lambda$, the weight of the regularizer relative to the prediction loss. And its optimal value is found by **bisection search** in $O(\log n)$, compared to $O(n^6)$ for grid search over PLDM's 6 hyperparameters.

## Results

LeWM is not only simpler, but also more performant and more efficient than existing approaches.

### Compactness and Efficiency

- **~15M parameters** only (compared to hundreds of millions for DINO-based approaches)
- Trainable on **a single GPU** in a few hours
- No need for pre-trained features: everything is learned end-to-end

### Planning Speed

This is where the difference is most spectacular. The world model is used for **planning**: at each moment, the robot simulates possible action sequences in latent space and chooses the best one.

| Model | Time per planning cycle |
|---|---|
| DINO-WM | 47 s |
| LeWM | **0.98 s** |

LeWM plans **48 times faster** than DINO-WM. The main reason: the latent space is compact (192 dimensions) and learned specifically for the task, instead of relying on a general-purpose DINO encoder not optimized for planning.

### Push-T Benchmark

On the **Push-T** robotics benchmark (a task where a robot must push a T-shaped object to a target position), LeWM achieves a **96% success rate**, demonstrating that the learned representations are not only stable but also functionally excellent for control.

## Why This Matters

### The Timing Is Striking

LeWM was published **just days after** the record-breaking fundraise of AMI Labs, the startup founded by Yann LeCun after his departure from Meta. AMI Labs raised **$1.03 billion** in a seed round, at a $3.5 billion valuation, with the explicit mission to build world models.

LeWM is not an AMI Labs product (it's an academic paper), but it experimentally validates the fundamental thesis that justifies that fundraise: **JEPA can work from pixels, without heavy heuristics**.

### "Structure Is All You Need"

<Callout type="paper" title={"A counterpoint to the dominant paradigm"}>
Where the LLM industry pushes toward ever more parameters and data ("scale is all you need"), LeWM demonstrates the opposite: with the **right structure** (JEPA + SIGReg), 15 million parameters and a single GPU are enough to outperform much heavier systems. Mathematical structure replaces brute force.
</Callout>

### LeCun's Vision

LeWM fits into a broader vision that Yann LeCun has championed for years: LLMs, as impressive as they may be, are not the path to real intelligence. A truly intelligent system must have an **internal model of the world** -- the ability to simulate the consequences of its actions before executing them, just as a human does when planning a movement.

LLMs manipulate symbols. World models understand **physics**. LeWM shows that this second path is not only viable, but can be simple, efficient, and accessible -- a single GPU, a few hours, 15 million parameters.
