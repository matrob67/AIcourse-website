## The Problem: Initial Retrieval Is Imperfect

You've done your retrieval: embeddings, vector search, maybe some BM25. You get back 20 candidate documents. The problem? The ranking is approximate. The most relevant document for your question might be at position 8 instead of position 1.

A **reranker** takes these 20 candidates and **reorders** them with a more powerful and precise model. Result: the right document moves to the top of the list, and the LLM receives the most relevant documents to generate its answer.

It's a **two-stage** architecture: first a fast but imprecise retrieval (20-100 candidates), then a slow but precise reranking (top 5-10).

<Callout type="info" title="The Fundamental Speed-Precision Tradeoff">
**Bi-encoder** (retrieval): the query and document are encoded **separately** into vectors, then compared by cosine similarity. Very fast (we pre-encode all documents). But imprecise: the two vectors never "talk" to each other directly.

**Cross-encoder** (reranking): the query and document are encoded **together** in the same Transformer. Much slower (one forward pass per pair). But very precise: the model sees the fine-grained interaction between every word of the question and every word of the document.

Reranking combines the best of both: the speed of the bi-encoder for rough sorting, the precision of the cross-encoder for refinement.
</Callout>

## Cross-Encoders: Precision at the Cost of Speed

A cross-encoder takes the query and document **concatenated** as input:

`[CLS] your_question [SEP] the_document [SEP]`

Everything goes through a Transformer that understands the **interaction** between the question and document. The `[CLS]` token is projected onto a relevance score between 0 and 1.

Why is this so much more precise than a bi-encoder? Because the Transformer sees every word of the question and every word of the document **at the same time**. It can capture subtle nuances: the question is about "Python the language" and the document is about "python the snake" -- a bi-encoder would confuse the two (similar embeddings), a cross-encoder distinguishes them immediately thanks to cross-context.

### Why You Can't Use It Everywhere

The price is simple: a cross-encoder must process each query-document pair individually. For a corpus of 1 million documents, that's 1 million forward passes per query. Totally impractical.

Hence the strategy: use the cross-encoder **only to rerank** the top 20 to 50 candidates from the initial retrieval.

## ColBERT: The Elegant Compromise Between Speed and Precision

ColBERT occupies a **fascinating sweet spot** between bi-encoders and cross-encoders. Its idea: instead of encoding an entire document into ONE single vector (bi-encoder), you generate **one vector per token of the document**. Same for the query.

The relevance score is calculated via **MaxSim** (Maximum Similarity): for each query token, you find the most similar document token, then sum:

$$\text{score} = \sum_i \max_j \text{sim}(\text{query\_token}_i,\ \text{doc\_token}_j)$$

It's like asking: "For each word in my question, does at least ONE word in the document answer it well?" Then you aggregate the answers.

**The advantages**:
- Documents are **pre-encoded** (like a bi-encoder): they can be indexed offline
- The interaction between query and document is **fine-grained** (like a cross-encoder): each query token interacts with each document token
- It scales on large corpora thanks to pre-computed document embeddings

**The downsides**:
- Storage explodes: one vector per token instead of one per document. For a corpus of 1M documents of 200 tokens each, that's 200M vectors.
- Indexing and search are more complex.

### ColBERT v2 and RAGatouille

ColBERT v2 solves the storage problem with **residual quantization** that compresses embeddings by 30 to 50x. The **RAGatouille** library makes usage trivial:

```python
from ragatouille import RAGPretrainedModel

RAG = RAGPretrainedModel.from_pretrained("colbertv2.0")
RAG.index(documents, index_name="my_index")
results = RAG.search("my question", k=10)
```

## The Optimal Production Pipeline: Three Stages

1. **Query** arrives
2. **Hybrid Retrieval** (BM25 + Dense) &rarr; Top 50-100 candidates
3. **Cross-encoder Reranking** &rarr; Top 5-10 documents
4. **LLM** generates the answer from the top documents

### Stage 1: Hybrid Retrieval (fast)
You combine lexical search (BM25, keyword-based) and dense search (embeddings, meaning-based). The two are complementary: BM25 is strong on exact terms, dense is strong on semantics. In a few milliseconds, you have 50-100 candidates.

### Stage 2: Reranking (precise)
The cross-encoder scores and reorders candidates. Acceptable latency: 50 to 200ms depending on the model and number of candidates.

### Stage 3: Generation
The LLM generates its answer based on the top 5 reranked documents. The quality of context provided to the LLM is drastically better than with retrieval alone.

```python
from sentence_transformers import CrossEncoder

reranker = CrossEncoder("cross-encoder/ms-marco-MiniLM-L-6-v2")

pairs = [(query, doc) for doc in retrieved_docs]
scores = reranker.predict(pairs)
reranked = sorted(zip(retrieved_docs, scores), key=lambda x: -x[1])
```

Adding a reranker costs little: 50ms of latency and about ten lines of code. The gain in recall is huge: 8 to 10 additional points. It's the improvement with the best return on investment in the entire RAG pipeline. If you make only one optimization, make it this one.

## Practical Considerations

### How Many Candidates to Rerank?
- **20-30**: lightweight cross-encoder, minimal latency. The default choice.
- **50-100**: powerful reranker, maximum quality. For critical cases.
- **100+**: diminishing returns. The marginal gain no longer justifies the cost.

### Watch Out for the Bottleneck
The reranker can become the **bottleneck** of the pipeline. If reranking accounts for 80% of total query time, you need to either reduce the number of candidates, use a lighter model, or parallelize.

<Callout type="warning" title="Monitor End-to-End Latency">
A fast RAG pipeline (200ms total) with slow reranking (500ms) = a pipeline that's now slow. Always measure the impact of reranking on total latency, and adjust the number of candidates and model choice accordingly.
</Callout>

Reranking has become **standard** in production RAG systems. The combination of hybrid retrieval + cross-encoder reranking is the recommended pipeline to maximize result quality while maintaining acceptable latency.
