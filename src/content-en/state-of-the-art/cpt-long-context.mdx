## Continued Pre-Training (CPT): specializing without starting from scratch

### The principle

A generalist LLM (Large Language Model) like LLaMA or Mistral is trained on hundreds of billions of tokens from the internet. The result: a model that knows a bit of everything, but lacks depth in specialized domains --- medicine, law, finance, code, or a specific under-represented language.

**Continued Pre-Training** (CPT) consists of taking this already-trained model and continuing its training on a specialized data corpus. The training objective remains the same: **next-token prediction** (predicting the next word). Only the corpus changes.

The analogy: rather than building a hospital from scratch, you renovate an existing building. The foundations (language comprehension), the structure (grammar, syntax, basic reasoning) are already solid. You add specialized equipment on top.

### Why not just fine-tuning?

Classic fine-tuning (SFT, Supervised Fine-Tuning) uses question/answer pairs to adjust the model's behavior. CPT, on the other hand, directly injects **raw knowledge** into the model's weights by letting it absorb entire documents --- scientific articles, legal texts, codebases. The distinction matters:

- **SFT**: teaches the model *how to respond* (format, style, instructions)
- **CPT**: teaches the model *what to know* (domain knowledge)

In practice, you often do both in sequence: CPT first to inject knowledge, then SFT to teach the model to use it in a conversational format.

### Data mixing: avoiding catastrophic forgetting

The biggest risk of CPT is called **catastrophic forgetting**. While specializing, the model can lose its general knowledge --- like a general practitioner who specializes so much in cardiology that they forget how to treat a cold.

The solution: **mix** specialized data with a sample of general data during training. This mixing acts as a permanent reminder for the model, preventing it from forgetting what it already knew.

<Callout type="info" title={"Typical data mixing ratios"}>
Common practice is to compose each training batch with:

- **70-90% specialized data** (target domain)
- **10-30% general data** (sample from the original corpus or generic web data)

The exact ratio depends on the desired depth of specialization. The more specialized you want the model, the more you increase the domain share --- but below 10% general data, catastrophic forgetting becomes a real risk.
</Callout>

### Learning rate: take it easy

The base model was trained with a certain learning rate --- typically around $3 \times 10^{-4}$ for a 7B model. For CPT, you resume with a **lower learning rate**, generally between 10% and 50% of the initial LR.

Why? Because the model's weights are already well calibrated. Updates that are too aggressive risk destroying existing representations. It's like tuning a piano that's already well-tuned: you make small fine adjustments, not big turns of the key.

In practice:
- **Initial pre-training LR**: $\sim 3 \times 10^{-4}$
- **CPT LR**: $\sim 3 \times 10^{-5}$ to $1.5 \times 10^{-4}$
- **Warmup**: short (a few hundred steps), since the model is already in a good convergence basin
- **Schedule**: cosine or WSD (Warmup-Stable-Decay), with WSD being preferred as it allows restarting a new CPT more easily

### Concrete examples

| Model | Base | Domain | CPT Data | Result |
|--------|------|---------|-------------|----------|
| **BloombergGPT** | BLOOM | Finance | 363B financial tokens | Surpasses GPT-3 on financial benchmarks |
| **BioMistral** | Mistral 7B | Biomedical | PubMed + medical articles | +25% on medical benchmarks (MedQA) |
| **Code LLaMA** | LLaMA 2 | Code | 500B code tokens | +30% on HumanEval compared to LLaMA 2 |
| **SaulLM** | Mistral 7B | Law | Legal texts | +40% on legal benchmarks |

### The cost: 10 to 100 times cheaper

Pre-training a 7B LLM from scratch requires about 1 to 2 trillion tokens and thousands of GPU-hours. CPT typically needs between 50B and 500B tokens --- that's 5 to 25% of the original budget. In terms of cost:

- **Pre-training from scratch (7B)**: $\sim$ $500K--2M
- **CPT on 100B tokens**: $\sim$ $10K--50K

This cost difference is what makes CPT so attractive. You get a competitive specialized model for a fraction of the price of a full training run.

<Callout type="tip" title={"When to choose CPT vs. fine-tuning"}>
- **CPT**: when you need to inject large amounts of knowledge from an entire domain (tens of billions of tokens of specialized data)
- **Fine-tuning (SFT/LoRA)**: when you want to adjust the model's behavior or output format with a few thousand examples
- **Both**: the most common combination in production. CPT for knowledge, then SFT for behavior.
</Callout>

## Long Context Extension: going beyond the training window

### The problem: a fixed window

LLMs are trained with a fixed context length --- 4K tokens for the first LLaMA models, 8K for Mistral 7B. Beyond this limit, performance collapses brutally. The model produces incoherent responses, forgets the beginning of the text, or generates gibberish.

Why? Because the model's positional encoding --- typically RoPE (Rotary Position Embedding) --- has never seen positions beyond its training window. RoPE encodes each token's position via rotations at different frequencies. At position 8001 of a model trained on 8K, the rotations take values unknown to the model. It's like asking someone to tell time on a clock whose hands suddenly spin at a speed they've never seen: the numbers are moving, but it no longer means anything.

Yet, users want to process entire documents, codebases, long conversations --- 32K, 128K, even 1M tokens. How do you get there?

### Technique 1: Position Interpolation (PI)

The most direct idea: **compress** all positions to fit within the range the model knows.

$$
\text{compressed\_position} = \text{real\_position} \times \frac{L_{\text{train}}}{L_{\text{target}}}
$$

Where $L_{\text{train}}$ is the original training length and $L_{\text{target}}$ the desired length. Concretely, to go from 4K to 16K: you divide each position by 4. Position 16000 becomes 4000, and the model stays in its comfort zone.

**Advantage**: extremely simple --- a single line of code, a multiplicative factor on positions.

**Drawback**: you lose local resolution. By compressing positions by a factor of 4, two adjacent tokens go from a distance of 1 to a distance of 0.25. The model distinguishes nearby tokens less well, which degrades fine understanding of local text. It's like taking a ruler graduated in centimeters and stretching it to measure in meters: you lose precision for small measurements.

### Technique 2: NTK-aware scaling

The key observation: in RoPE, not all frequencies play the same role.

- **High frequencies** (first dimension pairs) encode local relationships --- which token is just before or after me
- **Low frequencies** (last dimension pairs) encode global position --- where am I in the document

PI treats all frequencies the same way, which is suboptimal. NTK-aware scaling (Neural Tangent Kernel) modifies RoPE's **base** rather than the positions themselves:

$$
\text{base}_{\text{new}} = \text{base} \times \alpha^{d/(d-2)}
$$

Where $\alpha$ is the extension factor (e.g., 4 to go from 4K to 16K) and $d$ the embedding dimension. The effect: low frequencies are stretched (to cover the wider window) while high frequencies remain nearly intact (to preserve local resolution).

It's smarter than PI: you adjust the rotation frequencies rather than crushing the positions, which preserves the model's ability to distinguish neighboring tokens.

### Technique 3: YaRN (Yet another RoPE extensioN)

YaRN combines the best ideas from PI and NTK-aware scaling by adding a third ingredient: an **attention correction factor**.

Its three components:

1. **NTK-by-parts**: divides RoPE frequencies into three groups --- high frequencies are left unmodified, mid frequencies are partially interpolated, low frequencies are fully interpolated. It's a more granular version of NTK-aware scaling.
2. **Selective interpolation**: each frequency receives treatment adapted to its sensitivity, rather than uniform treatment.
3. **Attention scaling**: a factor $\sqrt{s}$ (where $s$ is the extension factor) is applied to attention scores to compensate for the increase in token count. Without this correction, attention scores get diluted over a larger number of positions, degrading quality.

<Callout type="info" title={"PI vs. NTK vs. YaRN at a glance"}>
- **PI**: compresses all positions uniformly. Simple but loses local resolution.
- **NTK-aware**: modifies RoPE's base to treat frequencies differently. Better local preservation.
- **YaRN**: combines NTK + selective interpolation + attention correction. Results closest to native training at the target length.

In practice, YaRN has become the standard method for context extension in recent open-source models.
</Callout>

### The essential step: fine-tuning on long sequences

Modifying positions via PI, NTK, or YaRN isn't sufficient on its own. The model has never learned to **use** longer contexts --- it doesn't know it needs to maintain attention on information located 50K tokens away.

After applying RoPE scaling, you therefore need to perform **continued pre-training** (or fine-tuning) on genuinely long documents: books, 30-page scientific articles, entire codebases, transcripts of long conversations.

This fine-tuning is relatively short --- a few hundred to a few thousand steps suffice --- but it's crucial for the model to learn to exploit its new context window.

### Progressive extension: climbing in stages

A direct extension from 4K to 128K is possible but risky: the jump is too large and the model may converge poorly. In practice, you often proceed in **progressive steps**:

$$
4\text{K} \rightarrow 16\text{K} \rightarrow 32\text{K} \rightarrow 128\text{K}
$$

At each step, you apply the corresponding RoPE scaling, then fine-tune on sequences of the target length. The model adapts gradually, producing more stable results than a direct jump.

<Callout type="warning" title={"Memory cost explodes with length"}>
Standard attention has quadratic complexity $O(n^2)$ relative to sequence length $n$. Going from 4K to 128K multiplies attention memory by $(128/4)^2 = 1024$. That's why context extension always comes with techniques like Flash Attention, Ring Attention, or Gradient Checkpointing to make fine-tuning on long sequences physically possible.
</Callout>

### Examples of models with extended context

| Model | Original context | Extended context | Technique |
|--------|-------------------|-----------------|-----------|
| **LLaMA 2 Long** | 4K | 32K | PI + progressive fine-tuning |
| **Mistral 7B** | 8K (sliding window) | 32K+ | NTK-aware + fine-tuning |
| **Code LLaMA** | 4K (LLaMA 2) | 100K | PI + fine-tuning on long code |
| **Yi 34B** | 4K | 200K | YaRN + fine-tuning |
| **Qwen 2** | 8K | 128K | YaRN + progressive fine-tuning |

The result of these techniques: models that can process documents of hundreds of pages, entire codebases, or conversations lasting several hours --- all while maintaining the comprehension quality they had on short sequences.
