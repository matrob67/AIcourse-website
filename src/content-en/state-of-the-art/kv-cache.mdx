## Why the KV Cache exists

When a Transformer generates text token by token (autoregressive mode), each new token must "look at" all previous tokens via the attention mechanism. Without a cache, that means recomputing the keys (K) and values (V) for **all previous tokens** at each step. For the 1000th token, you'd have to recompute K/V for the 999 tokens before it. That's absurd waste.

The solution: the **KV Cache**. You store each token's K and V at the moment it's processed, and reuse them for all subsequent tokens. Each new token only costs the computation of its own K/V, plus the attention operation with the existing cache.

In terms of complexity, you go from $O(L \times d^2)$ per token to $O(d^2)$ for the K/V computation (the attention itself remains $O(L \times d)$).



<Callout type="info" title={"The library analogy"}>
Imagine you're looking for a book in a library of 10,000 volumes. Without a cache, for each question you go back to the shelves to find all the relevant books. With a cache, you keep the useful books stacked on your table. Next question? You look at your stack directly. Enormously faster.
</Callout>

## Real KV Cache size: it blows up fast

Let's do the math for LLaMA 2 7B with an 8K token context in FP16 (2 bytes per value):

$$
\text{KV cache} = 2 \times 32 \text{ layers} \times 32 \text{ heads} \times 8000 \text{ tokens} \times 128 \text{ dim} \times 2 \text{ bytes} \approx 4 \text{ GB}
$$

The factor of 2 at the beginning counts both keys AND values. 4 GB just for the cache of **a single request**.

For GPT-3 175B with 8K tokens? About **18 GB**. On an A100 GPU with 80 GB, once the model is loaded and the cache allocated, there's almost nothing left to process other requests in parallel.

<Callout type="warning" title="The real bottleneck: memory bandwidth">
Autoregressive inference is limited by **memory bandwidth**, not by compute power. For each generated token, the GPU must read the entire KV cache from HBM memory. For 4 GB of cache on an A100 at 2 TB/s bandwidth, that takes about 2ms per token. The GPU may be incredibly powerful in compute, but it spends its time waiting for data to arrive from memory.
</Callout>

## KV Cache compression techniques

### Cache quantization

The most direct approach: reduce the precision of stored K/V.

**INT8**: instead of FP16 (2 bytes), you store in INT8 (1 byte). The cache is cut in half. Degradation is minimal for most tasks.

**INT4**: 4x reduction, but trickier. You need to quantize by groups to preserve precision.

**FP8**: a good compromise that preserves FP16's dynamic range while halving the size.

<Callout type="tip" title="Key/value asymmetry">
Keys and values have different statistical distributions. Keys tend to be uniformly distributed, while values exhibit outliers (extreme values). Asymmetric quantization (with a zero-point) works better than symmetric for values. A detail that matters in practice.
</Callout>

### Smart eviction: dropping what's no longer useful

Instead of keeping everything, you can intelligently remove cache entries that contribute little to attention.

**Heavy Hitter Oracle (H2O)**: identifies tokens that receive the most attention (the "heavy hitters") and only keeps those, plus a local window of recent tokens. Cache reduction of 5 to 10x.

**Sink Tokens**: systematically keeps the very first tokens (typically the system prompt) in addition to the local window. Essential for tasks that depend on an initial instruction.

**Sliding Window**: a radical approach used by Mistral. You only keep the last $W$ tokens in the cache. Ultra memory-efficient, but only works if the recent context is sufficient for the task.

### Structural compression: rethinking the architecture

**Multi-Head Latent Attention (MLA)**, introduced by DeepSeek, is the most aggressive compression. Instead of storing K and V separately for each attention head, they're compressed together into a shared latent space:

$$
\text{latent} = W_{\text{compress}} [K; V]
$$
$$
K' = W_{\text{decompress\_K}} \cdot \text{latent}, \quad V' = W_{\text{decompress\_V}} \cdot \text{latent}
$$

In plain terms: instead of storing K and V separately (large), you compress them together into a small `latent` vector via a learned matrix $W_{\text{compress}}$. When you need K and V to compute attention, you reconstruct them on the fly with two decompression matrices. The cache only stores the `latent` vector, which is much smaller. At attention time, K and V are reconstructed on the fly.

<Callout type="paper" title="MLA in DeepSeek-V2: 93% compression">
DeepSeek-V2 reduces the KV cache by 93% with MLA. This is far more aggressive than GQA (Group Query Attention) while being more expressive: K and V share a rich representation in latent space, rather than simply sharing heads.
</Callout>

## Implementation optimizations

### PagedAttention (vLLM)

vLLM's brilliant idea: manage the KV cache like an **OS's virtual memory**.

- The cache is divided into **pages** (fixed-size blocks, typically 16 tokens)
- Pages are dynamically allocated as needed, not pre-allocated
- Multiple requests with the same prefix (system prompt) **share the same pages**

The result: near-zero memory fragmentation. Where naive allocation wasted 60-80% of GPU memory, PagedAttention achieves near-optimal utilization.

### Prefix Caching

If 100 requests share the same 2000-token system prompt, you compute the KV cache for that prompt **once** and share it across all requests. The savings are massive in production deployments where the system prompt is fixed.

### Speculative Decoding

Instead of generating one token at a time (very bandwidth-inefficient), a small fast model generates several candidate tokens, then the large model verifies them in a single batch. The cache is updated in batch rather than token by token, which makes much better use of GPU bandwidth.

## The direct economic impact

Reducing KV cache per request means being able to process more requests in parallel on the same GPU:

$$
\text{batch\_max} = \frac{\text{GPU\_memory} - \text{model\_weights}}{\text{KV\_cache\_per\_request}}
$$

Halving the cache = doubling the batch size = doubling the throughput = **cutting the cost per token in half**. For a production LLM service, KV cache size is directly linked to profitability.

<Callout type="info" title={"This is not an optional optimization"}>
Cache quantization, GQA, MLA, eviction, PagedAttention: these are not marginal optimizations to squeeze out a few percentage points of performance. They are fundamental elements of economic viability. Without them, serving LLMs at scale would be financially impossible.
</Callout>

## TurboQuant: quantizing the KV cache to 3 bits without loss (Google, ICLR 2026)

TurboQuant is a **training-free** compression algorithm developed by Google that quantizes the KV cache to just 3 bits per coordinate, without precision degradation. The process relies on two complementary steps:

1. **PolarQuant**: converts each K/V vector to polar coordinates (radius = magnitude, angles = direction). After a random orthogonal rotation, the distribution of each coordinate becomes an analytically known Beta distribution. You can therefore precompute the optimal quantizer (Lloyd-Max) once and reuse it for all vectors, which eliminates the costly per-block normalization of classical quantizers.

2. **QJL (Quantized Johnson-Lindenstrauss)**: uses 1 additional bit per dimension to compensate for PolarQuant's residual error. The residual is projected via a random Gaussian matrix, and only the sign (+1 or -1) is stored. This single bit is enough to make the dot product estimate **mathematically unbiased**, eliminating systematic error.

In total, 2 bits (PolarQuant) + 1 bit (QJL) = **3 bits per coordinate**, a 5 to 6x memory reduction compared to FP16. On H100 GPUs, TurboQuant achieves up to **8x speedup** for attention logit computation.

Results are validated on **Llama-3.1-8B** and **Mistral-7B** with LongBench, Needle in a Haystack, ZeroSCROLLS, and RULER benchmarks. The compressed cache maintains near-perfect fidelity, including on long-context retrieval tasks.

No training or fine-tuning is needed: TurboQuant applies directly to any existing model in a single pass.

## In summary

The KV Cache is what makes autoregressive inference practical, but it quickly becomes a bottleneck in memory and bandwidth. Compression techniques (quantization, eviction, MLA) and optimization techniques (PagedAttention, prefix caching) are pillars of modern LLM infrastructure. This is a very active area of research, directly tied to the profits and viability of LLM services.
