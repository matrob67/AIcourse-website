## The Problem: You've Consumed the Entire Internet

According to estimates, approximately **10 to 15 trillion tokens** of high-quality text are available on the internet. Yet modern LLMs (Large Language Models) consume 10 to 15 trillion tokens for pre-training.

This means we've reached (or exceeded) the limit of natural data. This is what's called **the data wall**.

Synthetic data (generated by LLMs) represents the most promising path to continue training without a ceiling.

<Callout type="warning" title="The Challenge: Quality vs. Quantity">
Unfiltered synthetic data contains hallucinations, factual errors, and amplified biases. It must be rigorously filtered to improve (instead of degrade) the final model.
</Callout>

## Synthetic Data for Pre-Training

### Generation by Paraphrase

LLMs can rephrase or summarize existing texts, creating high-quality alternatives. This increases diversity without introducing new information.

Example: rephrasing a Wikipedia article 5 times creates 5 different versions of the same content, multiplying the training effect.

### Mathematical Data and Reasoning

**The primary use case**: generating math problems and chain-of-thought reasoning.

**Advantages**:
- Mathematical solutions are verifiable (correctness can be checked automatically)
- LLMs are good at math generation
- Demand for quality math data is very high

### Synthetic Code

Code generation is particularly well-suited because:
- Code is **verifiable** (compilation, tests)
- LLMs excel at code generation
- Demand is enormous

<Callout type="paper" title="Phi and the Textbook Revolution">
Microsoft showed with the Phi series that small models trained on synthetic "textbook"-style data can rival much larger models trained on web data. This changed the paradigm.
</Callout>

## Synthetic Data for Fine-Tuning

### Synthetic Instruction Tuning

The majority of instruction tuning data is **partially or entirely synthetic**:

- **Self-Instruct**: the model generates its own instructions and responses
- **Evol-Instruct**: iterative evolution of instruction complexity
- **WizardLM**: systematic difficulty augmentation

These approaches have replaced human data for large-scale instruction tuning.

### Data Distillation

A powerful model (teacher) generates high-quality responses. A smaller model (student) learns to reproduce them.

Example: Claude generates responses --- LLaMA 7B learns to imitate --- deploy LLaMA 7B.

<Callout type="info" title="Licenses and Restrictions">
Proprietary LLM providers (OpenAI, Anthropic) prohibit in their terms of use training competing models on their outputs. This is why open-source distillation chains (model A to B to C) have become the norm in the community.
</Callout>

### Synthetic RLHF/DPO

Preference data (for alignment) is often generated synthetically:

1. Model generates N responses to a question
2. Judge model (or same model) ranks by quality
3. (Best, less good) pairs for DPO/RLHF

This has drastically reduced the cost of large-scale fine-tuning.

## Generation Techniques

### Targeted Prompting

Carefully designed prompts generate data in a specific format and domain. The prompt's quality directly determines the quality of the produced data --- it's the most important factor in the generation pipeline.

### Persona-Based Generation

Assigning different personas to the model (professor, engineer, historian) to increase diversity of styles, perspectives, and language levels. Each persona produces text with different vocabulary, tone, and structure, enriching the dataset.

### Seed-Based Generation

Starting from real data (questions, topics, excerpts) and asking the model to generate around them. This approach ensures relevance and diversity, as each seed steers generation in a different direction.

## The Danger: Model Collapse

<Callout type="warning" title="Model Collapse: The Degenerative Spiral">
If you successively train on synthetic data (model A --- data --- model B --- data --- model C), with each iteration the distribution degenerates. Model A's biases get amplified in its data, which amplify model B's biases, etc.

Result: loss of diversity, bias amplification, progressive degradation.
</Callout>

## Filtering and Validation

### Absolute Necessity

Unfiltered synthetic data contains:
- Factual hallucinations
- Repetitions and redundancy
- Amplified biases
- Low-quality automatically generated content

Without filtering, it **degrades** model performance.

### Filtering Techniques

- **Automatic verification**: for math, code
- **Perplexity**: filter abnormal examples
- **Diversity**: detect and eliminate duplicates
- **Judge model**: use an LLM to evaluate
- **Consistency**: check internal coherence

## Best Practices

<Callout type="tip" title="Optimal Mix">
Never 100% synthetic data. Typical ratio: 70-80% natural + 20-30% synthetic. Natural data anchors diversity and factuality. Synthetic data fills domain gaps.

Filter rigorously. Verify automatically when possible.
</Callout>

### Indicators to Monitor

- **Diversity**: proportion of unique n-grams, vocabulary entropy
- **Quality**: perplexity measured on a validation set
- **Absence of model collapse**: tracking distribution evolution across iterations
- **Performance**: scores on standard benchmarks (pre-training + fine-tuning)

## Summary

Synthetic data has become essential for LLM training, overcoming the natural data wall. Used correctly (70/30 natural/synthetic mix, rigorous filtering, verification), they offer a powerful lever. Used poorly (100% synthetic, no filtering), they degrade the model via model collapse and amplified bias.

The future: a carefully optimized blend of natural and synthetic data, each compensating for the other's weaknesses.
