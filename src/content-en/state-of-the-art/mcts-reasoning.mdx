## From the Game of Go to Math Problems

In 2016, AlphaGo beat the world champion of Go thanks to Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS). The algorithm is elegant: faced with a gigantic tree of possibilities (Go has more positions than atoms in the universe), MCTS explores intelligently by balancing **exploration** (testing new branches) and **exploitation** (deepening promising branches).

The analogy with reasoning is direct: when an LLM solves a complex problem, each thinking step is a "move," and solving the complete problem is like winning the "game." Reasoning becomes a game, and MCTS knows how to play.

<Callout type="info" title={"The chess analogy"}>
In chess, MCTS explores possible moves, simulates entire games, then focuses its efforts on branches that seem to lead to victory. For LLM reasoning, each "move" is a thinking step ("let's apply the derivative," "let's factor"), and each "complete game" is an end-to-end problem resolution.
</Callout>

## The Four Phases of MCTS

The algorithm repeats four phases in a loop, each iteration refining its understanding of the possibility tree.

### Phase 1: Selection

Starting from the root (the problem posed), you descend through the tree by choosing at each node the most promising child according to the **UCB formula** (Upper Confidence Bound):

$$
UCB(\text{node}) = \frac{Q(\text{node})}{N(\text{node})} + c \cdot \sqrt{\frac{\ln N(\text{parent})}{N(\text{node})}}
$$

- $Q(\text{node})$ = total reward accumulated by this node
- $N(\text{node})$ = number of times this node has been visited
- **First term**: exploitation — we favor nodes that have performed well so far
- **Second term**: exploration — we give a chance to rarely visited nodes

It's this tension between "go toward what works" and "try what we don't know" that makes MCTS so powerful. Without the second term, you'd get stuck in a local optimum. Without the first, you'd explore randomly.

### Phase 2: Expansion

When you reach a leaf (a node with no children), you generate new reasoning steps via the LLM. Concretely, the model proposes multiple possible continuations of the partial reasoning, and each becomes a new node in the tree.

### Phase 3: Simulation (Rollout)

From the new node, you **complete the reasoning** all the way to a final answer. It's like playing a quick game to the end to get an idea of the outcome. The LLM generates the rest of the reasoning greedily or by sampling.

### Phase 4: Backpropagation

The simulation result (correct or incorrect) is propagated back along the path taken. The $Q$ and $N$ statistics of each visited node are updated. Over iterations, nodes that often lead to correct answers build a good reputation.

## Adaptation to LLMs

### State = A Partial Reasoning

Each node of the tree represents reasoning under construction:

> **Root:** Solve $f(x) = 2x^2 + 3x - 5$, find $f'(x)$
> - **Node 1:** Apply differentiation rules
>   - **Node 1.1:** $\frac{d}{dx}(2x^2) = 4x$ &check;
>   - **Node 1.2:** $\frac{d}{dx}(2x^2) = 2x$ &cross;
> - **Node 2:** Use polynomial derivative formula
>   - **Node 2.1:** $f'(x) = 4x + 3$

The tree naturally captures different approaches and their variants, including dead ends.

### Reward Function

This is the crux of the matter. How do you know if a branch is promising? Several options exist:

- **ORM (Outcome Reward Model)**: you only look at whether the final answer is correct (when it's verifiable)
- **PRM (Process Reward Model)**: you evaluate each step individually — a much richer signal
- **Self-evaluation**: the LLM itself rates the quality of each step
- **External verification**: code execution, symbolic computation, proof assistant

### The LLM as Game Policy

The LLM plays the role of the **policy**: it decides which reasoning steps to propose. Temperature controls diversity. Low temperature = conservative reasoning. High temperature = creative but risky explorations.

<Callout type="paper" title="MCTS + PRM: the winning combo">
The PRM (Process Reward Model) provides a reward signal at each step, which guides exploration in a fine-grained way. Clearly bad branches are penalized early. Good branches receive more attention. It's the most powerful combination for mathematical reasoning.
</Callout>

## Why MCTS Outperforms Beam Search

### 1. Exploration-Exploitation Balance

The UCB formula naturally manages this dilemma. Classic beam search simply keeps the K best candidates at each step, which can trap it in a local optimum. MCTS goes back to explore forgotten branches when favorite branches disappoint.

### 2. Adaptive Compute Allocation

Difficult problems automatically receive **more iterations**, because the tree doesn't converge quickly. Easy problems converge rapidly and consume little. Compute distributes itself intelligently.

### 3. Natural Backtracking

If a branch turns out to be bad after simulation, MCTS doesn't blindly pursue it. It redistributes attention to other branches. It's elegant backtracking, without an explicit backtracking mechanism.

## Concrete Use Cases

### rStar (Self-play Reasoning)

rStar combines MCTS with self-play: the model plays both the role of reasoner and evaluator. Each "game" is solving a problem, and the generated data is used to train the model itself. The model improves by playing against itself, exactly as AlphaGo did for Go.

### AlphaProof (DeepMind)

AlphaProof applies MCTS to **formal mathematical proofs**: the LLM generates proof steps, Lean (a proof assistant) verifies each step with certainty, and MCTS explores the space of possible proofs. Result: solving International Mathematical Olympiad problems, a domain long considered out of AI's reach.

<Callout type="tip" title="MCTS vs Tree of Thoughts">
Tree of Thoughts uses simple beam search: good for basic exploration. MCTS is superior when the search space is **immense** and per-step rewards are **available** (via a PRM). If you don't have a reliable PRM, ToT remains an excellent choice, simpler and less expensive.
</Callout>

## The Cost: The Price of Power

### Orders of Magnitude

A typical MCTS for reasoning:

| Metric | Value |
|---|---|
| Iterations per problem | 50-500 |
| Each iteration | 1 expansion + 1 simulation |
| Total LLM calls per problem | 100-1000 |

For comparison, self-consistency requires 10-40 calls. MCTS is 10 to 100 times more. **Much more expensive**, but potentially far more effective on truly difficult problems.

### Parallelization

MCTS simulations can be partially parallelized (leaf parallelization, root parallelization). This reduces latency, but makes exploration slightly less optimal since updates aren't instantaneous.

<Callout type="warning" title="MCTS: research rather than production">
MCTS for LLM reasoning remains a **research** technique more than a production one. The high cost and the need for a good reward signal limit it to high-value problems: advanced math, critical code, formal proofs, complex planning.

For everyday applications, CoT or self-consistency offer a much better quality-to-cost ratio.
</Callout>

## The Convergence of Two Worlds

MCTS applied to LLM reasoning embodies the **fusion** between classical AI planning techniques (MCTS comes from game theory and operations research) and the generative capabilities of modern LLMs. These are not two separate worlds: tree search and text generation complement each other naturally.

As PRMs and base models improve, MCTS for LLMs could unlock even more advanced reasoning capabilities, particularly in domains where verification is automatable.
