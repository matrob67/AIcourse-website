## From Flash Attention 1 to Flash Attention 2

Flash Attention 1 had solved the fundamental problem: making attention linear in memory through tiling. But in terms of raw GPU utilization, there was still a lot of performance left on the table. Flash Attention 1 reached about 50-70% of the GPU's theoretical throughput, meaning a significant portion of compute units remained idle.

Flash Attention 2, published by Tri Dao in 2023, tackles precisely this problem. The goal: approach 70-90% theoretical utilization by optimizing parallelism and work distribution within the GPU.

<ArxivLink id="2307.08691" title="FlashAttention-2: Faster Attention with Better Parallelism and Work Partitioning" />

## Key optimizations

### Reducing non-matmul operations

To understand this optimization, you need to grasp a crucial point about modern GPUs. GPUs like the A100 or H100 have **Tensor Cores**, specialized units that accelerate matrix multiplications (matmul) by a factor of 16x. But all other operations (additions, scalar multiplications, comparisons) run on classic CUDA cores, which are much slower.

<Callout type="info" title="Tensor Cores and matmul">
Imagine a 16-lane highway (the Tensor Cores) reserved for matrix multiplications, and a single-lane country road (the CUDA cores) for everything else. The highway throughput is enormous, but if your computation constantly alternates between the highway and the country road, you waste a ton of time on exits and entrances.
</Callout>

Flash Attention 1 contained rescaling operations (online softmax correction) interleaved with matmuls. These back-and-forth trips between Tensor Cores and CUDA cores wasted time. Flash Attention 2 reorganizes the computation to group rescaling operations and minimize these interruptions in the inner loop.

### Parallelism on the sequence dimension

In Flash Attention 1, the outer loop iterated over K/V blocks and the inner loop over Q blocks. This is natural, but it limits parallelism when the number of attention heads isn't enough to keep all the GPU's streaming multiprocessors busy.

Flash Attention 2 **reverses the loops**: the outer loop iterates over Q blocks, the inner over K/V. This seemingly minor change allows parallelizing on the **sequence** dimension in addition to the head dimension. Result: the GPU is better saturated, especially for models with few attention heads or long sequences.

### Work partitioning between warps

A GPU threadblock contains several **warps** (groups of 32 threads that execute in parallel). How work is distributed between warps has a direct impact on performance.

<Callout type="paper" title="Warp partitioning">
Flash Attention 1 partitioned K and V across warps, which required costly synchronization after each block (warps had to combine their results). Flash Attention 2 partitions Q across warps, allowing each warp to work independently on different rows of the output. Result: no more intra-block synchronization needed, each warp advances at its own pace.
</Callout>

## Performance results

### Speedup compared to Flash Attention 1

| GPU | Speedup FA2 vs FA1 | % of theoretical FLOP |
|-----|--------------------|--------------------|
| A100 (FP16) | 1.5-2x | 50-73% |
| A100 (BF16) | 1.5-2x | 50-73% |

### Comparison with standard attention

For a 2K token sequence on A100, the numbers speak for themselves:

- **Standard PyTorch attention**: ~30 TFLOP/s
- **Flash Attention 1**: ~120 TFLOP/s
- **Flash Attention 2**: ~220 TFLOP/s
- **Theoretical A100**: 312 TFLOP/s (FP16)

Flash Attention 2 is **7 times faster** than standard attention and approaches 70% of the hardware's theoretical maximum.

### Scaling with sequence length

An important point: Flash Attention 2 maintains near-constant throughput in TFLOP/s even for very long sequences, thanks to efficient tiling. Where standard attention explodes in memory and time, FA2 stays stable.

<Callout type="tip" title={"Practical impact on training"}>
Flash Attention 2 reduces end-to-end LLM training time by 20-40% compared to Flash Attention 1, and by 3-5x compared to standard attention. This isn't just an academic optimization: it translates directly into GPU cost savings and the ability to iterate faster on experiments.
</Callout>

## Optimized causal masking support

### Skipping masked blocks

In causal attention (each token only sees previous tokens), roughly half the blocks in the attention matrix are entirely masked (future tokens). Flash Attention 2 detects these blocks and **skips them completely**, offering an additional ~2x speedup for causal attention compared to bidirectional attention.

### Custom masks

Version 2 also supports custom masking patterns (like sliding window attention) via user-defined masking functions, offering significant architectural flexibility.

## Multi-GPU and variable-length support

### Variable-length sequences

In practice, batches contain sequences of different lengths. Flash Attention 2 handles this efficiently via a **packing** (varlen) format: sequences are concatenated end-to-end and start/end indices indicate the boundaries. No more need for costly padding.

### Variable head dimensions

Support for non-standard head dimensions (not just 64 or 128), giving more freedom in model architectural design.

## Ecosystem integration

### PyTorch SDPA

Flash Attention 2 is the default backend for `torch.nn.functional.scaled_dot_product_attention` on compatible GPUs. In practice, if you're using a recent version of PyTorch on an Ampere or newer GPU, you're already benefiting from FA2 transparently.

### Hugging Face

Hugging Face Transformers models automatically enable Flash Attention 2 when the package is installed, via the argument `attn_implementation="flash_attention_2"`. Adoption is virtually transparent for the user.

<Callout type="warning" title="GPU compatibility">
Flash Attention 2 requires NVIDIA GPUs with compute capability >= 8.0: Ampere (A100, A10G, RTX 3090/4090) or Hopper (H100). Older GPUs (V100, T4) cannot use FA2's optimizations, although FA1 remains available for them.
</Callout>

## Improved backward pass

Training is a forward pass followed by a backward pass. Flash Attention 2 applies the same optimization principles to the backward:

- Increased parallelism on the sequence dimension
- Better warp partitioning
- Optimized softmax rematerialization (recompute rather than store)

The backward pass reaches 60-70% of theoretical throughput, compared to 30-40% for Flash Attention 1. This is crucial since the backward pass is often the training bottleneck.

## Impact and adoption

Flash Attention 2 has become a foundational and nearly universal component in training and inference of modern LLMs. Its massive adoption has contributed to extending context windows (from 2K to 128K+ tokens), significantly reducing training costs, and generally accelerating NLP research.

The hardware-aware optimizations it embodies -- designing algorithms based on specific hardware capabilities -- have become an essential design paradigm in deep learning.

<Callout type="info" title="Toward Flash Attention 3">
Flash Attention 2 laid the groundwork for the optimizations exploited by Flash Attention 3 on Hopper GPUs (H100/H200), which introduce new hardware features like asynchronous pipelining and FP8 support. These advances are detailed in the next section.
</Callout>
