## The all-or-nothing problem

MQA is great for efficiency, but sharing **a single** K/V pair across all heads might be too radical. It's like having one waiter for an entire restaurant -- quick to coordinate, but the service suffers when tables have different needs.

MHA is perfect for quality, but its gigantic KV cache limits what you can do in practice. Each head has its own set of keys and values, it's like a dedicated waiter per table -- impeccable service, but the payroll explodes.

What if you didn't have to choose? What if you could have **as many K/V groups as you want** -- from 1 (MQA) to H (MHA), including any value in between?

That's **Grouped Query Attention (GQA)**, introduced by the Google team, and now adopted by virtually all modern open-source LLMs.

## The GQA principle

### Grouping the heads

Instead of one K/V pair per head (MHA) or one K/V pair for everyone (MQA), GQA creates **G groups**, each group sharing a K/V pair:

$$
Q_i = X \cdot W_i^Q \quad \text{(DISTINCT: each head has its own query)}
$$
$$
K_g = X \cdot W_g^K \quad \text{(G projections: one per group)}
$$
$$
V_g = X \cdot W_g^V \quad \text{(G projections: one per group)}
$$

The H heads are divided into G groups of H/G heads each. All heads within the same group use the same K and V, but keep distinct queries. Each head therefore asks its own "question" to the context, but the context is represented by a reduced number of K/V perspectives.

### The MHA -> GQA -> MQA continuum

GQA elegantly unifies MHA and MQA as extreme cases of the same mechanism:

- **G = H**: each group contains exactly one head -> this is **standard MHA**
- **G = 1**: a single group contains all heads -> this is **MQA**
- **1 < G < H**: intermediate configuration -> this is **GQA**

You turn a single knob (the number of groups G) to navigate the entire quality-efficiency spectrum.

<Callout type="info" title="The most common configuration">
The dominant configuration in modern models is **G = H/8** -- that is, 8 groups for 64 heads (each group contains 8 heads sharing the same K/V). The KV cache is reduced by a factor of 8 compared to MHA, while maintaining 8 distinct K/V sets to capture different aspects of the context. This is the choice of LLaMA 2 70B, Mistral 7B, and many other reference models.
</Callout>

## KV cache reduction: the real numbers

For a model with H = 64 heads, d_k = 128, and L context tokens:

| Configuration | G | KV cache per layer per token | Reduction vs MHA |
|--------------|---|-------------------------------|------------------|
| MHA | 64 | 2 x 64 x 128 = 16,384 elements | 1x |
| GQA-8 | 8 | 2 x 8 x 128 = 2,048 elements | **8x** |
| GQA-4 | 4 | 2 x 4 x 128 = 1,024 elements | **16x** |
| MQA | 1 | 2 x 1 x 128 = 256 elements | **64x** |

Let's put this in concrete perspective: with a context of 128,000 tokens and 80 layers (a GPT-4-type model), the difference between MHA and GQA-8 represents several hundred gigabytes of VRAM. In practice, that's the difference between needing 4 A100 GPUs or a single one -- a direct impact on deployment cost.

<Callout type="tip" title="How to choose G?">
In practice, G = 8 offers an excellent balance for large models: 8x KV cache reduction with nearly imperceptible quality loss on benchmarks. For very memory-constrained deployments (mobile, edge), G = 4 or G = 2 are viable with moderate degradation. G = 1 (MQA) remains useful for the most extreme cases where every megabyte counts. The rule of thumb: start with G = 8 and reduce only if memory requires it.
</Callout>

## Converting MHA -> GQA: reusing existing models

One of GQA's most important practical advantages: you can **convert an existing MHA model** to GQA without retraining from scratch. This is crucial when you've invested millions of dollars in an MHA training run.

### The uptrained conversion process

*Uptrained conversion* refers to converting an already-trained MHA model to GQA, followed by a short retraining -- you don't start from scratch.

1. **Group the heads**: distribute the H heads into G groups (consecutive groups, or based on a similarity heuristic)
2. **Average the K/V**: within each group, compute the average of the K and V weight matrices of the H/G constituent heads
3. **Fine-tune**: train the converted model on ~5% of the original tokens to recover performance

<Callout type="paper" title="Conversion results">
The uptrained conversion of LLaMA 65B (MHA) to GQA-8 with only 5% of the original tokens in fine-tuning recovers **over 99%** of the original performance, while multiplying inference throughput by ~5. The return on investment is enormous: a few hours of fine-tuning versus months of full training.
</Callout>

### Averaging vs selection

Two strategies for forming the group's K/V:
- **Averaging**: the group's K projection is the average of the K projections of the constituent heads. This approach preserves information from all original heads and generally gives better results.
- **Selection**: you choose a representative head per group (typically the median). Simpler to implement, slightly lower quality.

## The industry standard: who uses GQA?

GQA has become the **de facto standard** for open-source LLMs since mid-2023. The list speaks for itself:

- **LLaMA 2 70B** (Meta): first major model to popularize GQA (G = 8 for H = 64)
- **LLaMA 3** (Meta): GQA across the entire family, from 8B to 405B
- **Mistral 7B**: GQA with G = 8 for H = 32 (4 K/V heads for 32 Q heads)
- **Gemma** (Google): GQA across all sizes
- **Qwen 2** (Alibaba): GQA as the standard configuration
- **Command R** (Cohere): GQA for inference efficiency

<Callout type="info" title="Why this massive adoption?">
GQA is the first mechanism to offer a quality-efficiency trade-off good enough to be adopted without hesitation by teams targeting top performance. MQA was too aggressive -- the quality loss became visible on demanding tasks. GQA-8 is practically indistinguishable from MHA on benchmarks, while dividing the KV cache by 8. It's the rare case where you can have your cake and (almost) eat it too.
</Callout>

## Why does GQA work so well?

### K/V redundancy

Empirical analyses reveal that K/V projections from different heads are often **highly correlated** with each other. Concretely, if you look at the K weight matrices of 8 consecutive heads, they look very similar -- they capture essentially the same semantic information with slight variations. Grouping them by averaging therefore only destroys a small fraction of useful information.

### Queries carry the diversity

The real diversity of attention patterns comes from the **queries**: different heads ask different questions about the same content. One head asks "what's the grammatical subject?", another "what's the main verb?", a third "is there a cause-and-effect relationship?". These questions remain entirely distinct per head in GQA -- only the K/V "dictionary" they search is shared within a group.

### Sufficient residual capacity

With G = 8 K/V groups, the model still has 8 distinct representations of the context. That's enough to capture the different aspects needed: syntax, semantics, coreference, logical relationships, etc. The real loss only becomes noticeable below G = 4, on tasks that require multiple simultaneous perspectives on the context.


## Combinations with other optimizations

GQA isn't an isolated technique -- it combines naturally with other optimizations, and the gains multiply:

**GQA + Flash Attention**: perfectly compatible. Reducing the number of K/V heads reduces the size of manipulated tensors, which amplifies Flash Attention's gains by reducing memory transfers.

**GQA + KV cache quantization**: quantizing the KV cache in INT8 or INT4 further multiplies the reduction. The two techniques are orthogonal: GQA reduces the number of K/V groups, quantization reduces the precision of each value. Together, you can achieve 32x or more reduction.

**GQA + Sliding Window Attention**: Mistral combines GQA with a sliding context window. Double lever: fewer K/V groups and K/V history truncated to the window size.

<Callout type="warning" title="Beware of very small G">
With G = 1 or G = 2, the loss of K/V diversity can become significant, particularly for tasks requiring fine attention on multiple distinct aspects of the context simultaneously -- complex multi-step reasoning, questions about long documents with many varied details, or translation between structurally very different languages.
</Callout>

## Comparative performance

| Model | Attention | Relative quality | Relative throughput |
|--------|-----------|-----------------|---------------|
| LLaMA 65B (MHA) | MHA | 100% | 1x |
| LLaMA 65B -> GQA-8 | GQA-8 | ~99.5% | ~5x |
| LLaMA 65B -> MQA | MQA | ~98.5% | ~7x |

GQA-8's trade-off is crystal clear: 0.5% less quality for 5 times more throughput. For the vast majority of applications, that's a highly profitable exchange -- you lose half a point on benchmarks to serve 5 times more users with the same hardware.

## Conclusion

Grouped Query Attention represents a pragmatic and decisive advance for LLM inference optimization. By offering a continuum between MHA (maximum quality) and MQA (maximum efficiency), it allows practitioners to choose exactly the optimal trade-off for their use case. Its near-universal adoption confirms that GQA has become a standard component of every modern Transformer architecture -- as fundamental as layer normalization or residual connections.
