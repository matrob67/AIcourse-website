## The problem S4 can't solve

Imagine this simple test: you're given the sequence `a1 b2 c3 COPY 2`, and you need to answer `b`.

A human understands instantly: "COPY 2" means copy the second element of the list. To do this, you need to **decide in real time** which information to store in memory --- the number 2 retroactively changes the importance of each previous element.

S4, however, applies the same fixed filter to every token, without considering the content. It can't "understand" that the word COPY changes the game. S4 excels on music, time series, regular signals --- but it fails on this kind of task where the **context changes the rules**.

That's the problem **Mamba** solves.

## Time-invariant SSMs: their fundamental limitation

S4 is an **LTI** (Linear Time-Invariant) system, meaning the same matrices A, B, C apply at every time step, regardless of the input content. "Time-invariant" means these matrices are fixed once trained. Every token passes through the same filter, regardless of its meaning in context.

The most telling analogy: it's an audio amplifier with a fixed volume. Whether the music is soft or loud, whether the passage is a crucial violin solo or a silence between movements, the filter treats everything the same way. There's no way to "turn up the volume" when something important comes along.

<Callout type="info" title="Selectivity as an intelligent compression mechanism">
The fundamental idea behind Mamba: selectivity in SSMs is analogous to the **LSTM's forget gate**. It allows the model to dynamically decide, at each token, what to store in its hidden state and what to ignore. An important keyword? Engrave it in memory. A predictable function word? Let it pass without cluttering the state. It's adaptive rather than uniform compression.
</Callout>

## The Mamba architecture: making parameters input-dependent

The big idea behind Mamba, published by Albert Gu and Tri Dao in December 2023, is simple to state: make certain SSM parameters **dependent on the current token**. Instead of a fixed filter, the model adjusts its behavior at each time step based on what it sees.

### The three selective parameters

**Delta (the discretization step)**: this is the most intuitive parameter. It controls how fast the state evolves. A large delta = the model gives a lot of importance to the current token and "forgets" the past quickly. A small delta = the model preserves its current state and ignores the input. It's the slider between "absorb" and "ignore."

$$
\Delta = \text{softplus}(\text{Linear}(x))
$$

The softplus function ($\log(1 + e^x)$) guarantees that $\Delta > 0$: a negative time step has no physical meaning --- it's a "duration," always positive.

**B (input matrix --- write gate)**: controls **what to write** into the hidden state. In the recurrence equation, B multiplies the input $x_k$ to determine how the current token modifies the state. Each token can influence the state differently.

$$
B = \text{Linear}(x)
$$

**C (output matrix --- read gate)**: controls **what to read** from the hidden state. In the recurrence equation, C multiplies the state $h_k$ to determine which information to extract to the output. Each token can "read" the state differently.

$$
C = \text{Linear}(x)
$$

Although B and C have the same form (linear projection of $x$), they play opposite roles: B is the write gate (input -> state) while C is the read gate (state -> output). They are applied at different places in the recurrence equation and learn distinct weights.

These three projections are computed from the current token `x` --- hence the name "selectivity." The model adapts its filter at each position.

<Callout type="paper" title="Connection with LSTM">
When `delta` tends toward infinity, the model completely ignores the previous state and only sees the current token --- like a fully open forget gate. When `delta` tends toward 0, it ignores the token and preserves its state intact --- fully closed forget gate. This is exactly the behavior of the LSTM forget gate, but operating in a continuous, high-dimensional state space rather than on a discrete vector.
</Callout>

## The technical problem: how to parallelize without FFT?

Selectivity has a major technical cost. S4 could speed up via FFT because its convolution kernel was **fixed** for the entire sequence --- one kernel, one FFT. With Mamba, the kernel changes at every position (it depends on the token). You can no longer unroll the recurrence into a global convolution.

The recurrence becomes:

$$
h_k = \bar{A}_k \cdot h_{k-1} + \bar{B}_k \cdot x_k
$$

The "bar" notation designates the **discretized** versions of A and B: the continuous matrices are transformed into discrete matrices suited for token-by-token processing, recomputed at each step using the current $\Delta_k$. The exact discretization gives:

$$
\bar{A}_k = \exp(\Delta_k A), \quad \bar{B}_k = (\Delta_k A)^{-1}(\exp(\Delta_k A) - I) \cdot \Delta_k B
$$

Since $\bar{A}_k$ and $\bar{B}_k$ change at each step --- it's an **input-dependent recurrence**, impossible to reformulate as a simple convolution.

### The solution: parallel scan

Mamba uses a **parallel associative scan** --- a parallel algorithmic technique known since the 1980s, originally designed to compute prefix sums on parallel machines.

The key observation: even though `h_k` depends on `h_(k-1)`, this recurrence is **associative** --- it can be decomposed into independent sub-problems that are recombined afterward. All states are computed in O(L) total work, with a depth of only O(log L) on GPU. In practice, it's like a binary tree: you combine pairs, then quadruplets, then octets, until covering the entire sequence.

It's not as fast as S4's FFT, but it's incomparably better than pure sequential recurrence.

## Hardware optimizations: Tri Dao's touch

Tri Dao --- the same researcher who developed Flash Attention --- designed a **fused CUDA kernel** for Mamba's selective scan. The philosophy is identical to Flash Attention: minimize memory transfers, not FLOPs.

<Callout type="tip" title="Hardware-aware implementation">
Mamba's implementation keeps the hidden state in the GPU's **SRAM** (ultra-fast memory of a few MB, integrated directly into the streaming processors) instead of writing it to HBM (the GPU's main memory, larger but ~10x slower). The projections for B, C, and delta are computed in the same kernel, without materializing intermediate states in main memory. Result: the bottleneck shifts from memory bandwidth to pure computation --- exactly what you want.
</Callout>


### The complete Mamba block

A Mamba block stacks six operations in order:

1. Linear expansion projection (factor 2 --- doubles the dimension for more capacity)
2. Causal 1D convolution (kernel size 4 --- captures a few tokens of local context)
3. SiLU activation (smooth non-linear function, similar to GELU)
4. Selective SSM (Mamba's core --- the adaptive recurrence)
5. Multiplicative gate (controls information flow, like in Gated MLPs)
6. Projection back to the original dimension

This architecture draws direct inspiration from the Gated MLP block of modern Transformers (LLaMA, Mistral). The only structural difference: the selective SSM replaces attention.

## Performance: Mamba vs Transformers

### Quality on language benchmarks

Mamba 3B (3 billion parameters) achieves performance comparable to same-size Transformers on standard language benchmarks. On certain reasoning tasks involving long sequences, it surpasses them --- its fixed state doesn't suffer from the "lost in the middle" problems of long Transformer contexts.

### The major advantage: inference

| Metric | Transformer | Mamba |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| Inference memory | KV cache that grows with L | Fixed state, regardless of length |
| Cost per token | O(L) (reading KV cache) | O(1) (fixed state update) |
| Throughput on long context | Decreases with length | Stays constant |

<Callout type="info" title={"KV cache versus fixed state"}>
A Transformer with a 100,000-token context stores 100,000 key and value vectors per layer --- gigabytes of memory that grow with each generated token. Mamba stores a single fixed-size state vector (a few thousand dimensions), whether the context is 100 or 1,000,000 tokens. In theory, Mamba can process **infinitely long** sequences without any increase in inference memory.
</Callout>

### Mamba's limitations

Mamba is not a universal solution. Studies have identified specific weaknesses:

- **Precise recall**: retrieving exact information (a name, a date) mentioned 50,000 tokens earlier is difficult --- the state compresses information in a *lossy* way. It's like trying to find an exact sentence in a novel summary: the idea is there, the exact words are not.
- **In-context learning (few-shot)**: giving 5 examples in the prompt and asking the model to imitate the pattern --- Transformers are significantly better, because they "see" the examples directly in their KV cache.
- **Ecosystem**: fewer optimized inference tools, fewer pre-trained models, smaller community than the Transformer ecosystem.

## Mamba's legacy

Mamba demonstrated something many believed impossible: a model **without an attention mechanism** can be competitive on large-scale language tasks. Before December 2023, the consensus was that attention was irreplaceable for state-of-the-art NLP.

This impact triggered a wave of innovation: Mamba-2 (faster thanks to SSM-attention unification), Jamba (SSM+attention hybrid at production scale), RWKV-6 (adopting Mamba's selectivity), and applications in genomics, audio, and video.

<Callout type="tip" title="Mamba in practice">
Pre-trained Mamba models up to 2.8B parameters are available as open source. The architecture has been adopted and adapted by many teams, giving rise to Mamba-2 (faster), Jamba (hybrid with attention, 52B parameters), and specialized variants for genomics, long-duration audio, and protein modeling.
</Callout>
