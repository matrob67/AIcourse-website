## Introduction: the privileged directions

When a matrix acts on a vector, it transforms it. In general, the vector changes both its **direction** and **length**. But for certain special vectors, the matrix only **stretches or compresses** them -- without changing their direction.

These special vectors are the **eigenvectors**. The corresponding stretching factor is the **eigenvalue**. Together, they reveal the deep structure of a transformation.

## The intuition: a matrix as a transformation

Imagine a matrix $\mathbf{A}$ acting on all vectors in the plane. Most vectors are deformed into a new direction. But there exist "natural" directions of the transformation -- axes along which the matrix only pushes or pulls.

Concrete analogy: think of a rubber band being stretched. The rubber band deforms in all directions, but there's a principal stretching axis (the direction in which it elongates the most) and a perpendicular axis (in which it contracts). These axes are the eigenvectors of the transformation.

Another analogy: in a movie theater, sound vibrations have **natural modes** -- frequencies at which the room naturally resonates. Eigenvectors are the same thing: the "natural modes" of a matrix transformation.

## Formal definition

For a square matrix $\mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$, a nonzero vector $\mathbf{v}$ is an **eigenvector** if:

$$
\mathbf{A}\mathbf{v} = \lambda \mathbf{v}
$$

Where $\lambda$ is a scalar called the **eigenvalue** associated with $\mathbf{v}$.

In other words: applying $\mathbf{A}$ to $\mathbf{v}$ simply amounts to multiplying $\mathbf{v}$ by $\lambda$. The direction is preserved, only the magnitude changes.

### What the eigenvalue tells you

- $\lambda > 1$: the eigenvector is **stretched** (the transformation amplifies in this direction)
- $0 < \lambda < 1$: the eigenvector is **compressed**
- $\lambda = 1$: the eigenvector is **unchanged**
- $\lambda = 0$: the eigenvector is **crushed** to zero (information in this direction is lost)
- $\lambda < 0$: the eigenvector is **reversed** (and stretched/compressed according to $|\lambda|$)

### Concrete example

Take the matrix:

$$
\mathbf{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}
$$

You can verify that $\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}$ is an eigenvector with $\lambda_1 = 3$:

$$
\begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} = 3 \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}
$$

And $\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}$ is an eigenvector with $\lambda_2 = 2$:

$$
\begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ -2 \end{pmatrix} = 2 \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}
$$

## How to find them (the concept)

You start from the equation $\mathbf{A}\mathbf{v} = \lambda \mathbf{v}$ and rewrite:

$$
(\mathbf{A} - \lambda \mathbf{I})\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}
$$

For a nonzero $\mathbf{v}$ to exist, the matrix $(\mathbf{A} - \lambda \mathbf{I})$ must be singular, so:

$$
\det(\mathbf{A} - \lambda \mathbf{I}) = 0
$$

This is the **characteristic polynomial**. Its roots are the eigenvalues. An $n \times n$ matrix has at most $n$ eigenvalues (potentially complex).

In practice, you never solve this polynomial by hand for large matrices. You use iterative numerical algorithms (QR, Lanczos, etc.) implemented in NumPy, SciPy, or LAPACK.

## The eigendecomposition

If a matrix $\mathbf{A}$ has $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors, you can write:

$$
\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{V} \mathbf{\Lambda} \mathbf{V}^{-1}
$$

Where:
- $\mathbf{V}$ is the matrix whose columns are the eigenvectors
- $\mathbf{\Lambda}$ is the diagonal matrix of eigenvalues

This decomposition says: the transformation $\mathbf{A}$ is equivalent to (1) switching to the eigenvector basis, (2) stretching each axis by its eigenvalue, (3) switching back to the original basis.

For **symmetric matrices** (like covariance matrices), the eigenvectors are **orthogonal** and the decomposition becomes $\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{V}\mathbf{\Lambda}\mathbf{V}^T$. It's cleaner and numerically more stable.

## Applications in machine learning

### PCA: dimensionality reduction

**Principal Component Analysis** (PCA) is the most direct application of eigenvalues in ML.

The principle: you have high-dimensional data (say 1,000 features) and you want to project it into low dimensions (say 50) while losing minimal information.

How? You compute the **covariance matrix** of the data $\mathbf{C} = \frac{1}{n}\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{X}$ (after centering), then its eigenvectors. The eigenvectors associated with the **largest eigenvalues** are the directions of **maximum variance** -- the most "informative" directions.

Projecting onto these few eigenvectors means keeping the essentials while drastically reducing dimensionality. If the first 50 eigenvalues capture 95% of the total variance, the other 950 dimensions are noise.

### Spectral analysis of graphs

Social networks, molecules, and knowledge graphs are represented by **adjacency matrices** or **graph Laplacians**. The eigenvectors of these matrices reveal the community structure of the graph.

**Graph Neural Networks** (GNNs) use this spectral theory. Spectral filters on graphs are defined in terms of eigenvalues of the Laplacian.

### Training stability

The eigenvalues of the **Hessian matrix** (second derivatives of the loss) give information about the loss landscape:

- Very large eigenvalues in the Hessian indicate directions of strong curvature -- the gradient changes rapidly, making optimization unstable.
- The **ratio** between the largest and smallest eigenvalue (the **condition number**) measures how ill-conditioned the optimization problem is. A high condition number means gradient descent converges slowly.

Techniques like **normalization** (BatchNorm, LayerNorm) improve the conditioning of the Hessian, which stabilizes training.

### Markov models and PageRank

Google's PageRank algorithm models the web as a Markov chain. The ranking of pages is given by the **dominant eigenvector** of the transition matrix -- the one associated with the largest eigenvalue.

## Spectrum of a matrix

The set of all eigenvalues of a matrix is called its **spectrum**. Analyzing the spectrum gives a global view of the transformation:

- **Spectral radius**: the largest eigenvalue in absolute value, $\rho(\mathbf{A}) = \max|\lambda_i|$. If $\rho < 1$, the transformation contracts, which guarantees convergence of certain iterative algorithms.
- **Trace**: the sum of eigenvalues equals the trace of the matrix, $\text{tr}(\mathbf{A}) = \sum \lambda_i$.
- **Determinant**: the product of eigenvalues equals the determinant, $\det(\mathbf{A}) = \prod \lambda_i$.

## Why this matters for AI

Eigenvalues and eigenvectors are not an abstract concept reserved for mathematicians. They are at the heart of very concrete ML techniques:

- **PCA** for dimensionality reduction and visualization
- **Spectral analysis** for graphs and GNNs
- **Training diagnostics** via the Hessian spectrum
- **Model compression** by identifying "useless" directions

When you understand eigenvalues, you understand how a transformation preserves or destroys information. And in ML, everything is a transformation.
