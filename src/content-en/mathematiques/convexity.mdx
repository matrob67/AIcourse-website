## Optimization: finding the bottom of the valley

Training a machine learning model amounts to solving an optimization problem: finding the parameters $\theta$ that minimize a loss function $L(\theta)$. The shape of this loss function determines the difficulty of the problem. And convexity is the property that distinguishes "easy" problems from "hard" ones.

## Convex functions: the bowl

### Definition

A function $f$ is convex if, for all points $x$ and $y$ and any $t \in [0, 1]$:

$$f(tx + (1-t)y) \leq t \cdot f(x) + (1-t) \cdot f(y)$$

In geometric terms, this means that the line segment connecting any two points on the graph of $f$ always stays above the curve. In other words, the function "curves upward," like the inside of a bowl.

### Analogy: the bowl

Imagine a marble in a bowl. No matter where you drop it, it will always end up at the bottom. That's exactly the key property of convex functions: every local minimum is a global minimum. There's only one bottom, and gradient descent inevitably leads there.

### Examples of convex functions

- $f(x) = x^2$ (parabola)
- $f(x) = |x|$ (absolute value)
- $f(x) = e^x$ (exponential)
- $f(\mathbf{x}) = \|\mathbf{x}\|^2$ (squared norm)
- MSE for linear regression

### Fundamental property

For a differentiable convex function, the optimality condition is simple:

$$\nabla f(x^*) = 0 \implies x^* \text{ is a global minimum}$$

If you find a point where the gradient is zero, you can be certain it's the global minimum. No ambiguity, no trap -- that's what makes convex optimization so comfortable.

## Non-convex functions: the mountainous landscape

### The reality of deep learning

Loss functions of neural networks are massively non-convex. As soon as you introduce nonlinearities (ReLU, sigmoid, tanh) and multiple layers, the loss surface becomes a complex landscape with multiple valleys, hills, and passes.

### Analogy: the mountain

Imagine a blindfolded hiker in a mountain range, looking for the lowest point. By only following the descending slope, they might end up in a valley that isn't the deepest -- that's a local minimum, and they have no way of knowing that something better exists elsewhere.

### Types of critical points

In high dimensions, the points where the gradient is zero fall into three categories:

- **Local minimum**: the function goes up in all directions
- **Local maximum**: the function goes down in all directions
- **Saddle point**: the function goes up in some directions and down in others

A saddle point resembles a horse saddle: a minimum in the front-back direction but a maximum in the left-right direction.

## Why networks work despite non-convexity

This is the big question of deep learning. If the loss is non-convex, with potentially billions of local minima, how does SGD (Stochastic Gradient Descent) find good solutions? Several elements of the answer:

### Saddle points dominate

In very high dimensions (millions or billions of parameters), true local minima are extremely rare. Why? For a critical point to be a local minimum, curvature must be positive in all directions simultaneously. With millions of directions, this is statistically improbable.

The vast majority of critical points are saddle points. And SGD naturally traverses them thanks to stochastic noise: the noisy gradient pushes the trajectory away from saddle points, even when the average gradient is close to zero.

### Attraction basins are wide

Good solutions are not isolated, narrow minima, but wide flat basins. Research shows that these flat basins correspond to solutions that generalize well. The intuition: a wide minimum is "robust" to parameter perturbations, just as a generalizing solution is robust to data perturbations.

### Overparameterization helps

A network with more parameters than data has many zero-loss solutions. These solutions form connected manifolds in parameter space. SGD doesn't have to find an isolated point; it just has to reach this manifold, which is much easier.

### SGD noise is beneficial

The mini-batch noise in SGD acts as a natural regularizer. It prevents convergence to narrow, "sharp" minima (which overfit) and favors wide, flat minima (which generalize).

## The Hessian and convexity

### The Hessian

The Hessian $\mathbf{H}$ is the matrix of second derivatives:

$$H_{ij} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_i \partial x_j}$$

It captures the curvature of the function in each direction. In a sense, if the gradient tells you "which direction it's tilting," the Hessian tells you "how curved the terrain is" -- and it's this curvature that distinguishes minima from maxima and saddle points.

### Link with convexity

- $f$ is convex if and only if $\mathbf{H}$ is positive semi-definite everywhere (all eigenvalues $\geq 0$)
- At a critical point $\nabla f = 0$:
  - All eigenvalues $> 0$: local minimum
  - All eigenvalues $< 0$: local maximum
  - Mixed sign eigenvalues: saddle point

### In practice

Computing the full Hessian of a neural network is impossible: for $n$ parameters, it's an $n \times n$ matrix that would occupy petabytes of memory. But you can efficiently compute the Hessian-vector product, which is enough for some second-order optimization methods.

## Local convexity and "nearly convex" functions

### Strong convexity

A function is $\mu$-strongly convex if:

$$f(y) \geq f(x) + \nabla f(x)^\top (y - x) + \frac{\mu}{2} \|y - x\|^2$$

The term $\frac{\mu}{2}\|y - x\|^2$ guarantees a minimum curvature in all directions. The larger $\mu$, the more "curved" the function and the faster gradient descent converges to the minimum. In AI, L2 regularization makes the loss strongly convex around the minimum, which stabilizes training.

### Smoothness

A function is $L$-smooth if its gradient doesn't vary too fast:

$$\|\nabla f(x) - \nabla f(y)\| \leq L \|x - y\|$$

It's a bound on the maximum curvature. Concretely, for an $L$-smooth function, you know gradient descent converges as long as the learning rate satisfies $\alpha \leq 1/L$. That's why a learning rate that's too large makes training diverge: it exceeds the limit imposed by the curvature of the loss.

### The ratio $\kappa = L/\mu$

The condition number $\kappa$ measures the "imbalance" of curvature:
- $\kappa$ close to 1: the function is well-conditioned, convergence is fast
- $\kappa$ large: the function is ill-conditioned, optimization is slow (narrow, elongated valleys)

This is exactly what motivates the use of adaptive optimizers like Adam, which adapt to the local curvature in each direction.

## Key takeaways

- A convex function has a unique global minimum: optimization is "easy"
- Neural network loss functions are non-convex: multiple local minima and saddle points
- In high dimensions, saddle points are far more common than local minima
- SGD works despite non-convexity thanks to stochastic noise, overparameterization, and wide attraction basins
- The Hessian captures local curvature and determines the nature of critical points
- The condition number $\kappa$ quantifies the difficulty of optimization
