## Distributions: the vocabulary of models

A machine learning model, at its core, is a machine that produces probability distributions. A classifier produces a distribution over classes. An image generator produces a distribution over pixels. A language model produces a distribution over tokens. Understanding the fundamental distributions means understanding the language in which models express themselves.

## Random variables: discrete vs continuous

Before talking about distributions, we need to distinguish two types of random variables.

### Discrete variable

It takes a finite or countable number of values. Examples: the result of a die roll (1 to 6), the class predicted by a classifier (cat, dog, bird), the next token in a vocabulary of 50,000 words.

It's described by a probability mass function: $P(X = x)$ for each possible value $x$, with $\sum_x P(X = x) = 1$.

### Continuous variable

It takes values in a real interval. Examples: temperature, pixel value, a weight in a neural network.

It's described by a probability density function $f(x)$, with $\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} f(x) \, dx = 1$. Be careful: $f(x)$ is not a probability! It can exceed 1. It's the integral over an interval that gives a probability.

## Bernoulli distribution

### The coin flip

The simplest distribution: two possible outcomes, with probability $p$ for "success" and $1-p$ for "failure."

$$P(X = 1) = p, \quad P(X = 0) = 1 - p$$

We write $X \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p)$.

### In ML: binary classification

Each output neuron of a binary classifier models a Bernoulli. The sigmoid function $\sigma(z) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-z}}$ transforms a logit $z$ into a parameter $p \in [0, 1]$:

$$P(y = 1 | x) = \sigma(f_\theta(x))$$

The binary cross-entropy loss follows directly from the log-likelihood of a Bernoulli.

## Categorical distribution

### The N-sided die

A generalization of the Bernoulli to $K$ categories. We have a probability vector $(p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_K)$ with $\sum_{k=1}^K p_k = 1$.

$$P(X = k) = p_k$$

### In ML: multi-class classification

An ImageNet classifier that distinguishes 1,000 classes produces a categorical distribution at each prediction. A language model with a vocabulary of 50,000 tokens produces a categorical distribution with 50,000 categories for each position.

The cross-entropy loss for multi-class classification is exactly the negative log-likelihood of a categorical distribution.

## The softmax function

### Turning logits into probabilities

The output layers of a network produce logits: arbitrary real numbers $z_1, z_2, \ldots, z_K$. To turn them into a valid categorical distribution, we need positive numbers that sum to 1. That's the role of softmax:

$$\text{softmax}(z_i) = \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_{j=1}^K e^{z_j}}$$

Each logit is exponentiated (making it positive), then normalized by the sum (ensuring the total equals 1).

### Important properties

- **Preserves order**: if $z_i > z_j$, then $\text{softmax}(z_i) > \text{softmax}(z_j)$
- **Sensitive to gaps**: the exponential amplifies differences. A logit slightly larger than the others "wins" a disproportionate share of the probability
- **Translation invariance**: $\text{softmax}(z + c) = \text{softmax}(z)$ for any constant $c$ (useful for numerical stability)

### Temperature

By dividing the logits by a factor $T$ (the temperature) before softmax, you control the "shape" of the distribution:

$$\text{softmax}(z_i / T) = \frac{e^{z_i / T}}{\sum_{j=1}^K e^{z_j / T}}$$

- $T \to 0$: the distribution becomes one-hot (argmax), the model is "confident"
- $T = 1$: default behavior
- $T \to \infty$: the distribution tends toward uniform, the model is "undecided"

This parameter is ubiquitous in text generation with LLMs and in knowledge distillation.

## Gaussian (normal) distribution

### The bell curve

The most important distribution in statistics and ML. It's defined by two parameters: the mean $\mu$ and the variance $\sigma^2$:

$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp\left(-\frac{(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$

We write $X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$.

### Why it's everywhere

- **Central limit theorem**: the sum of many independent random variables tends toward a Gaussian, regardless of their original distribution. Since real-world data often results from multiple factors, it often approximately follows a normal distribution.
- **Maximum entropy**: among all distributions with a given mean and variance, the Gaussian has the highest entropy. It's the most "neutral" choice when you don't know anything else.
- **Algebraic properties**: sum of Gaussians = Gaussian. Conditioning a Gaussian = Gaussian. This enormously simplifies calculations.

### In ML

- Network weights are often initialized from a Gaussian
- L2 regularization corresponds to a Gaussian prior on the weights
- VAEs assume a Gaussian prior in the latent space
- The noise added in diffusion models is Gaussian

## Multivariate Gaussian

### When you have multiple dimensions

For a random vector $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^d$, the multivariate Gaussian is parameterized by a mean vector $\boldsymbol{\mu} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ and a covariance matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma} \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$:

$$f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{d/2} |\boldsymbol{\Sigma}|^{1/2}} \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2} (\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})^\top \boldsymbol{\Sigma}^{-1} (\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})\right)$$

### What the covariance matrix contains

- **Diagonal**: the variances of each dimension (how much each variable varies)
- **Off-diagonal**: the covariances between dimensions (how the variables vary together)

If $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ is diagonal, the dimensions are independent. Otherwise, they are correlated, and the "bell" is tilted or stretched in certain directions.

### In ML

- **PCA**: finding the directions of maximum variance means diagonalizing the covariance matrix
- **VAE**: the encoder produces $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ and $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ (often diagonal) for each point in latent space
- **Gaussian Mixture Models**: each component is a multivariate Gaussian
- **Diffusion models**: the noising and denoising process operates in a multivariate Gaussian space

## Summary: which distribution for which use

| Distribution | Type | Parameters | ML use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bernoulli | Discrete (2 values) | $p$ | Binary classification, dropout |
| Categorical | Discrete ($K$ values) | $(p_1, \ldots, p_K)$ | Multi-class classification, LLM |
| Gaussian | Continuous | $\mu, \sigma^2$ | Weight initialization, VAE, diffusion |
| Multivariate Gaussian | Continuous ($d$ dim.) | $\boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ | PCA, latent space, GMM |

## Key takeaways

- ML models produce and manipulate probability distributions
- Bernoulli for binary, categorical for multi-class, Gaussian for continuous
- Softmax is the bridge between raw logits and a valid categorical distribution
- Temperature controls the "certainty" of the softmax distribution
- The Gaussian is ubiquitous thanks to the central limit theorem and its algebraic properties
- The multivariate Gaussian encodes correlations between dimensions via the covariance matrix
