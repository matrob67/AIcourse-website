## Introduction: matrices do the work

Matrices aren't just tables of numbers. They are **transformation machines**. When a neural network receives an input and produces an output, it's a series of matrix operations doing the work.

Understanding these operations means understanding what actually happens in each layer of a model.

## Addition and subtraction

The simplest operation. You add matrices **element by element**. The two matrices must have the same size.

If $\mathbf{A}, \mathbf{B} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$:

$$
(\mathbf{A} + \mathbf{B})_{ij} = a_{ij} + b_{ij}
$$

Concrete example:

$$
\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 & 8 \\ 10 & 12 \end{pmatrix}
$$

In ML, addition appears everywhere: adding the **bias** $\mathbf{b}$ to the output of a linear layer, adding **residual connections** in Transformers.

## Scalar multiplication

You multiply every element by the same number:

$$
(\alpha \mathbf{A})_{ij} = \alpha \cdot a_{ij}
$$

When you multiply weights by a regularization factor, or when you apply a **learning rate** to the gradient, that's scalar multiplication.

## The matrix product: the king of operations

This is the most important operation in deep learning. It's at the heart of **every linear layer, every attention mechanism, every transformation**.

### Definition

To multiply $\mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times p}$ by $\mathbf{B} \in \mathbb{R}^{p \times n}$, the number of **columns of A** must equal the number of **rows of B**. The result is a matrix $\mathbf{C} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$:

$$
c_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^{p} a_{ik} \cdot b_{kj}
$$

Each element $c_{ij}$ is the **dot product** of the $i$-th row of $\mathbf{A}$ with the $j$-th column of $\mathbf{B}$.

### Example

$$
\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix} \times \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \cdot 5 + 2 \cdot 7 & 1 \cdot 6 + 2 \cdot 8 \\ 3 \cdot 5 + 4 \cdot 7 & 3 \cdot 6 + 4 \cdot 8 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 19 & 22 \\ 43 & 50 \end{pmatrix}
$$

### Important properties

- **Not commutative**: $\mathbf{AB} \neq \mathbf{BA}$ in general. Order matters.
- **Associative**: $(\mathbf{AB})\mathbf{C} = \mathbf{A}(\mathbf{BC})$
- **Distributive**: $\mathbf{A}(\mathbf{B} + \mathbf{C}) = \mathbf{AB} + \mathbf{AC}$

### Why it's fundamental in ML

A **linear layer** (or dense, or fully connected) in a neural network performs exactly:

$$
\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{W}\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}
$$

Where:
- $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is the input vector
- $\mathbf{W} \in \mathbb{R}^{p \times n}$ is the weight matrix
- $\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^p$ is the bias vector
- $\mathbf{y} \in \mathbb{R}^p$ is the output vector

It's a matrix-vector product followed by an addition. The matrix $\mathbf{W}$ encodes the **learned transformation**: it decides which combinations of inputs are relevant.

For a batch of $B$ examples, you stack the vectors into a matrix $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{B \times n}$ and compute $\mathbf{Y} = \mathbf{X}\mathbf{W}^T + \mathbf{b}$. That's a single matrix product that processes the entire batch at once. That's why GPUs, optimized for massive matrix multiplication, are so important.

### The attention mechanism

In a Transformer, attention is computed by:

$$
\text{Attention}(\mathbf{Q}, \mathbf{K}, \mathbf{V}) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{K}^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)\mathbf{V}
$$

It's a cascade of matrix products. $\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{K}^T$ computes the similarity scores between all pairs of tokens. Multiplication by $\mathbf{V}$ produces the weighted output. Everything relies on matrix multiplication.

## The transpose

### Definition

The transpose of $\mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ is the matrix $\mathbf{A}^T \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times m}$ obtained by swapping rows and columns:

$$
(\mathbf{A}^T)_{ij} = a_{ji}
$$

Concretely, the first row of $\mathbf{A}$ becomes the first column of $\mathbf{A}^T$.

$$
\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{pmatrix}^T = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 4 \\ 2 & 5 \\ 3 & 6 \end{pmatrix}
$$

### Properties

- $(\mathbf{A}^T)^T = \mathbf{A}$
- $(\mathbf{A} + \mathbf{B})^T = \mathbf{A}^T + \mathbf{B}^T$
- $(\mathbf{AB})^T = \mathbf{B}^T\mathbf{A}^T$ -- note, the order reverses

### Uses in ML

The transpose appears constantly:

- The **dot product** of two vectors is written $\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{y} = \sum_i x_i y_i$
- In the attention formula, $\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{K}^T$ transposes the keys to compute similarities
- The gradient of the loss with respect to a linear layer's weights involves the transpose of the activations

## The inverse of a matrix

### Definition

For a square matrix $\mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$, the inverse $\mathbf{A}^{-1}$ is the matrix such that:

$$
\mathbf{A}\mathbf{A}^{-1} = \mathbf{A}^{-1}\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{I}
$$

It's the analog of division for matrices. If $\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$, then $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{A}^{-1}\mathbf{b}$.

### When does it exist?

The inverse doesn't always exist. A matrix is **invertible** (or nonsingular) if and only if:

- Its **determinant** is nonzero
- Its columns are **linearly independent**
- It has **no zero eigenvalue**

If the matrix is not invertible, it's called **singular**. Intuitively, it "crushes" information in one direction -- it's impossible to go back.

### In ML

You often avoid computing the inverse directly (it's expensive and numerically unstable). But the concept is central:

- **Linear regression**: the analytical solution is $\mathbf{w} = (\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{y}$. In practice, more numerically stable methods are used.
- **Second-order optimization**: Newton's methods use the inverse of the Hessian matrix.
- **Multivariate Gaussian distribution**: involves the inverse of the covariance matrix.

## The determinant

### Intuitive definition

The determinant of a square matrix is a **scalar** that measures how the matrix **deforms space**.

For a $2 \times 2$ matrix:

$$
\det \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} = ad - bc
$$

### Geometric meaning

The determinant indicates:

- **Magnitude**: $|\det(\mathbf{A})|$ is the factor by which volumes are multiplied under the transformation $\mathbf{A}$. A determinant of 2 means areas double.
- **Sign**: a negative determinant means the transformation reverses orientation (like a mirror).
- **Zero**: if $\det(\mathbf{A}) = 0$, the matrix crushes space into a lower dimension. Information is lost. The matrix is not invertible.

### In ML

The determinant appears in:

- **Normalizing flows**: generative models that transform a simple distribution into a complex one. The log-determinant of the Jacobian is at the heart of the likelihood computation.
- **Multivariate Gaussian distribution**: the density involves $\det(\mathbf{\Sigma})$, the determinant of the covariance matrix.

## The linear layer: putting it all together

Let's recap. A linear layer in a neural network performs:

$$
\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{W}\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}
$$

That's:
1. A **matrix product** ($\mathbf{W}\mathbf{x}$) -- the learned transformation
2. An **addition** ($+ \mathbf{b}$) -- the offset
3. Followed by a nonlinear **activation function**

Stacking multiple layers means chaining matrix multiplications interspersed with nonlinearities. The entire network is a composition of matrix transformations.

That's why deep learning is fundamentally applied linear algebra. And that's why mastering these operations isn't optional -- it means understanding what your model actually does.
