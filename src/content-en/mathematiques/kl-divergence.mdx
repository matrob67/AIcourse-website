## Comparing two distributions

In machine learning, we constantly compare probability distributions. The model's predictions vs the true labels. The approximate posterior vs the true posterior. The student's policy vs the teacher's. Cross-entropy and KL divergence are the mathematical tools to quantify these gaps.

## Cross-entropy

### Definition

The cross-entropy between a "true" distribution $p$ and a "predicted" distribution $q$ is:

$$H(p, q) = -\sum_x p(x) \log q(x)$$

It measures the average number of bits needed to encode messages drawn from $p$ using the code optimized for $q$.

### Link with entropy

$$H(p, q) = H(p) + D_{KL}(p \| q)$$

Cross-entropy is the entropy of $p$ plus the KL divergence from $p$ to $q$. If $q = p$ (perfect predictions), cross-entropy is minimal and equals exactly $H(p)$.

### The cross-entropy loss in classification

In multi-class classification, the labels are one-hot: $p = (0, \ldots, 1, \ldots, 0)$ with a 1 for the true class $c$. The predictions are the softmax outputs: $q = (\hat{y}_1, \ldots, \hat{y}_K)$.

The cross-entropy simplifies to:

$$H(p, q) = -\log \hat{y}_c$$

That's the loss everyone uses. The higher the probability assigned to the correct class, the lower the loss. If $\hat{y}_c = 1$, the loss is 0. If $\hat{y}_c \to 0$, the loss tends toward infinity.

### Why not MSE for classification?

MSE $\sum (y_k - \hat{y}_k)^2$ also works mathematically, but it has gradients that weaken when the model is very confident and wrong. Cross-entropy, on the other hand, produces strong gradients when the model assigns low probability to the correct class. It severely penalizes confident errors, which speeds up learning.

## KL divergence

### Definition

The Kullback-Leibler divergence measures how different $q$ is from $p$:

$$D_{KL}(p \| q) = \sum_x p(x) \log \frac{p(x)}{q(x)} = H(p, q) - H(p)$$

It's the "extra cost" in bits when you use the code for $q$ to encode messages from $p$, beyond the theoretical minimum $H(p)$.

### Fundamental properties

- **Non-negativity**: $D_{KL}(p \| q) \geq 0$, with equality if and only if $p = q$
- **Not a distance**: KL divergence is not symmetric and doesn't satisfy the triangle inequality
- **Not symmetric**: $D_{KL}(p \| q) \neq D_{KL}(q \| p)$ in general, which has important practical consequences

### Asymmetry has consequences

The non-symmetry of KL divergence is not a flaw: the two directions capture different behaviors.

**$D_{KL}(p \| q)$ (forward KL):** mode-covering

Minimizing $D_{KL}(p \| q)$ with respect to $q$ forces $q$ to cover the entire support of $p$. Where $p(x) > 0$, we must have $q(x) > 0$ too (otherwise the log diverges). Result: $q$ tends to be too broad, covering areas where $p$ is zero.

**$D_{KL}(q \| p)$ (reverse KL):** mode-seeking

Minimizing $D_{KL}(q \| p)$ with respect to $q$ allows $q$ to concentrate on a single mode of $p$. If $p$ is multimodal (multiple peaks), $q$ can ignore some modes and lock onto just one. This is "mode-seeking" behavior.

### Illustration

Imagine that $p$ is a bimodal distribution (two peaks). With forward KL, $q$ will be a broad Gaussian covering both peaks. With reverse KL, $q$ will be a narrow Gaussian centered on a single peak. This difference has concrete consequences on model behavior: a VAE (reverse KL) may "choose" a single mode, while the cross-entropy loss (forward KL) forces the model to cover the entire data distribution.

## Applications in deep learning

### The cross-entropy loss = minimizing KL

When you train a model with cross-entropy, you minimize:

$$H(p_{\text{data}}, q_\theta) = H(p_{\text{data}}) + D_{KL}(p_{\text{data}} \| q_\theta)$$

Since $H(p_{\text{data}})$ is a constant (independent of $\theta$), minimizing cross-entropy is exactly equivalent to minimizing $D_{KL}(p_{\text{data}} \| q_\theta)$. You're looking for parameters $\theta$ such that the predictions $q_\theta$ are as close as possible to the true data distribution.

### VAE: KL between posterior and prior

In a Variational Autoencoder, the objective (ELBO) contains a KL divergence term:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{ELBO}} = E_{q_\phi(z|x)}[\log p_\theta(x|z)] - D_{KL}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p(z))$$

The first term is reconstruction. The second term forces the approximate posterior $q_\phi(z|x)$ to stay close to the prior $p(z) = \mathcal{N}(0, I)$.

For Gaussians, the KL has a closed-form analytical formula:

$$D_{KL}(\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2) \| \mathcal{N}(0, 1)) = \frac{1}{2}\left(\mu^2 + \sigma^2 - \log \sigma^2 - 1\right)$$

That's why VAEs use Gaussians: the KL is computable exactly, no approximation needed.

### Knowledge distillation

Distillation transfers knowledge from a large model (teacher) to a small model (student). You minimize:

$$D_{KL}(p_{\text{teacher}} \| q_{\text{student}})$$

where the distributions are softmax outputs with a high temperature. The student learns not only the correct class, but also the relationships between classes captured by the teacher's probabilities.

### RLHF and PPO

In RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback), you adjust an LLM according to human preferences while staying close to the base model. The loss contains a term:

$$\text{Reward} - \beta \cdot D_{KL}(\pi_\theta \| \pi_{\text{ref}})$$

The KL term prevents the aligned model $\pi_\theta$ from straying too far from the reference model $\pi_{\text{ref}}$, which avoids mode collapse and maintains language diversity.

### Choice of KL direction

| Application | KL direction | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Standard training | $D_{KL}(p_{\text{data}} \| q_\theta)$ | Forward KL via cross-entropy |
| Variational inference (VAE) | $D_{KL}(q_\phi \| p)$ | Reverse KL, tractable |
| Distillation | $D_{KL}(p_{\text{teacher}} \| q_{\text{student}})$ | Forward KL, cover the teacher |
| RLHF | $D_{KL}(\pi_\theta \| \pi_{\text{ref}})$ | Penalize deviations from the base model |

## Other divergences and distances

### Jensen-Shannon divergence

The JS divergence is the symmetric version of KL:

$$D_{JS}(p \| q) = \frac{1}{2} D_{KL}(p \| m) + \frac{1}{2} D_{KL}(q \| m), \quad m = \frac{p + q}{2}$$

It's symmetric, bounded between 0 and $\log 2$, and its square root is a true distance. It's the divergence used in the original GAN objective.

### Wasserstein distance

Also called the Earth Mover's Distance, it measures the "transportation cost" to transform $p$ into $q$. Used in Wasserstein GANs (WGAN) to solve the training problems of classic GANs.

## Key takeaways

- Cross-entropy $H(p, q) = -\sum p \log q$ is the standard classification loss
- KL divergence measures the "extra cost" of using $q$ instead of $p$ for encoding
- KL is not symmetric: forward KL covers everything, reverse KL concentrates
- Training with cross-entropy = minimizing the forward KL between data and predictions
- KL appears in VAEs, distillation, and RLHF with different roles
- For Gaussians, KL has an analytical form, which simplifies calculations
